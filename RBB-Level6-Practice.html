Group क (Banking) — Subjective Questions

1. [20 marks] Trace the concept and historical development of banking, and evaluate the major opportunities and challenges facing the banking sector in Nepal today.

Model answer — key points
  • Intro: Banking is a financial intermediary business that accepts deposits [nikshep] repayable on demand or otherwise, and lends [karja] or invests those funds for profit; a bank connects savers (surplus units) with borrowers (deficit units).
  • Origin of the word: "Bank" is commonly traced to the Italian banco/German banck (a bench or money-changer's table); early banking evolved from moneylenders, goldsmiths, and merchants.
  • Stages of development: (a) primitive/merchant banking (goldsmiths issuing receipts that circulated as early notes); (b) formal commercial banks; (c) central banking and regulated systems; (d) modern era — universal banking, digital/electronic banking, and financial inclusion.
  • Development in Nepal (milestones): Tejarath Adda (state credit office) as an early institution; Nepal Bank Limited (established 1994 B.S.) as the first commercial bank; Nepal Rastra Bank established 2013 B.S. as the central bank; later the second commercial bank and a wave of joint-venture and private banks after the liberalisation of the 1980s–1990s (verify exact dates/figures).
  • Present structure: NRB-licensed institutions are grouped into classes — "A" commercial banks, "B" development banks, "C" finance companies, "D" microfinance — plus infrastructure development banks.
  • Opportunities: low but rising banking penetration (room to grow deposits and access); remittance inflows as a deposit and business base; digital banking, mobile/QR payments and fintech; hydropower, tourism, agriculture and SME financing; financial inclusion drive; expansion into under-banked rural areas.
  • Challenges: excess liquidity vs. credit-deposit gaps and interest-rate volatility; rising non-performing loans [niskriya karja]; over-crowding and thin margins driving mergers/acquisitions; cyber-security and digital fraud risk; AML/KYC compliance burden; governance and related-party lending; limited financial literacy; concentration of credit in unproductive sectors (real estate, imports).
  • Conclusion: Nepal's banking has matured from informal lending to a regulated, technology-driven system; sustainable growth needs prudent risk management, good governance, and steering credit toward productive sectors.

2. [20 marks] What is meant by corporate governance in a bank? Discuss its key pillars and explain why sound governance is critical for banks and financial institutions (BFIs) in Nepal.

Model answer — key points
  • Definition: Corporate governance [sansthagat sushasan] is the system of rules, practices, and processes by which a company is directed and controlled — balancing the interests of shareholders, depositors, management, regulators, and the wider public.
  • Why banks are special: Banks are highly leveraged, use mostly other people's money (depositors' funds), and their failure creates systemic risk — so governance standards are stricter than for ordinary firms.
  • Key pillars/principles: accountability; transparency and disclosure; fairness/equitable treatment of shareholders; responsibility and independence of the board; risk management and internal control; ethical conduct and integrity.
  • Board of Directors: a competent, adequately independent board sets strategy and oversees management; needs fit-and-proper directors, independent directors, and functioning committees (audit committee, risk management committee).
  • Control functions: internal audit, compliance, and risk management functions with independence; robust internal controls to prevent fraud and insider abuse.
  • Disclosure: timely and accurate financial reporting, audited statements, and disclosure of related-party transactions.
  • Regulatory framework in Nepal: NRB directives on corporate governance, fit-and-proper criteria, limits on directors' tenure and cross-holdings, single-obligor and related-party lending limits; BAFIA 2073 and Company Act 2063 provisions (verify current directive figures).
  • Consequences of weak governance: related-party/insider lending, evergreening of bad loans, misreporting, loss of depositor confidence, and bank failure requiring regulatory intervention.
  • Conclusion: Sound governance protects depositors, preserves financial stability, and sustains public trust — the foundation on which the whole banking system rests.

3. [20 marks] Explain the principles of sound credit lending. Describe the "5 Cs of credit" and how a bank uses them, together with other safeguards, to appraise a loan proposal and protect itself from default.

Model answer — key points
  • Intro: Lending [karja pravaha] is a bank's main income-earning and highest-risk activity; sound credit appraisal balances profitability with safety and liquidity.
  • Cardinal principles of lending: safety (recoverability), liquidity, profitability, purpose, security/collateral, diversification (spreading risk), and national/priority-sector interest.
  • The 5 Cs of credit:
    • Character: borrower's integrity, credit history, willingness to repay (CIB/credit-bureau report).
    • Capacity: ability to repay from cash flow — income, debt-service coverage, repayment capacity.
    • Capital: the borrower's own stake/equity in the venture ("skin in the game").
    • Collateral: security pledged as a fallback source of repayment; requires proper valuation and marketability.
    • Conditions: purpose of the loan and the economic/industry/market conditions affecting repayment.
  • Sometimes a 6th C: Compliance/Control — meeting regulatory and documentation requirements.
  • Additional safeguards: credit-information bureau (CIB) checks; loan-to-value limits; single-obligor and sector exposure limits; loan covenants; guarantees; insurance of collateral; periodic monitoring and site inspection; loan-loss provisioning and classification (pass/watchlist/substandard/doubtful/loss) (verify current provisioning rates).
  • Loan process: application → appraisal/credit analysis → sanction/approval per delegated authority → documentation and disbursement → monitoring/follow-up → recovery.
  • Conclusion: The 5 Cs are a structured framework to judge both willingness and ability to repay; combined with limits, security and monitoring, they keep credit risk within acceptable bounds.

4. [20 marks] Write short notes on any TWO of the following: (a) Types of deposits and techniques of deposit mobilization; (b) Letter of Credit (LC) vs. Bank Guarantee; (c) Client protection principles in banking.

Model answer — key points
  • (a) Deposits and deposit mobilization:
    • Deposit [nikshep] is the primary raw material of a bank; mobilizing deposits funds lending.
    • Types: demand deposits — current account (no/low interest, for businesses) and savings account (small savers, limited withdrawals); time/term deposits — fixed deposit [muddati] for a set tenure at higher interest; recurring deposit; call deposit; margin and special deposits.
    • Mobilization techniques: attractive interest rates and schemes; branch and ATM network expansion; digital/mobile banking; targeted products (student, senior, remittance, women's savings); good service and trust; deposit-insurance assurance; marketing and financial literacy.
  • (b) LC vs. Bank Guarantee:
    • Letter of Credit — a bank's written undertaking to pay the seller/exporter on presentation of compliant documents; a payment mechanism mainly in trade; the bank is the primary payer once terms are met (governed by UCP 600 in international practice).
    • Bank Guarantee — the bank's promise to pay the beneficiary if the applicant fails to perform an obligation; a contingent/secondary undertaking (default-triggered). Types: bid/tender, performance, advance-payment, financial guarantees.
    • Key difference: an LC is invoked in the normal course of a successful transaction (payment against documents); a guarantee is invoked only on default/non-performance.
    • Both are non-fund-based (contingent liabilities) earning commission and requiring margin/collateral.
  • (c) Client protection:
    • Principles (aligned with Smart Campaign/consumer-protection norms): appropriate product design; prevention of over-indebtedness; transparent pricing and terms; responsible/fair collection practices; ethical staff behaviour; privacy and data protection; mechanisms for complaint resolution and grievance handling; fair and respectful treatment of clients.
    • In Nepal: NRB consumer-protection/grievance directives, disclosure of interest and charges, and financial literacy support client protection.
  • Conclusion cue: each note should end with one line on its importance to banking safety/trust.

5. [20 marks] Distinguish between the money market and the capital market. Describe the main instruments and institutions of each, with reference to their state of development in Nepal.

Model answer — key points
  • Intro: The financial market channels savings into investment; it is broadly split into the money market (short-term funds) and the capital market (long-term funds).
  • Money market: deals in short-term instruments (up to one year) for liquidity management. Instruments: treasury bills, inter-bank lending/call money, certificates of deposit, commercial paper, repos. Participants/institutions: NRB, commercial banks, and other BFIs. In Nepal the money market is dominated by treasury bills and inter-bank transactions, with NRB conducting open-market operations for monetary management.
  • Capital market: deals in long-term/medium-term securities (over one year). Instruments: shares/equity, debentures/bonds, government bonds, mutual funds. Segments: primary market (new issues — IPO, FPO, rights) and secondary market (trading of existing securities).
  • Capital-market institutions in Nepal: Securities Board of Nepal (SEBON) as regulator; Nepal Stock Exchange (NEPSE) as the sole secondary exchange; CDS and Clearing Ltd. (CDSC) for demat/settlement; merchant bankers/issue managers, brokers, and credit-rating agencies.
  • State of development in Nepal: capital market is dominated by BFI and insurance shares; largely equity-driven with a thin corporate-bond market; growing retail participation via online trading (TMS) and demat/ASBA/C-ASBA; challenges — limited product diversity, price volatility, and low institutional depth.
  • Conclusion: a developed money market ensures liquidity and effective monetary policy, while a deep capital market provides long-term investment finance; Nepal's markets are still maturing, especially on the debt side.

6. [20 marks] Explain the process of credit creation by commercial banks with a suitable example. What factors and regulatory tools limit a bank's power to create credit?

Model answer — key points
  • Concept: Credit creation [sadhan srijana / karja srijana] is the process by which the banking system as a whole multiplies an initial deposit into a larger volume of deposits and loans; often called "deposits create loans and loans create deposits."
  • Basis: banks keep only a fraction of deposits as reserves (because not all depositors withdraw at once) and lend the rest; the borrowed money returns to the banking system as new deposits, which are again partly lent — a multiplier effect.
  • Money multiplier: credit multiplier = 1 ÷ cash-reserve ratio. Example: with a 10% reserve ratio, an initial Rs.1,000 deposit can expand total deposits to about Rs.10,000 across the banking system (1,000 × 1/0.10).
  • Worked logic: Bank A keeps Rs.100 (10%) and lends Rs.900; that Rs.900 is deposited in Bank B, which keeps Rs.90 and lends Rs.810, and so on — the series sums to a multiple of the original deposit.
  • Limiting factors: the cash-reserve ratio (CRR) and statutory liquidity ratio (SLR); cash-leakage/currency drain (public holding cash outside banks); availability of creditworthy borrowers and loan demand; banks' willingness to lend and capital adequacy; excess reserves held for safety.
  • Regulatory (NRB) tools: CRR and SLR requirements; bank rate/policy rate; open-market operations; credit-to-deposit (CD) ratio limits; capital adequacy (Basel) and sectoral credit limits (verify current ratio figures).
  • Conclusion: commercial banks create credit through fractional-reserve lending, but the central bank controls the extent of expansion to manage money supply, inflation, and financial stability.

Group ग — Accounting (लेखा)

1. [20 marks] Explain the double-entry system of book-keeping. State its rules for the three traditional types of accounts (Personal, Real and Nominal), give the "golden rules" of debit and credit for each, and explain why the double-entry system is considered scientifically superior to the single-entry system.

Model answer / solution

Meaning. The double-entry system records every business transaction in two accounts — one account is debited and another is credited by an equal amount. It rests on the idea that every transaction has two aspects: a receiving (or benefit-getting) aspect and a giving (or benefit-giving) aspect.

Fundamental equation. The system keeps the accounting equation always in balance:

  • Assets = Liabilities + Capital (Owner's equity).
  • For every transaction, total debits = total credits, so the equation never breaks.

Three traditional types of accounts and their golden rules.

Type of accountWhat it coversGolden rule (Debit / Credit)
Personal accountPersons, firms, institutions (e.g. Ram's A/c, Bank A/c, Creditors)Debit the receiver, Credit the giver
Real accountAssets and property (e.g. Cash, Machinery, Stock, Furniture)Debit what comes in, Credit what goes out
Nominal accountExpenses, losses, incomes and gains (e.g. Salary, Rent, Interest received)Debit all expenses and losses, Credit all incomes and gains

Simple worked example. Paid rent Rs. 5,000 by cash. Rent is a nominal account (an expense) → debit Rent Rs. 5,000. Cash is a real account going out → credit Cash Rs. 5,000. Two equal entries, books stay balanced.

Why double-entry is superior to single-entry.

  • Arithmetical accuracy can be checked because total debits must equal total credits (via the trial balance).
  • Complete record — both personal and impersonal (real/nominal) accounts are kept, so full financial position is known.
  • Profit or loss can be found accurately by preparing a Profit & Loss Account.
  • Financial position can be shown through a Balance Sheet.
  • Fraud and error detection is easier; comparison across years is possible.
  • Single-entry keeps only cash and personal accounts, gives no trial balance, and cannot reliably show profit or financial position — hence it is incomplete and unscientific.

4. [20 marks] Distinguish between internal audit and external audit. Also explain the petty cash book and the imprest system of maintaining petty cash, and state why petty cash control matters in a government/public office.

Model answer / solution

Audit — meaning. Audit is the independent examination of the books of account and supporting records to verify that the financial statements give a true and fair view and that transactions are legal, authorised and properly recorded.

Internal audit vs. external audit.

BasisInternal auditExternal audit
Who performs itAn employee/internal department of the organisationAn independent qualified auditor outside the organisation (in the government sector, the Office of the Auditor General)
AppointmentBy managementBy owners/shareholders or by law/statute

Petty cash book. A subsidiary cash book used to record small, routine cash payments (postage, stationery, tea, local conveyance, minor repairs) so that the main cash book is not cluttered with tiny entries. It is kept by a petty cashier. The analytical (columnar) petty cash book classifies each payment under expense heads for easy posting.

Imprest system.

  • The petty cashier is given a fixed sum (the imprest / float) for a period — say Rs. 10,000 for a month.
  • Payments are made and recorded against vouchers during the period.
  • At period-end the amount actually spent is reimbursed, restoring the float back to Rs. 10,000 for the next period.
  • Example: Float Rs. 10,000; spent Rs. 7,200 in the month; reimbursement Rs. 7,200; opening float next month again Rs. 10,000.

Why petty cash control matters (public office).

1. [20 marks] Distinguish between low-level and high-level programming languages. Explain the role of assemblers, compilers, and interpreters in language translation, and compare compiled versus interpreted execution.

Model answer — key points
  • Low-level languages: machine language (binary opcodes, directly executable, fully machine-dependent) and assembly language (mnemonic instructions, one-to-one mapping to machine code, still hardware-specific).
  • High-level languages: English-like syntax, machine-independent, portable across platforms (C, Java, Python); provide abstractions — variables, control structures, functions, data types.
  • Trade-offs: low-level gives fine hardware control, speed, small footprint (used in embedded systems, device drivers, firmware); high-level gives readability, portability, faster development and easier maintenance at some cost to raw performance.
  • Assembler: translates assembly mnemonics into machine code, essentially a one-to-one substitution plus address resolution.
  • Compiler: translates the entire high-level source into object/machine code before execution in distinct phases — lexical analysis, syntax analysis, semantic analysis, intermediate code generation, code optimization, final code generation; produces a standalone executable.
  • Interpreter: translates and executes source statement-by-statement at run time without producing a separate persistent executable; easier to debug and more portable, but generally slower per run.
  • Hybrid model: source compiled to an intermediate bytecode, then interpreted or JIT-compiled at run time (Java JVM, .NET CLR, Python bytecode) — balances portability and performance.
  • Compiled vs interpreted comparison: error detection time (compile-time vs run-time), execution speed (compiled generally faster), portability (interpreted more portable, needs interpreter on target machine), development/debug cycle (interpreted faster to test).

Security Technologies and Fintech in Banking

1. [20 marks] Write short notes on the following security technologies used in the banking sector, explaining the purpose and working of each: (a) SIEM; (b) VPN; (c) Digital Certificate; (d) Digital Signature; (e) PKI.

Model answer — key points
  • (a) SIEM (Security Information and Event Management): centralises collection of logs/events from servers, network devices and applications; performs normalisation and correlation to detect anomalies; provides real-time alerting, dashboards, incident investigation and compliance reporting. It is the core of a Security Operations Centre (SOC) and supports early detection of intrusions and fraud.
  • (b) VPN (Virtual Private Network): creates an encrypted "tunnel" over a public/untrusted network so data travels confidentially and with integrity. Common forms: site-to-site (branch/DR connectivity, often IPsec) and remote-access (staff working remotely, often SSL/TLS). Provides confidentiality, integrity and authentication of endpoints.
  • (c) Digital Certificate: an electronic credential (typically X.509) issued by a Certifying Authority that binds a public key to a verified identity (person, server, or organisation). Contains subject, public key, issuer, validity period and the CA's signature; enables trust in the holder's public key (e.g., TLS server certificates, code signing).
  • (d) Digital Signature: a cryptographic mechanism in which the signer hashes the message and encrypts the hash with their private key; the verifier decrypts with the signer's public key and compares hashes. Provides authentication, data integrity and non-repudiation — legally recognised in Nepal under the Electronic Transactions Act, 2063. (Distinct from an electronic/scanned signature.)
  • (e) PKI (Public Key Infrastructure): the ecosystem of policies, roles and technology that manages asymmetric keys and certificates — comprising the Certifying Authority (CA), Registration Authority (RA), certificate repositories, and revocation services (CRL/OCSP). In Nepal the Office of the Controller of Certification acts as the root of trust over licensed CAs. PKI underpins digital signatures, TLS, secure email and strong authentication.
  • Linkage: digital certificates and signatures are products of PKI; VPN and TLS rely on certificates; SIEM monitors the whole environment for misuse — together they support confidentiality, integrity, availability and accountability.

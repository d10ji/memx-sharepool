Group क — Banking

1. Concept of Banking and its Development (बैंकिङको अवधारणा र विकास)

A bank is a financial institution licensed to accept deposits from the public and to lend money. The core idea of banking is financial intermediation: the bank stands between people who have surplus money (savers/depositors) and people who need money (borrowers). It collects small and large deposits, pools them, and channels this pooled fund into loans and investments. The bank profits mainly from the interest spread — the difference between the interest it pays depositors and the higher interest it charges borrowers.

The mechanism rests on three functions. First, accepting deposits (current, savings, fixed). Second, advancing credit (loans, overdrafts, cash credit). Third, providing payment and agency services (cheques, remittance, cards, standing instructions, safe custody). A key structural feature is that banks operate on fractional reserve — they keep only a fraction of deposits as reserve and lend out the rest, which lets the banking system create credit (money) far beyond the physical cash held.

The word "bank" is commonly traced to the Italian banco (a bench or table used by money-changers in medieval trading cities). Banking evolved in stages: (i) ancient temple and merchant lending in Mesopotamia, Greece and Rome; (ii) the medieval Italian merchant bankers (the Medici, and the Bank of Venice 1157, Bank of Barcelona 1401, Bank of Genoa 1407); (iii) the rise of modern joint-stock banks such as the Bank of England (1694), which became the model central bank; and (iv) the twentieth-century emergence of central banking, deposit insurance, and today's digital/electronic banking.

Banking matters because it mobilizes idle savings into productive investment, provides a safe payment system, allocates capital to the most productive uses, and transmits monetary policy. Without banks, savings would sit idle and investment would depend only on the saver's own funds. A simple example: a farmer deposits Rs 100,000 in savings; the bank keeps a small reserve and lends the rest to a shopkeeper to buy stock — the deposit is safe and earns interest, while the economy gets new business activity.

  • Exam pointers
  • Core function of a bank = financial intermediation (link savers and borrowers); profit source = interest spread.
  • Word "bank" derives from Italian banco (bench of money-changers).
  • Bank of Venice (1157) is often cited as an early public bank; Bank of England (1694) is the model for modern central banking.
  • Three primary functions: accept deposits, advance credit, provide payment/agency services.
  • Fractional-reserve system allows the banking system to create credit (money multiplier).
  • Evolution: temple/merchant lending → Italian merchant bankers → joint-stock banks → central banks → digital banking.

2. Banking Development in Nepal — History, Opportunities and Challenges (नेपालमा बैंकिङ विकास)

Organised banking in Nepal is relatively young. Before modern banks, credit needs were met by moneylenders (sahu-mahajan), the Tejarath Adda (1877 AD / 1933 BS) — a government lending office that gave loans against gold and silver but did not take deposits — and informal groups (dhukuti). The first true commercial bank was Nepal Bank Limited, established in 1937 AD (1994 BS). The central bank, Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB), was established in 1956 AD (2013 BS) under the Nepal Rastra Bank Act. Rastriya Banijya Bank (RBB) was established in 1966 AD (2022 BS) as a fully government-owned commercial bank.

The sector opened up after the financial liberalization of the mid-1980s, allowing joint-venture and private banks (Nepal Arab Bank / Nabil, 1984 being the first joint venture). NRB classifies licensed institutions into four classes: Class "A" commercial banks, Class "B" development banks, Class "C" finance companies, and Class "D" microfinance financial institutions. In recent years NRB has pushed merger and acquisition policy to consolidate the crowded sector and strengthen capital, sharply reducing the number of banks and financial institutions (BFIs).

Opportunities: large unbanked/under-banked population and expanding financial inclusion; rapid growth of digital banking, mobile wallets and QR payments; strong inward remittance flows that provide deposits; hydropower, tourism, agriculture and infrastructure financing; and the government's push to bring every local unit into the banking net.

Challenges: recurring liquidity shortages and interest-rate volatility; concentration of credit in real estate and imports (limited productive lending); rising non-performing loans (NPL) in stress periods; limited financial literacy in rural areas; cyber-security and IT risks; corporate-governance weaknesses; and the pressure to meet rising minimum capital and Basel-based prudential norms.

  • Exam pointers
  • Pre-modern credit: moneylenders, Tejarath Adda (1877 AD/1933 BS) — lent against gold/silver, no deposits.
  • First commercial bank: Nepal Bank Ltd, 1937 AD (1994 BS). Central bank: NRB, 1956 AD (2013 BS). RBB: 1966 AD (2022 BS).
  • First joint-venture bank: Nabil Bank (Nepal Arab Bank), 1984.
  • NRB classes: A = commercial, B = development, C = finance, D = microfinance.
  • Current policy thrust: mergers/acquisitions to consolidate BFIs and raise capital.
  • Opportunities: financial inclusion, digital banking, remittance-based deposits, infrastructure/hydropower finance.
  • Challenges: liquidity crunch, unproductive credit concentration, NPLs, low financial literacy, cyber risk, governance. (Verify latest BFI counts and minimum capital from the current NRB directive.)

3. Corporate Governance in the Banking Sector (संस्थागत सुशासन)

Corporate governance is the system of rules, practices and processes by which a company is directed and controlled. In banking it means the framework that balances the interests of a bank's many stakeholders — depositors, shareholders, borrowers, regulators (NRB) and the wider public — and ensures the bank is run prudently, ethically and transparently. Because banks lend out other people's money (mostly depositors' funds) and are highly leveraged, good governance in banks protects public trust and the stability of the whole financial system, not just one firm.

The mechanism works through a clear structure of accountability. The Board of Directors sets strategy and risk appetite and oversees management; senior management runs day-to-day operations; and independent control functions — risk management, compliance, and internal audit — act as "checks." Key pillars are: fit-and-proper directors, separation of the roles of Chairman and CEO, independent directors, functioning board-level committees (audit committee, risk management committee), sound disclosure and transparency, protection of minority shareholders, and effective external audit.

In Nepal, corporate governance for banks is driven by NRB directives, the Bank and Financial Institution Act (BAFIA) 2073, the Company Act 2063, and securities regulations for listed banks. NRB rules cover board size and composition, tenure limits, restrictions on loans to directors and related parties (related-party lending), single-obligor/large-exposure limits, mandatory audit committees, and "fit and proper" tests for directors and CEOs.

Its importance is direct: weak governance was behind many banking failures worldwide (excessive risk-taking, insider abuse, fraud). Good governance reduces the chance of insider lending scandals, protects depositors, improves credit discipline (lower NPLs), and raises investor confidence. Example: an audit committee independent of the CEO can catch mis-reported bad loans before they threaten the bank's solvency.

  • Exam pointers
  • Definition: system by which a bank is directed and controlled, balancing all stakeholders.
  • Why banks are special: highly leveraged, use depositors' money → governance protects systemic stability, not just shareholders.
  • Pillars: competent board, independent directors, separation of Chair/CEO, audit and risk committees, transparency/disclosure, strong internal audit.
  • "Three lines of defence": business units → risk & compliance → internal audit.
  • Nepal framework: NRB directives + BAFIA 2073 + Company Act 2063; "fit and proper" test for directors/CEO.
  • Controls on related-party / insider lending and large exposures are central governance tools.
  • OECD principles and Basel Committee guidance underpin modern bank governance.

4. Credit Lending and its Policies — the 5 Cs and Loan Types (कर्जा प्रवाह र नीति)

Credit lending is the process by which a bank advances money to a borrower who promises to repay the principal with interest over an agreed period. Lending is the main earning activity of a bank, but it is also the main source of risk (credit risk = risk the borrower will not repay). A credit policy is the written framework that guides who the bank lends to, how much, at what price, against what security, and how loans are monitored and recovered — ensuring lending is consistent, prudent and within regulatory limits.

Before granting a loan, banks assess the borrower using the classic 5 Cs of Credit: Character (integrity and repayment history of the borrower), Capacity (ability to repay, judged from income/cash flow), Capital (the borrower's own stake/net worth in the venture), Collateral (security pledged as a fallback), and Conditions (purpose of the loan and the economic/industry environment). Some texts add a sixth C, Control (compliance with laws and internal norms).

In Nepal, lending policy is shaped strongly by NRB directives: loan classification and loan-loss provisioning (pass, watch-list, substandard, doubtful, loss), single-borrower/large-exposure limits, the loan-to-value (LTV) ratio, sectoral caps (e.g. real estate), mandatory deprived-sector and priority-sector lending targets, and reference to a base rate for pricing. Example: for a Rs 50 lakh home loan, the bank checks the applicant's salary (capacity), CIB credit report (character), down-payment (capital), the property mortgage (collateral) and the market outlook (conditions) before approving.

5. Client Protection Principle (ग्राहक संरक्षणका सिद्धान्त)

Client protection means treating financial customers fairly and responsibly so that using a bank's products does not harm them. It grew out of the microfinance and financial-inclusion movement, where poor and less-literate clients are vulnerable to over-lending, hidden charges and aggressive collection. The most widely used framework is the Client Protection Principles (CPPs) promoted by the Smart Campaign / now the Cerise+SPTF standards, later adopted broadly by banks and regulators.

The core principles are usually listed as: (1) Appropriate product design and delivery — products suited to client needs, no harm; (2) Prevention of over-indebtedness — lend only what the client can repay, check repayment capacity; (3) Transparency — clear, full disclosure of prices, terms and conditions in a language the client understands; (4) Responsible pricing — affordable and sustainable, not exploitative; (5) Fair and respectful treatment of clients — no abusive collection or discrimination; (6) Privacy of client data — safeguard personal and financial information; and (7) Mechanisms for complaint resolution / grievance redressal.

These principles matter because banking rests on trust. Mis-selling, hidden fees, or coercive recovery destroy customer confidence, invite regulatory penalties, and can trigger over-indebtedness crises (as seen in some microfinance markets). Protecting clients is therefore both an ethical duty and a business necessity for long-term reputation and stability.

In Nepal, client protection is supported through NRB consumer-protection provisions, mandatory disclosure of interest rates and charges, a grievance-handling requirement at BFIs, the Consumer Protection Act generally, and specific attention to microfinance over-indebtedness. Example: before giving a microloan, the loan officer must verify the client's existing debts to avoid pushing them into an unpayable debt trap, and must clearly explain the effective interest rate and all fees.

  • Exam pointers
  • Origin: microfinance/financial-inclusion movement; framework = Client Protection Principles (Smart Campaign → SPTF/Cerise standards).
  • Seven common principles: appropriate design, prevent over-indebtedness, transparency, responsible pricing, fair treatment, data privacy, grievance redressal.
  • Goal: financial products must not harm the client; build trust.
  • Prevention of over-indebtedness = assess true repayment capacity and existing debts.
  • Transparency = full, understandable disclosure of price, terms and effective interest rate.
  • Nepal: NRB consumer-protection rules, mandatory disclosure and grievance mechanisms; relevant to microfinance (Class "D") especially.

6. Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and KYC (सम्पत्ति शुद्धीकरण निवारण र ग्राहक पहिचान)

Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained money ("dirty money" from crime, corruption, drugs, smuggling, etc.) appear legitimate ("clean"). It classically has three stages: Placement (putting the illegal cash into the financial system), Layering (moving it through complex transactions to hide its origin), and Integration (bringing it back as apparently legitimate wealth). Anti-Money Laundering (AML) is the set of laws, rules and procedures designed to detect and prevent this, and to stop terrorist financing (CFT).

KYC (Know Your Customer) is the first line of AML defence. Before opening an account and periodically thereafter, the bank must identify and verify who the customer really is, understand the nature of their business, and the source of their funds. KYC involves Customer Due Diligence (CDD) for ordinary clients and Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for higher-risk clients such as Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs). Banks must identify the beneficial owner (the real person behind a company or account).

The mechanism of AML compliance includes: risk-based customer categorisation, ongoing transaction monitoring, filing Threshold Transaction Reports (TTR) for large cash transactions and Suspicious Transaction Reports (STR) for unusual activity, record-keeping, staff training, and appointing a compliance officer. Reports go to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU-Nepal), which sits within Nepal Rastra Bank.

In Nepal, the governing law is the Asset (Money) Laundering Prevention Act 2064, supported by NRB AML/CFT directives, and Nepal is a member of the Asia/Pacific Group on Money Laundering (APG) and follows the FATF (Financial Action Task Force) 40 Recommendations. Importance: money laundering funds crime and terrorism, distorts the economy, and can get a country "grey-listed," raising the cost of all international transactions. Example: a customer who suddenly deposits many large cash amounts far beyond their known income should trigger EDD and possibly an STR.

  • Exam pointers
  • Three stages of laundering: Placement → Layering → Integration.
  • KYC = Know Your Customer; CDD for normal, EDD for high-risk (e.g. PEPs); identify the beneficial owner.
  • Reports: TTR (threshold/large cash) and STR (suspicious) filed to FIU-Nepal (within NRB).
  • Nepal law: Asset (Money) Laundering Prevention Act 2064 + NRB AML/CFT directives.
  • International: FATF 40 Recommendations; Nepal is an APG member; risk of grey-listing.
  • AML also covers Combating the Financing of Terrorism (CFT).
  • Bank obligations: customer identification, ongoing monitoring, record-keeping (typically several years), training, compliance officer. (Verify current TTR threshold amount and record-keeping period from the latest directive.)

7. Types of Deposit and their Mobilization (निक्षेपका प्रकार र परिचालन)

A deposit is money placed by a customer with a bank for safekeeping, with a promise of repayment (often with interest). Deposits are the bank's cheapest and largest source of funds — the raw material for lending. Deposit mobilization means the bank's effort to attract and collect these savings from the public and channel them into productive loans and investments; it is a key indicator of a bank's growth and of financial deepening in the economy.

The mechanism of mobilization uses attractive interest rates, convenient products, branch and digital access, remittance-linked accounts, and public trust (safety and deposit protection). NRB rules directly shape deposits: Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR) and Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR) set how much banks must keep in reserve, the credit-to-deposit (CD) ratio limits how much of deposits can be lent, and deposit insurance protects small depositors. Interest on deposits earns income for savers and TDS (tax) applies to that interest.

Importance and Nepal context: deposit mobilization converts scattered idle savings into investable capital, deepens financial inclusion, and gives the bank a stable funding base. Nepal relies heavily on remittance-fed deposits, which makes deposit growth sensitive to remittance flows and to periodic liquidity crunches. Example: RBB offering a special remittance-linked fixed-deposit scheme encourages migrant families to save formally rather than hold cash.

8. Letter of Credit (LC) and Bank Guarantee (प्रतीतपत्र र बैंक जमानत)

A Letter of Credit (LC / प्रतीतपत्र) is a written undertaking by a bank (the issuing bank), on behalf of its customer (the buyer/importer), to pay the seller/exporter a stated sum provided the seller presents specified documents (invoice, bill of lading, insurance, etc.) that comply exactly with the LC terms. It is mainly used in international trade to solve the trust gap between a buyer and a seller in different countries who do not know each other: the seller trusts the bank's promise instead of the unknown buyer.

The mechanism: the buyer applies to its bank to open an LC in favour of the seller. The issuing bank sends it (often through an advising/confirming bank in the seller's country). The seller ships the goods and submits documents; if the documents comply, the bank pays. A crucial principle is that banks deal in documents, not goods — payment depends on document compliance, not on the physical condition of the goods. LCs can be sight (pay on presentation) or usance/deferred (pay later), revocable/irrevocable (almost always irrevocable), and confirmed/unconfirmed. LCs are governed internationally by the ICC's UCP 600 rules.

A Bank Guarantee (जमानत) is a promise by a bank to pay a beneficiary a certain amount if the bank's customer (the applicant) fails to fulfil a contractual obligation. Common types: bid/tender guarantee (to enter a bid), performance guarantee (to ensure a contract is completed), advance-payment guarantee, and financial/payment guarantee. It is a contingent liability: the bank pays only if the customer defaults.

Importance and Nepal context: both are vital trade-finance and fee-income tools for banks and are essential for importers/exporters and contractors. In Nepal, LCs are heavily used because the economy is import-driven; NRB regulates margin requirements on LCs, foreign-exchange rules apply, and NRB has at times imposed cash-margin requirements on imports of certain goods to manage foreign reserves. Example: a Nepali importer opens an LC to buy machinery from India; the Indian seller ships only after the LC assures payment.

  • Exam pointers

  • Golden rule: banks deal in documents, not goods; LCs governed by ICC UCP 600.
  • LC types: sight vs usance; revocable vs irrevocable (usually irrevocable); confirmed vs unconfirmed.
  • Parties: applicant (buyer), beneficiary (seller), issuing bank, advising/confirming bank.
  • Bank Guarantee = bank pays only if the customer defaults (contingent liability).
  • Guarantee types: bid/tender, performance, advance-payment, financial.
  • Nepal: import-heavy → LC-intensive; NRB sets LC margin and forex rules. (Verify current import cash-margin rules from NRB.)

9. Money Market and Capital Market — Development, Present Status and Challenges in Nepal (मुद्रा बजार र पूँजी बजार)

The financial market has two broad parts. The money market (मुद्रा बजार) is the market for short-term funds (maturity up to one year): instruments include Treasury bills, inter-bank lending, call money, repo/reverse-repo, and commercial paper/certificates of deposit. Its purpose is short-term liquidity management for banks, businesses and the government. The capital market (पूँजी बजार) is the market for long-term funds (over one year): instruments include shares (equity), debentures/bonds, and government development bonds. Its purpose is to raise long-term capital for investment and growth.

The capital market has a primary market (new issues — IPO, FPO, rights, bonds) where companies raise fresh capital, and a secondary market (the stock exchange) where existing securities are traded, giving investors liquidity. In Nepal the key institutions are: the Securities Board of Nepal (SEBON) — the regulator; the Nepal Stock Exchange (NEPSE) — the only stock exchange; CDS and Clearing Ltd (CDSC) for dematerialised shares; and merchant bankers/issue managers. The money market is largely regulated by Nepal Rastra Bank.

Present status in Nepal: the money market is relatively narrow, dominated by T-bills and inter-bank transactions, with limited instruments (thin market for commercial paper and CDs). The capital market has grown rapidly in participation (millions of demat/Mero Share accounts, online trading, ASBA for IPOs, index growth), but it is equity-heavy and bank-and-finance-dominated, with a small and underdeveloped bond/debenture market.

Challenges: limited product diversity (few derivatives, weak corporate bond market); high volatility and speculative retail behaviour; concentration in the banking sector; weak long-term institutional investors (mutual funds, pension funds still growing); information asymmetry and disclosure gaps; and the need for stronger technology, investor education and market depth. Importance: well-developed money and capital markets mobilize savings into productive long-term investment, help price capital, and reduce the economy's over-dependence on bank loans. Example: a hydropower company raising equity through an IPO on NEPSE taps public savings directly instead of borrowing only from banks.

  • Exam pointers
  • Money market = short-term (≤1 yr): T-bills, inter-bank/call money, repo, commercial paper, CDs; regulated by NRB.
  • Capital market = long-term (>1 yr): shares, debentures, bonds; regulated by SEBON.
  • Primary market = new issues (IPO/FPO/rights); secondary market = trading on NEPSE.
  • Key institutions: SEBON (regulator), NEPSE (exchange), CDSC (depository), merchant bankers.
  • Nepal status: capital market equity-heavy and bank-dominated; bond market underdeveloped; money market shallow.
  • Modern features: dematerialisation (demat), Mero Share, online trading, ASBA for IPO allotment.
  • Challenges: low product diversity, volatility, weak corporate-bond and institutional-investor base, disclosure gaps.

10. Credit Creation and the Role of the Banking Sector (साख/कर्जा सिर्जना)

Credit creation (साख सिर्जना) is the process by which the banking system as a whole expands the money supply by making loans. It works because banks operate on a fractional-reserve basis: they are required to keep only a fraction of each deposit as reserve (the Cash Reserve Ratio, CRR) and can lend out the rest. When a bank lends, the borrower spends the money, and it is re-deposited into another bank, which again keeps a fraction and lends the rest — so the original deposit multiplies into a much larger amount of total deposits and loans across the system. This is why banks are sometimes called "manufacturers of credit / money."

The amount of expansion is governed by the money (credit) multiplier, which in the simple model equals 1 / CRR (more fully, it also depends on the cash-drawn/leakage ratio). For example, if the CRR is 10% (0.10), an initial fresh deposit of Rs 1,000 can theoretically support up to Rs 1,000 × (1/0.10) = Rs 10,000 of total deposits/credit across the banking system. A single bank cannot do this alone; it is the system of many banks, with re-deposits, that creates the multiple.

Several factors limit credit creation in practice: the CRR and SLR set by the central bank, the credit-to-deposit (CD) ratio, cash leakages (money held as cash outside banks), the availability of creditworthy borrowers, and the banks' own liquidity and capital. The central bank (NRB) controls the process using monetary-policy tools — changing CRR/SLR, the bank rate/policy rate, and open-market operations (buying/selling T-bills, repo) — to expand or contract credit as the economy needs.

Role and importance: credit creation is the main channel through which the banking sector finances investment, consumption and growth — it turns a limited amount of base money into a much larger flow of usable credit, funding businesses, trade and government. But it is double-edged: excessive credit creation fuels inflation and asset bubbles, while too little causes recession and liquidity shortage. In Nepal, NRB manages this balance through monetary policy; periodic liquidity crunches occur when deposit growth lags behind credit demand.

  • Exam pointers
  • Credit creation = the banking system multiplying deposits into loans via fractional reserves.
  • Money multiplier (simple) = 1 / CRR; higher CRR → less credit creation.
  • Example: CRR 10% → Rs 1,000 deposit can support up to Rs 10,000 total credit.
  • A single bank lends only its excess reserves; the multiple arises from re-deposits across banks.
  • Limits: CRR, SLR, CD ratio, cash leakage, creditworthy borrowers, bank capital/liquidity.
  • Central-bank (NRB) controls: CRR/SLR, bank/policy rate, open-market operations.
  • Importance: finances growth; but excess → inflation/bubbles, too little → recession. (Verify current CRR/SLR and policy rate from NRB monetary policy.)

Group ख — Acts, Rules and Regulations

11. Nepal Rastra Bank Act, 2058 (नेपाल राष्ट्र बैंक ऐन)

The Nepal Rastra Bank Act, 2058 (2002 AD) is the governing law of Nepal's central bank. It replaced the older 2012 BS Act and re-established NRB with clearer autonomy and modern central-banking functions. Its overall purpose is to maintain monetary and financial stability — stable prices, a sound banking and payment system, and confidence in the national currency — and to give NRB the independence needed to pursue these goals.

Main objectives stated in the Act typically include: to formulate and implement monetary and foreign-exchange policy; to maintain the stability of prices and the balance of payments; to develop a secure, healthy and efficient payment system; to regulate, inspect and supervise banks and financial institutions; and to promote the entire financial system. NRB is established as an autonomous, corporate body with perpetual succession.

Main functions and powers under the Act: sole right to issue currency (notes and coins); acting as banker to the government and managing public debt; acting as banker to banks / lender of last resort; managing the country's foreign-exchange reserves and gold; setting monetary tools (CRR, bank rate, open-market operations); and licensing, regulating and supervising BFIs (in conjunction with BAFIA). The Act sets up the Board of Directors headed by the Governor (appointed by the Government), along with deputy governors, and provides for the Governor's tenure and grounds for removal, to protect NRB's autonomy.

Importance and Nepal context: this Act is the legal foundation of monetary policy and financial regulation in Nepal. Every NRB directive that governs commercial banks like RBB derives its authority from this Act (together with BAFIA). Example: when NRB changes the CRR or issues a monetary-policy stance to control inflation, it exercises powers granted by the NRB Act 2058.

  • Exam pointers
  • Enacted 2058 BS (2002 AD); governs the central bank (NRB); replaced the 2012 BS Act.
  • Core purpose: monetary and financial stability, price stability, sound payment system.
  • NRB is an autonomous corporate body; headed by a Board of Directors under the Governor.
  • Exclusive power to issue currency; manages foreign-exchange reserves.
  • Roles: banker to government, banker to banks, lender of last resort, public-debt manager.
  • Regulates, licenses, inspects and supervises BFIs; formulates monetary and forex policy.
  • Provides Governor's tenure/removal safeguards to protect central-bank independence.

12. Bank and Financial Institution Act (BAFIA), 2073 (बैंक तथा वित्तीय संस्था सम्बन्धी ऐन)

BAFIA, 2073 (2017 AD) is the umbrella law that governs the establishment, operation, management, regulation and dissolution of banks and financial institutions (BFIs) in Nepal. It replaced BAFIA 2063 and earlier separate Acts for commercial banks, development banks and finance companies, consolidating them into a single, unified framework. Its purpose is to make the banking and financial system trustworthy, healthy, competitive and reliable, and to protect the rights and interests of depositors.

15. Negotiable Instrument Act, 2034 (विनिमेय अधिकारपत्र ऐन)

The Negotiable Instrument Act, 2034 (1977 AD) governs negotiable instruments — written documents that contain an unconditional promise or order to pay a certain sum of money, and that can be freely transferred from one person to another so that the transferee gets a good title. The Act's purpose is to give legal certainty and enforceability to these instruments, which are the everyday tools of trade and banking payments.

The three main negotiable instruments are the promissory note (a written promise by one party to pay another), the bill of exchange (a written order by one party directing a second party to pay a third), and the cheque (a bill of exchange drawn on a bank, payable on demand). Key features of negotiability are: free transferability (by delivery, or by endorsement and delivery), the holder in due course concept (a person who takes the instrument in good faith and for value gets a clear, protected title), and presumptions in favour of the holder.

The Act defines important terms and rules: parties (drawer, drawee, payee, endorser, endorsee, holder), endorsement (signing to transfer), crossing of cheques (general and special, restricting to bank collection for safety), dishonour of an instrument (by non-acceptance or non-payment) and the consequent notice and liability, and rules on maturity, negotiation and discharge. It also deals with the bank's duties and protections when paying or collecting cheques (e.g. protection for a paying banker acting in good faith).

Importance and Nepal context: cheques and bills remain central to Nepal's banking, so this Act underpins every cheque a bank like RBB honours, dishonours or collects. It is closely linked to banking-offence law where dishonour involves fraud. Example: if a cheque is returned for insufficient funds, the payee's legal remedies and the drawer's liability are determined under this Act.

GROUP ग — Accounting System

1. Double Entry System of Accounting and its Features

Accounting is the language of business. It is the process of recording, classifying, summarising and reporting the financial transactions of an organisation so that owners, managers, lenders and the government can understand how the business is doing. The Double Entry System is the scientific method of accounting that is used all over the world, including by every bank in Nepal such as Rastriya Banijya Bank (RBB).

The core idea is simple: every business transaction has two sides (a dual effect). If something comes in, something else goes out; if you receive a benefit, you must give a benefit in return. For example, if a bank pays cash Rs. 50,000 to buy a computer, the bank gains a computer (asset in) and loses cash (asset out). The double entry system records both sides of this event — one as a debit (Dr.) and the other as a credit (Cr.). For every transaction the total of debits must equal the total of credits. This is why the books always stay in balance.

The system rests on the accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Capital (Equity). Because every entry keeps this equation balanced, the double entry system is self-checking. It works through a set of rules called the golden rules of accounting, which tell us which account to debit and which to credit.

The Golden Rules (traditional / British approach)

Type of accountRule for DebitRule for Credit
Personal account (persons, firms, banks)Debit the receiverCredit the giver
Real account (assets, property)Debit what comes inCredit what goes out
Nominal account (expenses, incomes, losses, gains)Debit all expenses and lossesCredit all incomes and gains

Worked micro-example. Suppose RBB pays Rs. 5,000 as office rent in cash. "Rent" is an expense (nominal account) and "Cash" is an asset (real account). Applying the rules: debit all expenses → Rent A/c Dr. Rs. 5,000; credit what goes out → To Cash A/c Rs. 5,000. Debit equals credit, so the books stay balanced.

The double entry system is important because it gives a complete and accurate record, helps prepare the Trial Balance, Profit & Loss Account and Balance Sheet, prevents and detects fraud and errors, and satisfies legal and tax requirements. Its main limitation is that it needs trained staff and is more time-consuming than single-entry bookkeeping.

  • Exam pointers
  • Every transaction has a dual aspect — one debit and one matching credit; total debits always equal total credits.
  • The system was systematised by Luca Pacioli (Italy, 1494), often called the "Father of Accounting".
  • Foundation equation: Assets = Liabilities + Capital.
  • Golden rules: Personal — debit the receiver, credit the giver; Real — debit what comes in, credit what goes out; Nominal — debit expenses/losses, credit incomes/gains.
  • Features: dual aspect, complete record, arithmetical accuracy check (Trial Balance), scientific and self-balancing.
  • Merits: accurate profit/loss, easy fraud detection, legally accepted; Demerits: costly and needs skilled manpower.

2. Cash Account and Petty Cash (Imprest System)

The Cash Account (Cash Book) is the book of original entry that records all receipts and payments of cash. It works like a ledger account: money received is written on the debit (left) side and money paid out is written on the credit (right) side. The difference between the two sides is the cash balance in hand. A Cash Account can never show a credit balance, because you cannot pay out more cash than you actually have.

In a bank or a large office, hundreds of small payments happen every day — tea, stationery, postage, taxi fare, photocopies. Recording each tiny payment in the main cash book would waste the chief cashier's time. To solve this, a small amount of money is given to a junior clerk called the petty cashier to meet these small day-to-day expenses. This is petty cash, and it is recorded in a separate Petty Cash Book.

The most common way to run petty cash is the Imprest System. Here the petty cashier is first given a fixed sum (the "imprest" or float), say Rs. 10,000, for a fixed period (a week or month). The petty cashier spends from it and keeps vouchers. At the end of the period, the main cashier reimburses exactly the amount that was spent, so the petty cashier again starts the next period with the full imprest of Rs. 10,000. This keeps the float constant and makes control easy.

  • Types of cash book: single column, double column (cash + discount / cash + bank), and three column.

  • Imprest means a fixed advance float; reimbursement equals the amount spent so the float is restored to its original level.
  • Benefits: control over small payments, division of labour, fewer entries in the main cash book, clear responsibility and audit trail.
  • Every petty payment must be supported by a voucher.

3. Various Types of Accounts (Personal, Real, Nominal)

Under the traditional classification, every ledger account falls into one of three groups. Knowing the type of an account tells you which golden rule to apply, so this classification is the gateway to correct journal entries.

A Personal Account relates to persons, firms, companies or institutions with whom the business deals — for example, a customer, a supplier, RBB itself, Nepal Rastra Bank, or a capital account of the owner. Personal accounts can be natural persons (Ram, Sita), artificial/legal persons (a company, a bank, a co-operative) or representative persons (outstanding salary account, prepaid rent account, which represent amounts due to/from people). Rule: debit the receiver, credit the giver.

A Real Account relates to assets and property, whether tangible (cash, building, machinery, furniture, stock) or intangible (goodwill, patents, trademarks). These accounts are not closed at year end; their balances are carried to the Balance Sheet. Rule: debit what comes in, credit what goes out.

A Nominal Account relates to expenses, losses, incomes and gains — for example rent, salary, interest paid, commission received, discount, and profit. These accounts exist only to measure profit or loss; they are closed at year end by transferring their balances to the Profit & Loss Account. Rule: debit all expenses and losses, credit all incomes and gains.

4. Trial Balance

A Trial Balance is a statement that lists the closing balances of all ledger accounts on a particular date, with debit balances in one column and credit balances in another. Because the double entry system records equal debits and credits for every transaction, the total of the debit column should equal the total of the credit column. The Trial Balance is therefore an arithmetical check on the accuracy of the ledger.

Its main purposes are: (1) to test the arithmetical accuracy of the books, (2) to provide a summary of all ledger balances in one place, and (3) to serve as the base for preparing the final accounts — the Profit & Loss Account and the Balance Sheet. In practice, assets, expenses and losses carry debit balances, while liabilities, capital, incomes and gains carry credit balances.

Important caution: a Trial Balance that agrees does not guarantee that the books are error-free. Some errors do not affect agreement — for example, a transaction completely omitted, an entry posted to the wrong (but same-side) account, or two errors that cancel each other (compensating errors). So agreement is a comfort, not a proof.

5. Profit & Loss Account

The Profit & Loss Account (P&L) — also called the Income Statement — is prepared at the end of an accounting period to find out whether the business earned a net profit or suffered a net loss. It matches the incomes earned during the period against the expenses incurred to earn them. If income exceeds expenses, the result is net profit; if expenses exceed income, the result is net loss.

In a trading business the process has two stages. First the Trading Account finds the gross profit (Sales − Cost of goods sold). Then the Profit & Loss Account takes that gross profit, adds other incomes, deducts operating expenses (salaries, rent, interest, depreciation), and gives the net profit. The net profit is finally transferred to the capital account (or retained earnings) in the Balance Sheet. For a bank, the equivalent is interest income and fee income less interest expense and operating costs.

The P&L Account is prepared on the accrual basis: incomes and expenses are recorded when they are earned or incurred, not necessarily when cash is received or paid. This is why adjustments such as outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, accrued income and depreciation are made before arriving at the final profit figure.

  • Prepared on the accrual (matching) basis; needs adjustments (outstanding, prepaid, depreciation).
  • Net profit increases capital; net loss reduces capital.
  • It relates to a period (e.g. a year), unlike the Balance Sheet which is at a point in time.

6. Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet is a statement of the financial position of a business on a particular date. It lists what the business owns (assets) and what it owes (liabilities and owner's capital/equity). It is built directly on the accounting equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity (Capital)

Because the two sides come from the same equation, the totals must always be equal — that is why it is called a "balance" sheet. Assets are usually grouped into fixed (non-current) assets such as land, building and machinery, and current assets such as cash, debtors and stock. Liabilities are grouped into long-term (non-current) liabilities such as loans, and current liabilities such as creditors and outstanding expenses. Capital is the owner's stake, increased by profit and reduced by drawings and losses.

The Balance Sheet is important because it shows the solvency and liquidity of the business, tells owners the net worth of their investment, and helps lenders and regulators (such as Nepal Rastra Bank for a bank) judge financial strength. It is a snapshot at a moment in time, whereas the P&L Account covers a whole period.

7. Role of Internal and External Audit in Bank Operation

Audit is the independent examination of the financial records and operations of an organisation to check whether they give a true and fair view and follow the rules. In a bank like RBB, which handles public deposits, audit is critical for protecting depositors, maintaining public confidence and meeting regulatory requirements. There are two broad types: internal audit and external (statutory) audit.

Internal Audit is carried out by the bank's own internal audit department (or an outsourced firm reporting to the Audit Committee of the Board). It is a continuous, ongoing activity. Its role is to check that internal controls are working, that transactions follow the bank's policies and NRB directives, that fraud and errors are prevented and detected early, that assets are safeguarded, and that risk management is effective. Internal auditors report to the management and the Board's Audit Committee, helping them run the bank better. Under good corporate governance, the internal audit function is kept independent of day-to-day operations.

External Audit (statutory audit) is done by an independent, qualified chartered accountant / audit firm appointed from outside the bank, usually by the shareholders in the Annual General Meeting. It is normally an annual exercise. The external auditor examines the final accounts and expresses an independent opinion on whether the financial statements show a true and fair view and comply with accounting standards (NFRS/NAS), the Companies Act, the Banks and Financial Institutions Act (BAFIA) and NRB regulations. The audited report is submitted to shareholders and regulators.

8. Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy is the use of the government's revenue (taxation) and expenditure (spending) to influence the economy. In Nepal it is carried out by the Government of Nepal through the Ministry of Finance and is announced every year in the national budget. While monetary policy is about money and interest rates (run by the central bank), fiscal policy is about the government's own income and spending decisions.

The main tools of fiscal policy are: (1) Public revenue — mainly taxes such as income tax, VAT, customs duty and excise, plus non-tax revenue like fees and dividends; (2) Public expenditure — spending on development (roads, schools, hospitals) and on regular administration; (3) Public debt / borrowing — internal and external loans to finance a deficit; and (4) Deficit financing. By changing tax rates and spending levels, the government can speed up or cool down the economy.

The objectives of fiscal policy in a developing country like Nepal include: promoting economic growth and capital formation, generating employment, reducing poverty and inequality (redistribution of income), maintaining price stability, mobilising domestic resources, and financing development priorities. In a slowdown the government follows an expansionary policy (more spending, lower taxes); in times of high inflation it may follow a contractionary policy (less spending, higher taxes).

Budget balance: Surplus when revenue > expenditure; Deficit when expenditure > revenue (the common case in Nepal, financed by borrowing and grants).

9. Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is the policy by which the central bank controls the supply of money, the availability of credit and interest rates in the economy. In Nepal it is formulated and implemented by Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) and announced annually (with periodic reviews). Its ultimate aims are price stability, a stable exchange rate, and support for balanced economic growth and financial stability.

NRB uses two kinds of tools. Quantitative (general) tools affect the overall quantity of credit, while qualitative (selective) tools direct credit to or away from particular sectors (for example margin requirements, moral suasion, and directed lending to priority sectors like agriculture and small business).

10. Commercial (Trade) Policy

Commercial policy, also called trade policy or foreign trade policy, is the set of government rules and instruments that govern a country's imports and exports with the rest of the world. It decides how open or protected the domestic market will be, how to promote exports, and how to manage the balance of trade. In Nepal it is guided mainly by the periodically revised Trade Policy and related laws, and administered by the Ministry of Industry, Commerce and Supplies.

Trade policy can broadly be of two kinds. A free trade policy keeps few restrictions, letting goods flow with low tariffs. A protective policy uses barriers to shield domestic industry from foreign competition. The main tools are tariff barriers (customs duties on imports/exports) and non-tariff barriers such as quotas, licensing, standards, and outright bans. Governments also give export incentives — cash subsidies, duty drawback, tax rebates and simplified procedures — to make exports more competitive.

For Nepal the objectives are especially important because it is a landlocked, least-developed country with a large and growing trade deficit (imports far exceed exports). Trade policy aims to promote and diversify exports, reduce dependence on a few products and markets (mainly India), substitute some imports through domestic production, integrate into regional and global trade, and improve the balance of payments. Nepal is a member of the WTO (since 2004), SAFTA/SAARC and BIMSTEC, which shape its trade commitments.

  • Two broad types: free trade and protectionism.

11. Industrial Policy

Industrial policy is the government's overall statement of principles, strategies and rules for the establishment, promotion, protection and regulation of industries in the country. It sets the direction for industrialisation — which sectors to encourage, how to attract investment, and how to create jobs and add value domestically. Nepal has issued industrial policies in several years (for example the Industrial Policy 2010/2067), supported by the Industrial Enterprises Act and the Foreign Investment and Technology Transfer Act (FITTA).

It also lays down rules on labour, the environment and industrial security.

The objectives of Nepal's industrial policy are to increase the contribution of industry to GDP, create employment, promote exports and import substitution, attract domestic and foreign direct investment (FDI), transfer technology and skills, ensure balanced regional development, and move toward sustainable, environment-friendly industrialisation. The main challenges are low investment, an energy and infrastructure gap, labour issues, and a difficult business environment — the very problems the policy tries to solve.

14. Concepts and Principles of Management

Management is the process of planning, organising, staffing, directing and controlling the human, financial and physical resources of an organisation to achieve its goals efficiently (with least waste) and effectively (achieving the intended result). In simple words, management is getting things done through and with people. It applies to every organisation — a bank, a hospital, a school or a government office. Management is often described as both a science (it has principles and a body of knowledge) and an art (it needs skill and judgement in applying them).

The functions of management are the ongoing activities every manager performs. A popular short version is POLC — Planning, Organising, Leading, Controlling. A fuller classic version by Luther Gulick is POSDCORB — Planning, Organising, Staffing, Directing, Coordinating, Reporting, Budgeting. These describe how a manager turns objectives into results.

The most famous principles of management were given by Henri Fayol, a French engineer regarded as the father of modern (administrative) management. He identified five functions (plan, organise, command, coordinate, control) and 14 principles that guide good management practice. These principles are flexible guidelines, not rigid laws.

Fayol's 14 Principles (in brief)

  1. Division of work (specialisation)
  2. Authority and responsibility (go together)
  3. Discipline
  4. Unity of command (one boss per employee)
  5. Unity of direction (one plan per group with one objective)
  6. Subordination of individual interest to general interest
  7. Remuneration (fair pay)
  8. Centralisation and decentralisation (right balance)
  9. Scalar chain (clear line of authority)
  10. Order (right person/thing in right place)
  11. Equity (fairness and kindness)
  12. Stability of tenure of personnel
  13. Initiative
  14. Esprit de corps (team spirit)

These concepts matter because they give managers a common framework to organise work, avoid confusion (e.g. unity of command), motivate staff (fair pay, equity, initiative) and build teamwork — all directly useful in running a bank branch or an IT department.

  • Exam pointers
  • Management = achieving goals efficiently and effectively through people; it is both a science and an art.
  • Functions: POLC (Planning, Organising, Leading, Controlling) or the fuller POSDCORB (Gulick).
  • Henri Fayol = father of modern management; gave 5 functions and 14 principles.
  • Remember key principles: unity of command, unity of direction, scalar chain, division of work, esprit de corps.
  • F.W. Taylor = father of Scientific Management (work study, standardisation) — often contrasted with Fayol.
  • Levels of management: top, middle, lower (supervisory).

15. Leadership, Motivation, Decision-making, Participative Management and Control

Leadership is the ability to influence and guide people so that they willingly work toward group goals. A leader provides direction, inspires confidence and sets an example. Common leadership styles are autocratic (leader decides alone), democratic/participative (leader consults the team), laissez-faire (leader gives freedom), and transformational (leader inspires change through vision). Leadership differs from mere authority: a manager has formal authority, but a leader earns followers.

Motivation is the inner drive that makes people put in effort. Motivated staff are more productive, loyal and creative. Key theories include Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (physiological → safety → social → esteem → self-actualisation), Herzberg's Two-Factor theory (hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction; motivators create satisfaction), and McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y (pessimistic vs optimistic views of workers). Motivation can be financial (pay, bonus, incentives) or non-financial (recognition, promotion, good work environment).

Decision-making is the process of choosing the best course of action from among alternatives. A rational process runs: define the problem → gather information → identify alternatives → evaluate them → choose the best → implement → review the result. Decisions may be programmed (routine, rule-based) or non-programmed (new, complex, strategic). Good decision-making needs relevant information, which is why data and ICT support it strongly.

Participative management means involving employees in decisions that affect them — through consultation, committees, suggestion schemes, works councils or joint problem-solving. It improves the quality of decisions, raises motivation and commitment, reduces resistance to change, and builds a sense of ownership. Control is the final management function: it means setting standards, measuring actual performance, comparing it against the standard, finding deviations and taking corrective action. Control closes the loop so that plans actually get achieved.

Steps in the control process

  1. Set standards (targets, benchmarks)
  2. Measure actual performance
  3. Compare actual with standard
  4. Analyse deviations
  5. Take corrective action / feedback
  • Exam pointers
  • Leadership styles: autocratic, democratic/participative, laissez-faire, transformational.
  • Motivation theories: Maslow (5-level hierarchy), Herzberg (hygiene vs motivators), McGregor (Theory X/Y).
  • Motivation = financial + non-financial incentives.
  • Decision types: programmed (routine) vs non-programmed (strategic); follow the rational step-by-step process.
  • Participative management raises commitment and reduces resistance to change.
  • Control process (5 steps): set standard → measure → compare → find deviation → correct; control and planning are twin functions.

17. Corporate Governance

Corporate governance is the system of rules, practices and processes by which a company is directed and controlled. It defines the relationship among the Board of Directors, management, shareholders and other stakeholders, and ensures that the company is run honestly, responsibly and in the interest of all stakeholders — not just a few insiders. For banks it is especially important because they run on public deposits and public trust.

Good corporate governance rests on core principles, often summarised as TARP: Transparency, Accountability, Responsibility and Fairness (plus independence and integrity). Transparency means honest and timely disclosure of information; accountability means those in charge answer for their actions; responsibility means following laws and ethics; and fairness means treating all shareholders and stakeholders equitably. A key structure is the Board of Directors with independent directors and specialised committees such as the Audit Committee and Risk Management Committee.

In Nepal, corporate governance of banks is enforced through the Companies Act, the Banks and Financial Institutions Act (BAFIA), the Securities laws, and above all Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) directives on board composition, disclosure, related-party lending and risk management. Good governance builds investor and depositor confidence, protects minority shareholders, reduces fraud and mismanagement, improves performance, and helps the institution survive in the long term. Weak governance, by contrast, leads to scandals, loss of trust and even collapse.

Low-Level versus High-Level Programming Languages

A programming language is classified as low-level or high-level based on how close its instructions are to the machine's native instruction set versus how close they are to human/problem-domain language. Low-level languages include machine language (binary opcodes and operands executed directly by the CPU) and assembly language (mnemonic representation of machine instructions such as MOV, ADD, JMP, translated to machine code by an assembler). High-level languages such as C, C++, Java, Python, and COBOL use English-like syntax, arithmetic expressions, and structured control constructs that are translated to machine code by a compiler or interpreter.

The key working difference is the level of abstraction from hardware. Low-level code manipulates registers, memory addresses, and flags directly, giving the programmer full control over hardware resources (memory layout, I/O ports, interrupts) but making programs hardware-dependent, hard to read, and time-consuming to write and debug. High-level languages abstract away registers and addresses using variables, data types, functions, and libraries; the same source code (with minor changes) can often run on different machine architectures because the compiler/interpreter handles the hardware-specific translation. This abstraction is achieved through a translator: an assembler converts assembly to machine code one-to-one; a compiler converts an entire high-level program to machine code (or object code) before execution; an interpreter translates and executes high-level statements one at a time without producing a standalone machine-code file.

Example: to add two numbers, an assembly program on a simple CPU may need several explicit instructions (LOAD A, LOAD B, ADD, STORE C), while the equivalent high-level statement is simply C = A + B;. The high-level statement is portable and readable; the assembly version is fast, compact, and gives precise control but is tied to that CPU's instruction set. Low-level languages are still used for device drivers, embedded firmware, operating-system kernels, and performance-critical routines, while high-level languages dominate application, web, and business software development because they boost programmer productivity and maintainability.

Data Encoding Schemes: ASCII, EBCDIC and Unicode

Computers store and process all data, including text, as binary numbers, so a character encoding scheme defines a fixed mapping between each character (letters, digits, punctuation, control codes) and a unique bit pattern. ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange) is a 7-bit code (extended to 8 bits) supporting 128 (or 256) characters, covering English letters, digits, and basic symbols; it is the most widely used encoding in PCs, the internet, and programming languages. EBCDIC (Extended Binary Coded Decimal Interchange Code) is an 8-bit encoding developed by IBM for mainframe and midrange systems (System/360 and successors); it supports 256 characters but arranges them in a different, non-contiguous bit pattern than ASCII, so ASCII and EBCDIC systems cannot exchange raw text without conversion.

The major limitation of both ASCII and EBCDIC is that they were designed around English/Latin characters and cannot represent the vast number of characters used in world languages (Devanagari, Chinese, Arabic, emoji, mathematical symbols, etc.). Unicode solves this by assigning a unique code point to every character in every major writing system, using a 21-bit conceptual code space (over 1.1 million possible code points, of which about 149,000+ are assigned). Unicode is implemented through encoding forms such as UTF-8 (variable length, 1–4 bytes, backward-compatible with ASCII for the first 128 characters), UTF-16 (2 or 4 bytes, used internally by Windows/Java), and UTF-32 (fixed 4 bytes per character).

Example: the letter "A" is 1000001 (65) in ASCII, but its EBCDIC bit pattern is entirely different (11000001, hex C1, but at a different relative position than digits), which is why old file transfers between IBM mainframes and other computers needed a translation table. In Unicode, "A" is code point U+0041 (same numeric value as ASCII for compatibility), but Devanagari "क" is U+0915, and it can be encoded in UTF-8 as multiple bytes. Unicode is now the universal standard for the web, operating systems, and databases because it enables one system to correctly display and process every language and symbol without ambiguity or data loss.

  • Exam pointers
  • ASCII: 7-bit (128 characters) or extended 8-bit (256); used in PCs and internet protocols.
  • EBCDIC: 8-bit, IBM mainframe standard, incompatible bit ordering with ASCII, needs conversion for interchange.
  • Unicode: universal, multilingual, code points expressed as U+hhhh; encoded via UTF-8/16/32.
  • UTF-8 is byte-oriented and ASCII-compatible, making it the dominant encoding on the web (HTML, JSON, most modern software).
  • Key exam angle: why Unicode was needed (limitation of ASCII/EBCDIC = limited character repertoire, no support for non-Latin scripts, incompatibility between systems).

Communication and Networking: An Overview

Data communication is the exchange of data between two devices through a transmission medium such as wire, cable, or wireless channel. A communication system requires five components: (1) a message (the data to be communicated), (2) a sender, (3) a receiver, (4) a transmission medium (the physical path), and (5) a protocol (the agreed set of rules governing data communication). A computer network is a collection of interconnected computing devices that can exchange data and share resources such as printers, files, and internet connections.

Networks are commonly classified by geographic scope: LAN (Local Area Network, confined to a building/campus), MAN (Metropolitan Area Network, a city-wide network), and WAN (Wide Area Network, spanning countries/continents, e.g., the internet). Networks are also classified by topology (bus, star, ring, mesh), by transmission mode (simplex, half-duplex, full-duplex), and by switching technique (circuit switching, packet switching). Communication can be wired (twisted pair, coaxial, fiber optic) or wireless (radio, microwave, satellite, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth).

For example, when an employee at an RBB branch prints a report, the request travels from the workstation over the branch LAN (star topology, twisted-pair cable, Ethernet protocol) to a shared printer; when the same employee accesses the core banking system hosted at a data center in another city, the request goes out over a WAN link (leased line or MPLS/internet VPN) using the TCP/IP protocol suite. Networking enables resource sharing, centralized data processing, real-time transaction processing, and remote/branchless banking, which is fundamental to modern banking operations.

Internet and Web Technologies: An Overview

The internet is a global network of interconnected networks that communicate using the standardized TCP/IP protocol suite, allowing millions of independent networks and billions of devices to exchange data seamlessly. Every device connected to the internet is identified by an IP address (IPv4, 32-bit, e.g., 192.168.1.1, or the newer IPv6, 128-bit, to overcome IPv4 address exhaustion). The Domain Name System (DNS) translates human-readable domain names (e.g., rbb.com.np) into numeric IP addresses so users do not need to memorize numbers.

The World Wide Web (WWW) is a system of interlinked hypertext documents (web pages) accessed via the internet using the HTTP/HTTPS protocol. Web pages are written in HTML (structure), styled with CSS (presentation), and made interactive with JavaScript (behavior); dynamic, database-driven sites additionally use server-side technologies (PHP, ASP.NET, Java/JSP, Python/Django) and databases (MySQL, SQL Server, Oracle). A web browser (client) sends an HTTP request to a web server, which returns the requested page/resource; HTTPS adds an SSL/TLS encryption layer for secure, authenticated communication, essential for e-banking.

Example: when a customer opens RBB's internet-banking site, the browser resolves the domain via DNS, opens a secure HTTPS/TLS connection to the bank's web server, submits login credentials over an encrypted channel, and the web application server (running server-side code) queries the core banking database and returns the account page dynamically rendered in HTML/CSS/JavaScript. Other important web/internet technologies include Web 2.0 (interactive, user-generated content), APIs and web services (SOAP/REST, enabling system-to-system integration such as connectized banking and mobile-wallet interfaces), cloud computing, and content delivery networks (CDNs).

Basic Principles of Information Security

Information security is the practice of protecting information and information systems from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. Its foundation is the CIA triad: Confidentiality ensures information is accessible only to authorized individuals (achieved via encryption, access control, authentication); Integrity ensures information is accurate and unaltered except by authorized action (achieved via checksums, hashing, digital signatures, access controls, version control); Availability ensures information and systems are accessible to authorized users when needed (achieved via redundancy, backups, disaster recovery, load balancing, protection against denial-of-service attacks).

Beyond the CIA triad, security frameworks add Authentication (verifying that a user or system is who it claims to be, e.g., passwords, OTP, biometrics, digital certificates), Authorization (granting the right level of access/privilege once identity is confirmed), and Non-repudiation (ensuring a party cannot deny having performed an action, achieved via digital signatures and audit logs). Common threats include malware (viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware), phishing, man-in-the-middle attacks, SQL injection, denial-of-service (DoS/DDoS) attacks, and insider threats. Countermeasures include firewalls, intrusion detection/prevention systems (IDS/IPS), antivirus software, encryption, strong password/multi-factor authentication policies, regular patching, security audits, and staff awareness training.

Example in a banking context: an ATM transaction must keep the customer's PIN and account balance confidential (encryption over the network), must not allow the transaction amount to be altered in transit (integrity, via message authentication codes), must ensure the ATM/core banking system is up and working when the customer needs it (availability, via redundant servers and network links), must verify it is really the account holder transacting (authentication via PIN/card chip), and must produce a non-repudiable log so the customer cannot later deny withdrawing cash (transaction logs, digital signatures). Information security in banks is also governed by regulatory frameworks (e.g., NRB IT guidelines, ISO/IEC 27001) requiring formal information security management systems (ISMS), risk assessment, and business continuity/disaster recovery planning.

  • Exam pointers
  • CIA triad: Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — always define and give a mechanism for each.
  • Extended principles: Authentication, Authorization, Non-repudiation, Accountability.
  • Common threats: malware, phishing, DoS/DDoS, SQL injection, insider threat, social engineering.
  • Common controls: encryption, firewalls, IDS/IPS, MFA, backups, patch management, security audit, ISMS/ISO 27001.
  • Relate to banking: PIN/OTP confidentiality, transaction integrity, 24x7 core banking availability, audit trails for non-repudiation, disaster recovery/BCP.

Information Systems: Concepts and Types

An information system (IS) is an organized combination of people, hardware, software, communication networks, data resources, and procedures that collects, processes, stores, and disseminates information to support decision-making, coordination, control, analysis, and visualization in an organization. The core cycle of any IS is input to process to output, with feedback used to evaluate and refine the input stage, all wrapped by a control mechanism that ensures the system meets its objectives.

Information systems are commonly classified by the organizational level and function they support. Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) handle routine, day-to-day business transactions (e.g., deposit/withdrawal recording in core banking). Management Information Systems (MIS) summarize TPS data into structured reports for middle management. Decision Support Systems (DSS) provide interactive, model-based tools to support semi-structured or unstructured managerial decisions (e.g., loan risk analysis). Executive Information Systems (EIS), also called Executive Support Systems, give senior executives quick, high-level, graphical dashboards of organizational performance. Expert Systems (ES) use a knowledge base and inference engine to mimic expert human decision-making in narrow domains (e.g., credit-scoring rule engines). Office Automation Systems (OAS) support day-to-day communication and documentation (email, word processing, scheduling).

Example: in RBB, the core banking application recording every deposit, withdrawal, and loan repayment is a TPS; a monthly branch-performance report summarizing transaction volumes for regional managers is generated by an MIS; a loan-approval model that lets a credit officer test different interest rates and tenures against a customer repayment capacity is a DSS; and a dashboard showing the CEO real-time NPA (non-performing asset) ratios across all branches is an EIS. These systems are often layered, with TPS data feeding MIS reports, which feed DSS models, which ultimately inform EIS dashboards, forming an integrated organizational information architecture.

  • Exam pointers
  • Definition: IS = people + hardware + software + data + procedures + network, organized to produce information for decision-making.
  • Basic model: Input to Process to Output to Feedback, under Control.
  • Pyramid of systems by management level: TPS (operational) leads to MIS (middle management) leads to DSS (tactical/semi-structured) leads to EIS (strategic/top management); ES and OAS support specialized/clerical functions across levels.

  • Distinguish structured (TPS, MIS), semi-structured (DSS), and unstructured (EIS, top-management strategic) decision types.

Systems Analysis and Design: Modern Techniques

Systems analysis and design (SA&D) is the process of studying an existing or proposed business system, identifying its information requirements, and designing a new or improved system (manual or computerized) that meets those requirements efficiently. The systems analyst acts as a bridge between end users/management and technical developers, gathering requirements through interviews, questionnaires, observation, and document/record review, then modeling the system logically before it is designed physically.

Traditional SA&D followed the structured approach, using tools such as Data Flow Diagrams (DFD) to model process/data flow, Entity-Relationship Diagrams (ERD) to model data, decision tables/trees for complex logic, and structured English/pseudocode for process specification, all under the classical Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC/waterfall). Modern SA&D has largely shifted toward the object-oriented approach, using the Unified Modeling Language (UML) (use case, class, sequence, activity, state diagrams) to model the system as a set of interacting objects with attributes and behaviors, which maps more naturally onto object-oriented programming languages and reusable components.

Modern techniques also include prototyping (building a working mock-up early to validate requirements with users before full development), Rapid Application Development (RAD) (using CASE tools, reusable components, and iterative user feedback to shorten development time), agile methodologies (Scrum, XP; iterative, incremental delivery with continuous stakeholder collaboration instead of a rigid sequential process), and CASE (Computer-Aided Software Engineering) tools that automate diagramming, code generation, and documentation. For example, a bank redesigning its loan-processing workflow today would likely use UML use-case and activity diagrams to model borrower/loan-officer interactions, build a clickable prototype for user validation, and deliver the system in agile sprints rather than a single big-bang release, reducing risk and improving user acceptance compared with pure waterfall/structured methods.

  • Exam pointers
  • Structured (traditional) tools: DFD, ERD, decision table/tree, structured English, process-centric.
  • Object-oriented (modern) tools: UML diagrams, object/data-and-behavior-centric, better for reusable, component-based systems.
  • Modern methodologies: prototyping, RAD, Agile/Scrum, CASE tools, faster, more iterative, more user-involved than classic waterfall.
  • Role of the systems analyst: requirement gathering (interview, questionnaire, observation, document review), feasibility study, modeling, and liaison between users and developers.

Systems Overview and the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

A system is a set of interrelated components working together toward a common objective by accepting inputs and producing outputs through an organized transformation process; systems can be open (interact with environment) or closed, and have boundaries, subsystems, interfaces, and control mechanisms. The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a structured, phased approach used to plan, create, test, and deploy an information system, ensuring the final product meets user requirements within budget and time constraints.

The classical SDLC (waterfall model) consists of sequential phases: (1) Preliminary investigation/Problem definition, identify the problem and conduct a feasibility study (technical, economic, operational, schedule); (2) Requirement analysis, gather and document what the system must do (often producing an SRS); (3) System design, translate requirements into logical design (DFD/ERD/UML) and physical design (architecture, database schema, UI, hardware); (4) Implementation/coding, write and unit-test the program code; (5) Testing, integration, system, and acceptance testing to verify the system meets requirements; (6) Deployment/Implementation, install the system, convert data, and train users (conversion strategies: direct/parallel/pilot/phased); (7) Maintenance, ongoing correction, adaptation, and enhancement after deployment.

Alternative SDLC models address waterfall's rigidity: the prototyping model builds a working sample early for user feedback; the spiral model combines iterative prototyping with formal risk analysis at each cycle, suited to large, high-risk projects; the incremental/iterative model delivers the system in usable increments; and agile models deliver working software in short sprints with continuous feedback. Example: RBB migrating to a new core banking system would run a feasibility study, gather requirements from branch/operations staff (SRS), design the data migration and module architecture, code and unit test modules, run system/user-acceptance testing in a test environment, go live using a parallel or pilot-branch rollout to reduce risk, and then maintain the system through patches and enhancement requests, a lifecycle best supported by a phased/incremental rather than a single big-bang cutover.

  • Exam pointers
  • SDLC phases (classic waterfall): Feasibility study, Requirement analysis, Design, Implementation/coding, Testing, Deployment/conversion, Maintenance, in that order.
  • Feasibility types: technical, economic, operational, legal, schedule.
  • Conversion/rollout strategies: direct (abrupt), parallel (old+new together), pilot (one location first), phased (module by module).
  • Alternative models: waterfall, prototyping, spiral (risk-driven), incremental, agile, know when each is appropriate (spiral for high-risk/large systems, agile for evolving requirements).
  • Maintenance types (useful cross-reference): corrective, adaptive, perfective, preventive.

Management Information System (MIS)

A Management Information System (MIS) is an integrated, computer-based system that converts data from internal and external sources into timely, accurate, and relevant information, presented in a usable format to support planning, control, and decision-making at all management levels of an organization. MIS is not just software, it is a combination of people, procedures, databases, and devices designed to provide the right information to the right person at the right time in the right format.

An MIS typically draws data from transaction processing systems (operational level) and organizes it into structured, periodic, and exception reports for tactical and strategic decisions. Key subsystems commonly found in a bank's MIS include financial/accounting MIS, human-resource MIS, marketing/customer-relationship MIS, and loan/credit-risk MIS. Example: RBB's MIS aggregates daily transaction data from all branches to generate a weekly liquidity position report for treasury management, a monthly non-performing-loan exception report flagging accounts overdue beyond a threshold for the credit department, and an on-demand branch profitability report for regional managers preparing a board presentation. A well-designed MIS improves managerial efficiency, enables timely corrective action, supports regulatory reporting to Nepal Rastra Bank, and provides the data foundation for higher-level decision support and executive information systems.

Types of Programming Languages and Binding Policies

Programming languages are classified by generation and paradigm. By generation: 1GL (machine code), 2GL (assembly), 3GL (procedural, high-level, e.g., C, Pascal, Java), 4GL (very high-level, problem-oriented, e.g., SQL, report generators), and 5GL (declarative/knowledge-based, e.g., Prolog). By paradigm: procedural/imperative languages (C, Pascal) express computation as a sequence of statements/procedures that change program state; object-oriented languages (C++, Java, C#) organize code around objects that bundle data and behavior; functional languages (Haskell, Lisp, and functional features in Python/JavaScript) treat computation as evaluation of mathematical functions and avoid mutable state; logic/declarative languages (Prolog, SQL) specify what result is wanted rather than how to compute it; and scripting languages (Python, PHP, JavaScript) are typically interpreted and used for rapid development, automation, and web scripting.

Binding refers to the association of an attribute (such as a data type, value, memory address, or operation) with an entity (a variable, name, or identifier) at some point in the program's life; the time this association happens is called binding time. Bindings can occur at language design time (e.g., meaning of +), language implementation time (e.g., range of an int), compile time (e.g., variable's data type in statically typed languages), link time (e.g., calls to library routines), load time (e.g., static variable's memory address), and run time (e.g., dynamic memory allocation, value of a variable). A binding done before run time is called static binding (early binding); a binding delayed until execution is called dynamic binding (late binding).

Static binding gives faster execution and earlier error detection (e.g., type errors caught at compile time in C), while dynamic binding gives greater flexibility (e.g., run-time polymorphism through virtual functions in C++/Java, where the actual method invoked is decided at run time based on the object's real type). Example: in C, a variable declared int x; is statically bound to the type int at compile time and to a memory address at load/run time; in Java, calling an overridden method through a base-class reference (Shape s = new Circle(); s.draw();) is dynamically bound, so the JVM decides at run time to invoke Circle's draw(). This static-versus-dynamic binding distinction is central to understanding how OOP languages implement polymorphism and how compilers/interpreters optimize code.

  • Exam pointers
  • Language generations: 1GL to 5GL; paradigms: procedural, object-oriented, functional, logic/declarative, scripting.
  • Binding = association of an attribute with a name; binding time = when this happens.
  • Binding times in order: language design time, implementation time, compile time, link time, load time, run time.
  • Static (early) binding: resolved before execution, fast, type-safe, e.g., normal function calls and variable types in C.
  • Dynamic (late) binding: resolved at execution, flexible, basis of run-time polymorphism, e.g., virtual functions/method overriding in C++/Java.

Structured Programming

Structured programming is a programming paradigm that improves the clarity, quality, and development time of a program by using only three basic control structures: sequence (statements executed one after another in order), selection (conditional branching using if-else or switch), and iteration (repetition using while, for, or do-while loops). It was formalized based on the Bohm-Jacopini theorem, which proved that any computable function can be implemented using only these three constructs, eliminating the need for unrestricted goto statements that made older programs hard to read, debug, and maintain (so-called "spaghetti code").

A key technique in structured programming is top-down design (stepwise refinement): a complex problem is decomposed into smaller, manageable sub-problems (modules/functions/procedures), each solving one well-defined task, and each module is further refined until it can be directly coded. This produces modular programming, where modules communicate through well-defined parameters/interfaces and (ideally) have high cohesion (each module does one thing) and low coupling (modules are as independent of each other as possible), which makes programs easier to test, debug, reuse, and maintain.

Example: a program to process a bank's daily transactions can be structured into modules such as readTransaction(), validateTransaction(), updateAccountBalance(), and printReceipt(), each called in sequence from a main control module, each using selection to check for errors, and each using iteration to process all transactions in a batch, instead of one long block of code full of goto jumps. Structured programming, formalized in the languages Pascal, C, and Ada, remains the conceptual foundation beneath modern procedural and even object-oriented code, since methods/functions inside classes are still written using sequence, selection, and iteration.

  • Exam pointers
  • Three control structures: sequence, selection, iteration; based on the Bohm-Jacopini theorem, avoids unrestricted goto.
  • Top-down design/stepwise refinement: break a problem into modules, refine each until directly codable.
  • Goals: readability, easier debugging/testing, reusability, maintainability; concepts of high cohesion and low coupling.
  • Historical languages: ALGOL, Pascal, C, Ada, Modula-2.

Programming Language C: Key Features

C is a general-purpose, procedural, structured, and middle-level programming language developed by Dennis Ritchie at Bell Labs (1972), originally to implement the UNIX operating system. It is called a middle-level language because it combines the control and efficiency of low-level (assembly) programming (direct memory/pointer manipulation, bit-level operations) with the readability and portability of high-level languages.

Key features of C include: portability (C programs, written to the ANSI C standard, can be compiled and run on different hardware/OS platforms with little or no change); structured programming support (functions, blocks, and the three control structures, without needing goto); rich operator set (arithmetic, relational, logical, bitwise, increment/decrement, conditional/ternary); pointers (variables that store memory addresses, enabling direct memory access, dynamic memory allocation via malloc()/free(), efficient array/string handling, and pass-by-reference-like behavior via pass-by-address); modularity (programs organized into functions and separately compiled files linked together); a small set of keywords (32 in ANSI C) with a large standard library (stdio.h, stdlib.h, string.h, math.h, etc.) providing input/output, memory, string, and math functions; and speed/efficiency, since C compiles to compact, fast machine code, making it suitable for system programming.

Example: #include <stdio.h>
int main() {
  int a = 5, b = 3;
  printf("Sum = %d", a + b);
  return 0;
}. This program shows the structure of a C program: preprocessor directive (#include), the mandatory main() function as entry point, variable declarations, a library function call (printf), and a return statement. Because of its efficiency and closeness to hardware, C is widely used for operating systems, embedded systems, device drivers, and performance-critical system software, and it is the ancestor of C++, Java, and C#, making it foundational to understanding modern programming languages.

  • Exam pointers
  • C = middle-level, procedural, structured language by Dennis Ritchie (1972) at Bell Labs, for UNIX.
  • Core features: portability, structured constructs, rich operators, pointers, modularity (functions), rich standard library, compiled and fast.
  • Data types: int, float, double, char, derived types (arrays, pointers, structures, unions), void.
  • Program structure: preprocessor directives, main() function, declarations, statements, functions.
  • Know a short, correct example program and be ready to explain pointers and dynamic memory allocation (malloc, calloc, free) since these are common follow-up questions.

Software Requirement Specification (SRS)

A Software Requirement Specification (SRS) is a formal document that completely describes what a software system is expected to do (functional requirements) and the constraints under which it must operate (non-functional requirements), serving as the primary agreement/contract between the client/users and the development team. The SRS is produced at the end of the requirement-analysis phase of the SDLC and becomes the baseline against which the design, coding, and testing of the system are validated.

A good SRS should be correct (accurately reflects real needs), unambiguous (only one interpretation possible), complete (all requirements stated), consistent (no conflicting requirements), verifiable (each requirement can be tested), ranked for importance/stability, modifiable, and traceable (each requirement can be traced to its origin and to design/test artifacts). A widely used structure (based on IEEE 830) includes: introduction (purpose, scope, definitions), overall description (product perspective, functions, user characteristics, constraints), specific requirements (functional requirements, external interface requirements, performance requirements, design constraints, non-functional requirements such as security, reliability, usability), and appendices.

Example: an SRS for RBB's mobile-banking application would specify functional requirements (fund transfer, balance inquiry, bill payment, OTP-based login), non-functional requirements (must support 10,000 concurrent users, 99.9% uptime, data encrypted in transit and at rest, response time under 2 seconds), external interface requirements (integration with the core banking system and NPI/payment switch), and constraints (must comply with NRB regulations). Requirements are typically gathered through interviews, questionnaires, JAD (Joint Application Development) sessions, use cases, and document analysis, and are validated with stakeholders (requirement review/walkthrough) before design begins, since errors in requirements are the most expensive to fix if discovered late in the project.

  • Exam pointers
  • SRS = formal specification of functional + non-functional requirements + constraints; produced after requirement analysis, before design.
  • Characteristics of a good SRS: correct, unambiguous, complete, consistent, verifiable, modifiable, traceable, ranked by importance.
  • Typical IEEE 830 structure: Introduction, Overall description, Specific requirements (functional/non-functional/interface/performance), Appendices.
  • Requirement elicitation techniques: interview, questionnaire, observation, document analysis, JAD sessions, use cases/prototyping.
  • Emphasize: requirement errors are cheapest to fix at this stage and most expensive if discovered after deployment (cost-escalation curve).

Implementation Issues in Software Development

Implementation is the phase where the designed system is translated into actual working code and then deployed into the production/operational environment. It includes coding the modules per the design specification, integrating them, converting/migrating existing data, installing hardware/software, training users, and switching over from the old system (if any) to the new one. Several practical issues commonly arise during this phase.

Coding issues include choosing an appropriate programming language/platform, following coding standards/conventions for readability and maintainability, and managing version control when multiple developers work on the same codebase. Data conversion issues involve migrating data from the old system's format/structure to the new system without loss or corruption, which requires careful mapping, cleansing, and validation, especially critical in banking where account balances and transaction histories must reconcile exactly. Testing and integration issues arise when independently developed modules are combined and unexpected interface mismatches or performance bottlenecks surface. Training and change-management issues involve preparing end users (e.g., branch staff) to operate the new system, since resistance to change or inadequate training can cause the new system to fail even if it is technically sound. Conversion strategy issues require choosing between direct, parallel, pilot, or phased cutover, each with different risk/cost trade-offs, described further under SDLC.

Example: when RBB implements a new loan-management module, implementation issues include ensuring the new module correctly reads the existing customer master data (data conversion), coordinating with the core-banking vendor on API integration (integration issue), training loan officers on the new workflow (change management), and deciding to run the module in parallel with the manual process for one month before fully switching over (conversion strategy) to reduce the risk of business disruption. Poor handling of implementation issues, even with excellent design, is one of the most common reasons information-system projects fail or face costly delays.

  • Exam pointers
  • Implementation = coding + integration + data conversion + installation + training + cutover.
  • Key issue categories: coding standards/version control, data migration/cleansing, module integration, user training/resistance to change, conversion strategy selection.
  • Data conversion accuracy is especially critical in banking (balances must reconcile exactly, zero tolerance for data loss).
  • Link back to conversion strategies (direct/parallel/pilot/phased) already covered under SDLC.
  • Mention that inadequate user training/change management is a leading real-world cause of implementation failure, even for technically sound systems.

Software Testing, Verification and Validation

Software testing is the process of executing a program with the intent of finding defects (bugs) and confirming that it behaves as expected. Verification asks "are we building the product right?", it checks that each phase's output correctly implements the specification of the previous phase (e.g., does the design match the SRS), typically done through reviews, walkthroughs, and inspections without executing code. Validation asks "are we building the right product?", it checks that the final system actually satisfies the user's real needs and intended use, typically done by actually executing the software (dynamic testing) against user requirements.

Testing techniques are divided into black-box testing (tests functionality against specifications without knowledge of internal code structure; techniques include equivalence partitioning, boundary value analysis, and decision tables) and white-box testing (tests internal logic/structure with knowledge of the source code; techniques include statement coverage, branch/decision coverage, and path coverage). Testing is also organized by level: unit testing (testing individual functions/modules in isolation), integration testing (testing interfaces between combined modules, using top-down, bottom-up, or big-bang strategies), system testing (testing the complete, integrated system against the SRS, including performance, security, load/stress testing), and acceptance testing (validation by the client/end user, often as alpha testing done in-house and beta testing done by real users in the field, to obtain sign-off before deployment).

Example: for RBB's fund-transfer module, unit testing checks that the interest-calculation function returns the correct value for given inputs; integration testing checks that the fund-transfer module correctly calls the account-balance module and the audit-log module together; system testing checks the whole application under simulated peak transaction load; and user-acceptance testing has actual bank staff confirm the module meets their operational needs before go-live. Regression testing (re-running existing test cases after a change) is also essential whenever the system is modified, to ensure existing functionality is not broken.

  • Exam pointers
  • Verification = "building the product right" (static, spec-conformance, reviews/inspections); Validation = "building the right product" (dynamic, user-need conformance, actual execution).
  • Black-box (functional, specification-based) vs white-box (structural, code-based) testing; name at least one technique for each (equivalence partitioning/boundary value; statement/branch/path coverage).
  • Testing levels in order: Unit to Integration to System to Acceptance (alpha/beta).
  • Integration strategies: top-down, bottom-up, big-bang, sandwich/hybrid.
  • Mention regression testing and non-functional testing types (performance, load, stress, security, usability) as they often earn extra marks.

Software Reliability, Quality and Maintenance

Software reliability is the probability that software will operate without failure for a specified period under specified conditions; it is measured using metrics such as Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF), Mean Time To Failure (MTTF), and defect density (defects per thousand lines of code). Reliability is improved through rigorous requirement analysis, structured design, thorough testing, fault-tolerant design (redundancy, exception handling, graceful degradation), and formal reliability-growth models applied during testing.

Software quality is the degree to which software meets specified requirements and user expectations; it is commonly assessed against models such as McCall's quality factors (correctness, reliability, efficiency, integrity, usability, maintainability, flexibility, testability, portability, reusability, interoperability) or the international standard ISO/IEC 9126 / ISO 25010, which groups quality characteristics into functionality, reliability, usability, efficiency, maintainability, and portability. Software Quality Assurance (SQA) is the set of planned, systematic activities (standards, reviews, audits, metrics) that ensure the development process itself produces a quality product.

Software maintenance is the modification of software after delivery to correct faults, improve performance, or adapt to a changed environment; it typically consumes more effort over a system's lifetime than its original development. The four standard maintenance types are: corrective (fixing bugs/defects discovered in production), adaptive (modifying the software to work with a changed environment, e.g., a new operating system or a new NRB regulation), perfective (enhancing features or improving performance/maintainability based on user feedback), and preventive (re-engineering code to prevent future problems, improve maintainability, e.g., refactoring). Example: RBB's core banking system needs corrective maintenance when a bug causes incorrect interest calculation, adaptive maintenance when NRB issues a new reporting regulation, perfective maintenance when branch staff request a faster search feature, and preventive maintenance when the IT team refactors old modules to reduce future defect risk; consistently high reliability and quality reduce the long-term cost and risk of all four types of maintenance.

  • Exam pointers
  • Reliability metrics: MTBF, MTTF, defect density; improved via good design, testing, redundancy, fault tolerance.
  • Quality models: McCall's factors and ISO/IEC 9126/25010 (functionality, reliability, usability, efficiency, maintainability, portability).
  • SQA = planned process activities (standards, reviews, audits) ensuring quality is built in, not just tested in.
  • Four maintenance types: corrective, adaptive, perfective, preventive, know one example of each.
  • Maintenance typically costs more than initial development over the software lifecycle, a frequently tested fact.

Object-Oriented Programming: Inheritance, Encapsulation, Polymorphism and Abstraction

Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) is a programming paradigm that organizes software around objects, instances of classes, that bundle together data (attributes/state) and behavior (methods/operations), modeling real-world entities more naturally than purely procedural code. The four fundamental principles of OOP are encapsulation, abstraction, inheritance, and polymorphism.

Encapsulation is the bundling of data and the methods that operate on it within a single unit (class), combined with information hiding, restricting direct external access to an object's internal state (using access specifiers like private, protected, public) so that data can only be changed through well-defined methods, improving security and reducing unintended interference. Abstraction is the process of exposing only the essential features of an object while hiding implementation complexity, achieved through abstract classes and interfaces that define what an object does without specifying how, letting the programmer work at a higher conceptual level. Inheritance allows a new class (subclass/derived class) to acquire the attributes and methods of an existing class (superclass/base class), enabling code reuse and the modeling of an "is-a" relationship (e.g., SavingsAccount and CurrentAccount both inherit common fields/methods from a base class Account). Polymorphism (meaning "many forms") allows objects of different classes to be treated through a common interface, with the actual method executed determined by the object's real type at run time (run-time/dynamic polymorphism via method overriding and virtual functions) or by the same method name behaving differently based on parameters (compile-time/static polymorphism via method/operator overloading).

Example in banking software: a base class Account defines common attributes (accountNumber, balance) and a method calculateInterest(); SavingsAccount and FixedDepositAccount inherit from Account but override calculateInterest() with their own interest formula (inheritance and polymorphism); the balance attribute is kept private and modified only via deposit()/withdraw() methods that enforce business rules like minimum balance (encapsulation); and a teller module can call account.calculateInterest() on any account object without knowing or caring exactly which subclass it is, since the abstract interface hides that complexity (abstraction). These principles together make banking software more modular, reusable, secure, and easier to extend with new account types without modifying existing tested code (the open/closed principle).

  • Exam pointers
  • Four pillars: Encapsulation (data+method bundling, information hiding), Abstraction (essential features only, hides complexity), Inheritance (code reuse, "is-a" relationship), Polymorphism ("many forms", one interface many implementations).
  • Polymorphism types: compile-time/static (function/operator overloading) and run-time/dynamic (method overriding via virtual functions), tie this back to dynamic binding.
  • Access specifiers for encapsulation: private, protected, public.
  • Always give a class-hierarchy example (Account to SavingsAccount/CurrentAccount is a natural, examiner-friendly banking example).
  • Mention benefits: reusability, extensibility, maintainability, modeling real-world entities more naturally than procedural code.

Code Generation and Optimization

Code generation is the phase of the compilation process where the compiler's intermediate representation of a program (e.g., an abstract syntax tree or three-address code) is translated into target code, either machine code, assembly code, or bytecode (as in Java's .class files run by the JVM). The code generator must correctly implement the semantics of the source program while making efficient use of the target machine's registers, memory, and instruction set.

Code optimization is the process of transforming code (either the intermediate representation or the generated target code) to improve its efficiency, reducing execution time, memory usage, or power consumption, without changing its output/behavior. Optimizations are classified as machine-independent (applied to intermediate code, portable across target machines) or machine-dependent (applied to final code, exploits specific CPU features like registers and addressing modes). Common machine-independent techniques include: constant folding (evaluating constant expressions at compile time, e.g., replacing x = 2 + 3 with x = 5), dead-code elimination (removing code whose result is never used), common subexpression elimination (computing a repeated expression once and reusing the result), loop optimization (loop-invariant code motion: moving computations that do not change inside a loop to outside it; strength reduction: replacing expensive operations like multiplication with cheaper ones like addition; loop unrolling: reducing loop-control overhead by expanding loop bodies), and copy propagation (replacing a variable that is a copy of another with the original, enabling further optimization).

Example: given the code for (i = 0; i < n; i++) { y = x * 2; arr[i] = y + i; }, an optimizing compiler recognizes that y = x * 2 does not depend on the loop variable i and hoists it outside the loop (loop-invariant code motion), turning it into a single computation before the loop instead of n repeated computations, significantly improving performance for large n. Machine-dependent optimization would then, for instance, allocate i and y to CPU registers instead of memory (register allocation) for faster access. Good code generation and optimization are essential for performance-critical banking systems that must process thousands of transactions per second with minimal latency.

  • Exam pointers
  • Code generation = final phase of compilation, translates intermediate code to target machine/assembly/bytecode.
  • Optimization goal: improve speed/memory/power without changing program output.
  • Machine-independent techniques: constant folding, dead-code elimination, common subexpression elimination, loop-invariant code motion, strength reduction, loop unrolling, copy propagation.
  • Machine-dependent techniques: register allocation, instruction scheduling, peephole optimization.
  • Give the loop-invariant code motion example, it is the most commonly asked illustration.

Visual Modeling with UML

Unified Modeling Language (UML) is a standardized, general-purpose visual modeling language used to specify, visualize, construct, and document the artifacts of a software system, particularly one built using the object-oriented approach. UML was created by Grady Booch, Ivar Jacobson, and James Rumbaugh (the "three amigos") and is now maintained by the Object Management Group (OMG). UML diagrams are broadly divided into structural (static) diagrams, which show the system's static parts and their relationships, and behavioral (dynamic) diagrams, which show the system's behavior/interactions over time.

Key structural diagrams include: the Class Diagram (shows classes with their attributes, operations, and relationships such as association, aggregation, composition, inheritance/generalization), the Object Diagram (shows a snapshot of class instances at a point in time), the Component Diagram (shows how software components/modules are organized and interconnected), and the Deployment Diagram (shows the physical hardware nodes and how software components are deployed onto them). Key behavioral diagrams include: the Use Case Diagram (shows actors, the use cases/functions they perform, and relationships like include/extend, used to capture functional requirements at a high level), the Sequence Diagram (shows the time-ordered exchange of messages between objects for a particular scenario), the Activity Diagram (shows the workflow/business logic as a flowchart-like sequence of activities, decisions, and parallel flows), the State (Statechart) Diagram (shows the states an object can be in and the transitions/events that move it between states), and the Collaboration/Communication Diagram (similar to a sequence diagram but emphasizes the structural links between objects).

Example: for RBB's ATM system, a use-case diagram would show actors "Customer" and "Bank System" with use cases "Withdraw Cash", "Check Balance", "Transfer Funds"; a class diagram would show classes Account, Card, Transaction, and their relationships (e.g., an Account "has-a" list of Transaction objects, aggregation); a sequence diagram for "Withdraw Cash" would show the ordered messages between the ATM, the bank server, and the database (insert card, enter PIN, validate PIN, request amount, check balance, dispense cash, print receipt); and a state diagram for a Card object would show states like Inactive, Active, Blocked, and the events (three wrong PIN attempts) causing transitions between them. UML gives analysts, designers, and developers a common visual language, bridging the gap between business requirements and object-oriented code.

  • Exam pointers
  • UML = standardized visual modeling language for OO systems, by Booch/Jacobson/Rumbaugh, maintained by OMG.
  • Structural/static diagrams: Class, Object, Component, Deployment, (also Package).
  • Behavioral/dynamic diagrams: Use Case, Sequence, Activity, State (Statechart), Collaboration/Communication.
  • Use case diagram captures functional requirements (actors + use cases); class diagram captures static data structure; sequence/activity diagrams capture dynamic behavior.
  • Be ready to sketch/describe at least a use-case diagram and a class diagram for a simple banking scenario (ATM/account) as this is a very common practical sub-question.

Telephone Modems and Remote Network Access

A modem (modulator-demodulator) is a device that converts digital data from a computer into analog signals for transmission over an analog telephone line (modulation) and converts incoming analog signals back into digital data (demodulation), enabling computers to communicate over the ordinary telephone network. Modems were the classical means of remote network access, letting a remote user or branch office dial in to a central computer or the internet over a standard phone line.

Modem types include: dial-up modems (use the standard analog telephone line, historically up to 56 kbps under the V.90/V.92 standards); DSL (Digital Subscriber Line) modems (use the existing copper telephone line but a different frequency band than voice, allowing simultaneous voice and data, with speeds from a few Mbps to tens of Mbps, e.g., ADSL, VDSL); cable modems (use the coaxial cable TV network, offering higher speeds, tens to hundreds of Mbps); and fiber/broadband modems (ONT/ONU) (used with fiber-to-the-home, offering very high speeds, hundreds of Mbps to Gbps). Modem/line speed standards historically progressed from 300 bps, 1200 bps, 2400 bps, 9600 bps, 14.4 kbps, 28.8 kbps (V.34), to 56 kbps (V.90/V.92), and are now dominated by broadband DSL/cable/fiber standards measured in Mbps.

Remote network access today is achieved through Remote Access Service (RAS)/dial-up servers, Virtual Private Networks (VPN) (which create an encrypted, secure "tunnel" over a public network such as the internet so a remote user or branch appears to be on the private corporate network), and broadband/leased-line connections. Example: an RBB branch in a remote district may connect to the head-office data center via a leased line or a VPN over an ISP's broadband/fiber connection, replacing older dial-up modem links, to run core-banking transactions in real time with adequate speed and security; a field auditor working from home may use a VPN client over a home broadband connection to remotely access internal bank systems securely.

  • Exam pointers
  • Modem = converts digital to analog (modulation) and back (demodulation), enabling data transmission over analog lines.
  • Types: dial-up, DSL (ADSL/VDSL), cable modem, fiber ONT; speeds increase in that order.
  • Key standards to mention: V.34 (28.8 kbps), V.90/V.92 (56 kbps) for dial-up.
  • Remote access mechanisms: RAS/dial-up server, leased lines, broadband, and VPN (encrypted tunnel over a public network).
  • Banking relevance: branch-to-data-center connectivity, VPN for secure remote/work-from-home access to core banking systems.

Error Detection and Correction

During data transmission over any medium, noise, attenuation, or interference can corrupt bits, so error detection techniques are used to determine whether the received data matches what was sent, and error correction techniques are used to actually fix the corrupted data without retransmission. These techniques generally work by adding controlled redundant bits to the original data, computed from the data itself, so the receiver can check consistency.

Common error-detection methods include: parity check (adding a single bit to make the total number of 1-bits even (even parity) or odd (odd parity); detects single-bit errors but not all multi-bit errors); checksum (the sender sums up data segments and sends the sum, the receiver repeats the calculation and compares); and Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) (the data is treated as a polynomial, divided by a fixed generator polynomial, and the remainder is sent as the CRC value; the receiver performs the same division and checks the remainder is zero; CRC is highly effective at detecting burst errors and is widely used in Ethernet, ZIP files, and storage devices).

Error correction can be done via two strategies: Automatic Repeat reQuest (ARQ) (the receiver detects an error and requests retransmission of the corrupted frame, used in most reliable protocols like TCP; variants include stop-and-wait ARQ, go-back-N ARQ, and selective-repeat ARQ) and Forward Error Correction (FEC) (the sender adds enough redundant information for the receiver to detect and correct errors without retransmission, useful when retransmission is costly/impossible, e.g., satellite links, streaming). The classic FEC technique is the Hamming code, which places parity bits at specific bit positions (powers of 2) so that the pattern of failed parity checks (the "syndrome") directly points to the position of the single bit in error, allowing it to be corrected by simply flipping that bit. Example: in banking data transmission (e.g., an inter-branch fund transfer message), CRC is used at the data-link layer (Ethernet frame) to detect transmission errors, and if an error is found, ARQ (built into TCP) automatically requests retransmission of the affected packet, ensuring the transaction data arrives intact.

  • Exam pointers
  • Detection techniques: parity check (single-bit), checksum, CRC (polynomial division, strong burst-error detection).
  • Correction strategies: ARQ (retransmission-based: stop-and-wait, go-back-N, selective-repeat) and FEC (redundancy-based, no retransmission needed).
  • Hamming code: classic FEC single-bit-error-correcting code; know that parity bits are placed at power-of-2 positions and the syndrome locates the error bit.
  • CRC is used at the data-link layer (e.g., Ethernet); checksum is used at higher layers (e.g., IP/TCP/UDP headers).
  • Be ready for a numeric example: computing even/odd parity for a bit string, or a simple Hamming-code construction.

Data Compression Techniques

Data compression is the process of encoding information using fewer bits than the original representation, reducing storage space and transmission time/bandwidth. Compression techniques are broadly divided into lossless compression (the original data can be perfectly reconstructed from the compressed data, essential for text, program files, and financial data where every bit matters) and lossy compression (some data is permanently discarded to achieve a much higher compression ratio, acceptable for audio/image/video where perfect fidelity is not required).

Common lossless techniques include: Run-Length Encoding (RLE) (replaces consecutive repeated symbols with a single symbol and a count, e.g., "AAAAAA" becomes "A6"; effective for data with long repeated runs); Huffman coding (builds a binary tree assigning shorter bit codes to more frequently occurring symbols and longer codes to rare symbols, based on symbol frequency, achieving near-optimal compression for known symbol distributions); and Lempel-Ziv (LZ77/LZ78/LZW) family algorithms (build a dictionary of repeated substrings/patterns seen so far and replace repeated occurrences with references to the dictionary; used in ZIP, GIF, and the basis for many modern archive formats). Common lossy techniques include JPEG (image compression using discrete cosine transform and quantization to discard less perceptible visual detail) and MP3/MPEG (audio/video compression using psychoacoustic/perceptual models to discard imperceptible data).

Example: a bank archiving years of scanned loan documents (images/PDFs) would use lossless compression (e.g., ZIP/LZW-based or PNG for images) so that legal/financial documents remain byte-for-byte accurate, while a bank's promotional video content on its website might use lossy MPEG/MP4 compression to reduce file size and bandwidth without materially affecting viewing quality. Compression reduces network bandwidth consumption for data transmission between branches and reduces storage costs for backups and archives, both practically important for a large banking IT infrastructure.

  • Exam pointers
  • Two categories: lossless (perfect reconstruction, needed for text/financial/program data) vs lossy (some data lost, used for audio/image/video).
  • Lossless techniques: RLE (repeated runs), Huffman coding (frequency-based variable-length codes), LZ77/LZ78/LZW (dictionary-based, used in ZIP/GIF).
  • Lossy techniques: JPEG (images, DCT-based), MP3/MPEG (audio/video, perceptual coding).
  • Key trade-off: compression ratio vs fidelity/quality vs computational cost (compression/decompression time).
  • Banking must use lossless compression for financial records/documents; lossy is acceptable only for non-critical media content.

Communication Software

Communication software refers to programs that manage and enable the exchange of data between computers over a network, handling tasks such as establishing connections, managing protocols, formatting data for transmission, error control, and presenting a usable interface to the end user or application. It sits conceptually between the network hardware and the application, ensuring reliable, ordered, and secure data exchange.

Categories of communication software include: terminal emulation software (allows a PC to act as a terminal connecting to a remote host/mainframe, e.g., Telnet clients, SSH clients); network operating system components and protocol stacks (implement TCP/IP and related protocols within the OS to allow applications to use the network); groupware and collaboration software (email clients, video conferencing, instant messaging, shared document/workflow tools, e.g., Microsoft Outlook, Teams, Zoom); file transfer software (FTP/SFTP clients for moving files between systems); and middleware (software that connects and manages communication between distributed applications/systems, e.g., message queues, API gateways, ESBs used to connect a bank's core system with mobile banking, ATM switches, and payment gateways).

Example: RBB's IT infrastructure relies on communication software at several levels: SSH/Telnet clients for administrators to remotely manage servers, an inter-branch messaging/middleware layer (often built on protocols like ISO 8583 for financial messaging) to route ATM and POS transactions to the switch and core banking system, email/collaboration software (Outlook, Teams) for internal staff communication, and secure file-transfer software for exchanging batch files (e.g., clearing-house files) with Nepal Rastra Bank or other financial institutions. Good communication software must guarantee reliability (no lost/duplicated messages), security (encryption, authentication), and interoperability between heterogeneous systems.

  • Exam pointers
  • Communication software = layer managing protocol handling, connection setup, error control, and interface between network and application/user.
  • Categories: terminal emulation (Telnet/SSH), protocol stacks/NOS components, groupware/collaboration (email, video conferencing), file transfer (FTP/SFTP), middleware (message queues, ESB, API gateways).
  • In banking, financial messaging standards like ISO 8583 (card/ATM transactions) and SWIFT (international transfers) are important examples of specialized communication software/protocols.
  • Key required properties: reliability, security, and interoperability across heterogeneous systems.
  • Middleware is a good term to bring up when linking core banking with ATMs, mobile banking, and third-party payment switches.

Local Area Network (LAN) and Network Topologies

A Local Area Network (LAN) is a network of computers and devices confined to a relatively small geographic area, such as a single office, building, or campus, characterized by high data transfer rates, low latency, and typically owned/managed by a single organization. LANs allow shared access to files, printers, applications, and internet connections among connected devices, and form the foundation on which larger WANs are built by interconnecting multiple LANs.

A network topology is the arrangement/layout of nodes and links in a network. In a bus topology, all devices connect to a single shared backbone cable (terminated at both ends); it is simple and cheap but a break in the cable disables the whole network, and performance degrades as more devices are added (collisions on the shared medium). In a star topology, every device connects individually to a central device (hub or switch); it is the most common LAN topology today because a single cable/node failure does not affect other devices, and it is easy to add/remove devices, though the central device is a single point of failure. In a ring topology, each device connects to exactly two neighboring devices, forming a closed loop, and data travels around the ring in one direction (or both, in a dual ring); Token Ring (IBM's implementation, IEEE 802.5) uses a special bit pattern called a token that circulates around the ring, and a device may only transmit data when it holds the free token, which avoids collisions but adds token-management overhead and complexity when a node fails. In a mesh topology, devices are interconnected with many redundant point-to-point links (full mesh: every node connects to every other node; partial mesh: only some nodes are fully interconnected), giving very high fault tolerance and redundancy at high cabling cost, typically used for critical backbone/WAN links rather than ordinary LANs.

ARCnet (Attached Resource Computer Network) is an older LAN technology that used a token-passing access method (similar in principle to Token Ring) over a star or bus-wired physical layout, running at modest speeds (2.5 Mbps originally); it was popular in the 1980s for its low cost and reliability but has been almost entirely superseded by Ethernet. Example: a modern RBB branch LAN is wired in a star topology using Ethernet switches (each PC/printer/server connects to a central switch via twisted-pair cable), because this offers easy fault isolation (one bad cable does not bring down the branch) and simple expansion as new counters/devices are added, whereas older Token Ring/ARCnet networks are essentially obsolete in modern deployments, replaced by switched Ethernet star networks.

  • Exam pointers
  • LAN: small geographic area, high speed, single-organization ownership; building block of larger networks.
  • Topologies: bus (shared backbone, single point of failure = cable), star (central hub/switch, most common today), ring (closed loop, Token Ring/IEEE 802.5 uses token-passing), mesh (redundant point-to-point links, high fault tolerance, high cost).
  • Token Ring: token-passing access control avoids collisions; ARCnet: older token-passing LAN technology, largely obsolete now.
  • Compare topologies on: cost, fault tolerance, scalability, ease of troubleshooting, cabling requirement.
  • Modern default: switched star-topology Ethernet LANs dominate real-world deployments including bank branches.

Transmission Media of LAN

Transmission media are the physical or wireless paths through which data signals travel between devices in a network. They are broadly classified as guided (wired) media, where signals travel along a solid physical path, and unguided (wireless) media, where signals propagate through free space/air.

Guided media used in LANs include: Twisted Pair cable (two insulated copper wires twisted together to reduce electromagnetic interference; available as Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP, common Cat5e/Cat6 Ethernet cable, low cost, up to 1 Gbps or more over short distances) and Shielded Twisted Pair (STP, added shielding for noisy environments)); Coaxial cable (a central copper conductor surrounded by insulation, a metallic shield, and an outer jacket, offering better noise immunity and higher bandwidth than twisted pair, historically used in bus-topology Ethernet and cable TV/cable internet); and Fiber Optic cable (transmits data as pulses of light through a glass/plastic core, offering very high bandwidth, very long distance without signal loss, and complete immunity to electromagnetic interference, at higher installation cost, used for LAN backbones and inter-building/inter-branch links).

Unguided (wireless) media include radio waves (used by Wi-Fi/WLAN, IEEE 802.11 standards, omnidirectional, penetrates walls), microwave (line-of-sight, used for point-to-point links between buildings/towers, higher bandwidth than radio), and infrared (very short range, line-of-sight, used for remote controls and some short-range device links, blocked by walls). Example: a typical RBB branch LAN uses UTP Cat6 cable to connect desktop PCs and printers to switches (cost-effective, adequate speed for office use), a fiber-optic backbone or link to connect the branch's core switch to the head-office data center or ISP point of presence (for high bandwidth over longer distance), and Wi-Fi access points for staff laptops/tablets or guest access, choosing the medium based on the required distance, bandwidth, cost, and immunity to interference for each segment of the network.

  • Exam pointers
  • Guided media: twisted pair (UTP/STP, cheapest, common in LANs), coaxial (better noise immunity, moderate cost), fiber optic (highest bandwidth/distance/immunity, highest cost).
  • Unguided media: radio (Wi-Fi, omnidirectional), microwave (line-of-sight, point-to-point), infrared (very short range, line-of-sight).
  • Compare on: bandwidth, distance, cost, noise immunity, installation complexity, this comparison table is a common exam ask.
  • Fiber optic is preferred for backbones/inter-branch links; twisted pair (UTP) for desktop/end-device connections.
  • Mention relevant standards: IEEE 802.3 (Ethernet, wired), IEEE 802.11 (Wi-Fi, wireless).

Network Operating System (NOS)

A Network Operating System (NOS) is system software that manages and coordinates the hardware and software resources of a network, allowing multiple computers to communicate, share files/printers/applications, and providing services such as user authentication, access control, and resource management across the network, in addition to the functions of a normal single-computer operating system.

NOS architectures are broadly of two types: peer-to-peer NOS (every computer on the network can act as both client and server, sharing its own resources directly with others, e.g., Windows workgroup networking; simple and cheap but harder to secure/manage centrally at scale) and client-server NOS (one or more dedicated, powerful servers run the NOS and provide centralized services like authentication, file storage, printing, and application hosting to many client computers, e.g., Windows Server with Active Directory, Novell NetWare, Linux-based servers; offers centralized security, administration, and scalability, at higher hardware/licensing cost). Core NOS functions include user account and group management, centralized authentication and authorization (e.g., via Active Directory/LDAP), file and print sharing services, network protocol support (TCP/IP stack), remote access services, and network monitoring/management tools.

Example: RBB's branch and head-office network would typically run a client-server NOS such as Windows Server (with Active Directory for centralized user login/authentication across all branches) or a Linux-based server environment, so that a teller logging in at any branch is authenticated centrally, access to shared drives/applications is controlled by group policy, and IT administrators can centrally manage security patches, user permissions, and audit logs across the entire bank network, a level of centralized control and security that a simple peer-to-peer NOS could not provide at banking scale.

  • Exam pointers
  • NOS = OS managing network-wide resource sharing, authentication, and access control, in addition to normal OS functions.
  • Two architectures: peer-to-peer (simple, decentralized, good for small networks) vs client-server (centralized, secure, scalable, needed for large organizations).
  • Examples: Windows Server/Active Directory, Novell NetWare, Linux/UNIX server distributions.
  • Core services: authentication/authorization, file/print sharing, protocol support, remote access, network management/monitoring.
  • Banking always requires client-server NOS with centralized authentication (Active Directory/LDAP) for security and auditability across branches.

Wide Area Networks: Bridges, Routers and Gateways

A Wide Area Network (WAN) connects LANs and devices across large geographic distances (cities, countries, continents), typically using telecommunication carrier links (leased lines, MPLS, satellite, fiber backbone) rather than privately owned cabling throughout. Because a WAN interconnects many different LANs and networks, specialized internetworking devices are needed to connect and manage traffic between them, operating at different layers of the OSI model.

A bridge operates at the data-link layer (Layer 2) and connects two LAN segments, forwarding frames between them based on MAC addresses while filtering out unnecessary traffic, effectively extending a LAN and reducing collision-domain size, but it does not understand IP addressing or routing between different networks. A router operates at the network layer (Layer 3) and connects two or more different networks (which may use different addressing/technologies), forwarding packets based on destination IP address using routing tables and routing protocols (e.g., RIP, OSPF, BGP) to determine the best path; routers are the essential devices that interconnect LANs into WANs and connect organizations to the internet. A gateway is a more general device/software that connects two networks using different protocols or architectures altogether, performing protocol translation so that dissimilar systems can communicate (e.g., an email gateway translating between different mail protocols, or a payment gateway translating between a bank's internal transaction format and an external payment network's format); a gateway can operate at any OSI layer, up to the application layer, depending on what translation is needed.

Example: RBB's head-office network uses routers at each branch to connect the branch LAN to the WAN link (leased line/MPLS/internet VPN) back to the data center, using routing protocols to find the best path if multiple links exist; a bridge (or more commonly today, a Layer-2 switch performing bridging functions) might be used within a branch to segment a large LAN into smaller collision domains without needing IP-level routing; and a payment gateway is used to translate and securely relay transactions between RBB's core banking system and external payment networks (card networks, national payment switch, other banks), since these systems may use different protocols/message formats (e.g., ISO 8583) that require translation.

  • Exam pointers
  • Bridge: Layer 2 (data-link), connects similar LAN segments, filters by MAC address.
  • Router: Layer 3 (network), connects different networks/WAN links, forwards by IP address, uses routing tables/protocols (RIP, OSPF, BGP).
  • Gateway: any layer up to application, connects networks with different protocols/architectures, performs protocol translation.
  • Progression of scope: bridge (extends a LAN) less than router (connects networks/builds WAN) less than gateway (connects dissimilar systems/protocols).
  • Banking example: payment gateway for protocol translation between core banking and card/payment networks is a strong applied example.

TCP/IP Protocol Suite

TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol) is the foundational suite of communication protocols used by the internet and virtually all modern networks to enable reliable, standardized data exchange between heterogeneous devices. Unlike the seven-layer OSI reference model, the TCP/IP model is organized into four (sometimes described as five) layers: Network Interface/Link layer (handles physical addressing and transmission over the actual network medium, e.g., Ethernet, Wi-Fi), Internet layer (handles logical addressing and routing of packets across networks, primarily the IP protocol, plus supporting protocols like ICMP and ARP), Transport layer (handles end-to-end communication between applications, primarily TCP and UDP), and Application layer (combines the OSI session, presentation, and application layers, hosting protocols like HTTP, FTP, SMTP, DNS, Telnet).

IP is a connectionless, best-effort protocol responsible for addressing and routing packets (datagrams) from source to destination across interconnected networks, without guaranteeing delivery, order, or error checking on its own. TCP sits above IP and is a connection-oriented, reliable transport protocol, it establishes a connection (via a three-way handshake: SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK), breaks data into segments, numbers them for correct reordering, retransmits lost segments, and performs flow/congestion control, making it suitable for applications needing guaranteed, ordered delivery (web browsing, file transfer, email, banking transactions). UDP (User Datagram Protocol) is a simpler, connectionless transport protocol with no handshake, ordering, or retransmission guarantees, offering lower overhead and latency, suitable for applications tolerant of some data loss (video streaming, VoIP, DNS queries).

Example: when a customer performs an online fund transfer, the banking application uses TCP (via HTTPS) to guarantee that every byte of the transaction request reaches the bank's server exactly once, in order, with automatic retransmission of any lost segment, since losing or duplicating part of a financial transaction is unacceptable; by contrast, a customer-support video call from the same bank might use UDP-based protocols since occasional dropped frames are more tolerable than the added delay of TCP's reliability mechanisms. TCP/IP's layered, modular design allows different physical networks (Ethernet, Wi-Fi, fiber, satellite) and different applications to interoperate seamlessly, which is why it is the universal standard underlying the internet and modern banking networks.

  • Exam pointers
  • TCP/IP model layers: Network Interface/Link, Internet (IP), Transport (TCP/UDP), Application; four layers versus OSI's seven.
  • IP: connectionless, best-effort, handles addressing/routing (Internet layer).
  • TCP: connection-oriented, reliable, ordered, handshake-based (three-way handshake), flow/congestion control, used where reliability matters.
  • UDP: connectionless, unreliable, low overhead, used where speed matters more than guaranteed delivery.
  • Always be ready to map TCP/IP layers against OSI layers, a very common comparison question.

Packet Switching versus Circuit Switching

Switching techniques determine how data is transferred through the intermediate nodes of a network from source to destination. In circuit switching, a dedicated, exclusive physical communication path (circuit) is established between sender and receiver before data transfer begins, and this path remains reserved for the entire duration of the session (the classic example is the traditional analog telephone call); the path is released only when the session ends. In packet switching, data is broken into small, discrete units called packets, each carrying a header with addressing/sequencing information, and each packet is routed independently through the network, potentially via different paths, then reassembled in the correct order at the destination; the internet and virtually all modern data networks use packet switching.

Circuit switching guarantees a fixed, dedicated bandwidth and consistent, predictable delay (good for real-time voice), but wastes bandwidth when the circuit is idle (e.g., silence during a phone call still reserves the full circuit) and cannot be shared by other users during the connection. Packet switching allows many users to share the same network links efficiently (statistical multiplexing), is more resilient (if one path/node fails, packets can be rerouted), and is naturally suited to bursty data traffic (like web browsing or file transfer), but individual packets may experience variable delay (jitter), may arrive out of order, and require reassembly and error/flow control (typically handled by TCP) at the destination. Packet switching itself has two sub-types: datagram (connectionless) packet switching (each packet is routed independently, no fixed path, used by IP) and virtual-circuit packet switching (a logical path is set up before data transfer, all packets of a session follow this same logical path, though it is not physically dedicated, used by technologies like ATM and MPLS, and conceptually by TCP's connection-oriented session).

Example: a traditional voice call over the old PSTN (Public Switched Telephone Network) uses circuit switching, a dedicated 64 kbps circuit is reserved end-to-end for the call's duration; RBB's data communication, whether internet banking, inter-branch file transfer, or VoIP over the corporate network, uses packet switching (IP-based), allowing the same WAN links to be shared efficiently across many simultaneous transactions and services from different branches, which is far more cost-effective and scalable than dedicating a separate circuit per branch or per transaction.

  • Exam pointers
  • Circuit switching: dedicated path reserved for entire session, fixed bandwidth, predictable delay, bandwidth wasted when idle; example: traditional PSTN telephone call.
  • Packet switching: data split into packets, routed independently/shared links, efficient bandwidth use (statistical multiplexing), variable delay/jitter, needs reassembly; example: the internet.
  • Packet switching sub-types: datagram/connectionless (IP) vs virtual-circuit (ATM, MPLS, conceptually TCP sessions).
  • Comparison table is a common exam ask: setup time, bandwidth utilization, delay predictability, fault tolerance, cost efficiency.
  • Modern networks (including banking WANs) are packet-switched (IP/MPLS-based) for cost and scalability reasons.

Email System

An electronic mail (email) system allows users to compose, send, receive, and store messages electronically across a network. It works on a client-server model involving a Mail User Agent (MUA) (the email client/application the user interacts with, e.g., Outlook, Gmail web client, Thunderbird), a Mail Transfer Agent (MTA) (server software that routes/relays the message toward its destination across the internet, e.g., Postfix, Exchange Server, Sendmail), and a Mail Delivery Agent (MDA) (delivers the incoming message into the recipient's mailbox on the destination mail server).

Three main protocols govern email exchange: SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol) is used to send/relay outgoing mail from the client to the server and between mail servers (push protocol, typically port 25/587); POP3 (Post Office Protocol version 3) is used by a client to download/retrieve messages from the mail server to the local device, typically removing them from the server after download (simple, but not well suited to accessing mail from multiple devices); and IMAP (Internet Message Access Protocol) is used by a client to access and manage messages that remain stored on the server, synchronizing read/unread status, folders, and messages across multiple devices (better suited to modern multi-device use). Web-based email (webmail) uses HTTP/HTTPS for the client-to-server interaction instead of POP3/IMAP directly.

Example: when an RBB employee sends an internal email, their MUA (Outlook) uses SMTP to submit the message to the organization's mail server (MTA), which relays it (using SMTP between servers, possibly via DNS MX-record lookup to find the recipient's mail server) to the recipient's mail server, where it is stored by the MDA; the recipient's Outlook client then uses IMAP to synchronize and read the message from any of their devices (desktop, mobile) while keeping it on the server. Email security is reinforced through TLS encryption of the SMTP/IMAP connections, spam/malware filtering, and organizational policies restricting external attachments, which is important since email remains a common vector for phishing attacks against bank staff and customers.

  • Exam pointers
  • Components: MUA (client), MTA (server relay), MDA (final delivery to mailbox).
  • SMTP: sending/relaying mail (push); POP3: download-and-delete retrieval (single device); IMAP: server-synchronized retrieval (multi-device).
  • DNS MX records direct SMTP servers to the correct destination mail server for a domain.
  • Security add-ons: TLS/SSL encryption of mail protocols, spam filters, anti-phishing measures, are important banking-context talking points.
  • Be ready to draw/describe the sender MUA to sender MTA to (relay) to recipient MTA to recipient MDA to recipient MUA flow.

Data Encryption

Data encryption is the process of converting readable data (plaintext) into an unreadable, scrambled form (ciphertext) using a mathematical algorithm (cipher) and a key, so that only someone possessing the correct key can reverse the process (decryption) and recover the original data. Encryption is the primary technical mechanism for achieving confidentiality (and, combined with other techniques, integrity and authentication) in the CIA triad.

There are two main categories of encryption. Symmetric-key encryption uses the same single key for both encryption and decryption; it is fast and efficient for large volumes of data, but the key must be securely shared between sender and receiver beforehand (key-distribution problem); common algorithms include DES (Data Encryption Standard, 56-bit key, now considered weak/obsolete), Triple DES (3DES), and AES (Advanced Encryption Standard), the current widely adopted standard (128/192/256-bit keys). Asymmetric-key encryption (public-key cryptography) uses a mathematically related key pair, a public key (freely shared, used to encrypt or to verify a signature) and a private key (kept secret, used to decrypt or to create a signature); anything encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the corresponding private key, solving the key-distribution problem at the cost of being computationally slower; the most common algorithm is RSA, along with elliptic-curve cryptography (ECC).

In practice, most secure systems use a hybrid approach: asymmetric encryption is used to securely exchange a temporary symmetric session key (solving the distribution problem), and then fast symmetric encryption is used for the bulk of the actual data (for speed); this is exactly how SSL/TLS (the protocol behind HTTPS) works. Related concepts include digital signatures (a sender encrypts a hash of a message with their private key; anyone can verify it using the sender's public key, proving authenticity and non-repudiation) and digital certificates/Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) (a trusted Certificate Authority (CA) issues certificates binding a public key to a verified identity, e.g., a bank's website certificate). Example: when a customer logs into RBB's internet-banking portal over HTTPS, the browser and server perform a TLS handshake using asymmetric encryption (RSA/ECC, validated via the bank's digital certificate from a trusted CA) to agree on a shared symmetric session key, after which all transaction data is encrypted with fast symmetric encryption (AES) for the rest of the session, giving both strong security and good performance, while digital signatures on transaction records provide non-repudiation.

  • Exam pointers
  • Symmetric encryption: one shared key, fast, key-distribution problem; examples: DES (weak/obsolete), 3DES, AES (current standard).
  • Asymmetric encryption: public/private key pair, solves key distribution, slower; example: RSA, ECC.
  • Hybrid approach (used by TLS/HTTPS/SSL): asymmetric to exchange a session key, symmetric (AES) for bulk data, know this is the real-world standard pattern.
  • Digital signature: private key encrypts a hash for authenticity/non-repudiation, verified with the public key; different purpose from confidentiality encryption.
  • PKI/digital certificates/Certificate Authority: binds public keys to verified identities, foundation of trust for HTTPS/e-banking security.

General Principles of Computer Organization and Design

Computer organization refers to the operational structure that realizes a given architecture: the register set implementation, control signal generation, bus structure, memory technology, and the interconnection of functional units. Computer architecture, by contrast, is the set of attributes visible to the assembly-language programmer or compiler writer — the instruction set, addressing modes, data types, and register model. Two machines can share an architecture (for example, the x86 instruction set) while differing completely in organization (pipeline depth, cache hierarchy, number of execution units), which is why successive processor generations run the same binaries yet perform very differently.

A computer system is built from five classical functional units: the input unit, output unit, memory unit, arithmetic-logic unit (ALU), and control unit, all tied together by an interconnection structure (buses or crossbars). Design proceeds top-down: the architecture fixes the programmer's contract, then organization decisions (single-cycle vs. pipelined datapath, hardwired vs. microprogrammed control, cache levels) implement that contract subject to cost, power, and performance constraints. Good design applies hierarchy (breaking the system into levels — digital logic, microarchitecture, ISA, system software, application), modularity (independent, replaceable functional blocks), and regularity (reusing identical building blocks, such as one-bit ALU slices replicated to form an n-bit ALU).

Design goals are traded off against each other: raw performance (clock rate, instructions per cycle), cost (silicon area, memory cost), power/energy (critical in mobile and embedded design), and reliability/availability (error detection and correction, redundancy). The famous performance equation, CPU time = Instruction count × CPI × Clock cycle time, captures how architecture (instruction count, achievable CPI) and organization/technology (clock cycle time) jointly determine speed, and is the standard tool for reasoning about design trade-offs.

  • Exam pointers
  • Architecture = programmer-visible (ISA); Organization = implementation of that ISA (datapath, control, memory tech). Give an x86 example.
  • Five classical functional units: input, output, memory, ALU, control unit, linked by buses.
  • Design principles: hierarchy, modularity, regularity, and the performance equation CPU time = IC × CPI × Cycle time.
  • Trade-offs: performance vs. cost vs. power vs. reliability.

Von Neumann Machine Architecture

The von Neumann architecture, proposed by John von Neumann in 1945 (EDVAC report), is the "stored-program" model in which both instructions and data reside in the same read-write memory, addressed uniformly, and instructions are fetched and executed sequentially unless a branch alters the flow. This is the foundation of virtually every general-purpose computer built since. The model has four main components: the Central Processing Unit (split into the Control Unit and the Arithmetic Logic Unit), the Memory Unit, the Input/Output devices, and a single System Bus connecting them, carrying address, data, and control signals.

Because instructions and data share one memory and one bus, the CPU cannot simultaneously fetch an instruction and read/write a data operand — this structural limitation is called the von Neumann bottleneck. The CPU operates on a repeating fetch-decode-execute cycle: fetch the instruction addressed by the Program Counter (PC) into the Instruction Register (IR), increment the PC, decode the opcode, fetch any operands, execute the operation in the ALU, and store results back to a register or memory.

The main contrasting model is the Harvard architecture, which uses physically separate memories (and buses) for instructions and data, allowing simultaneous instruction fetch and data access — this removes the bottleneck at the cost of extra hardware, and is common today inside CPU caches (split I-cache and D-cache) and in DSP/microcontroller cores, even though the outward-facing memory is still unified (a "modified Harvard" design).

  • Exam pointers
  • Core idea: stored-program concept — instructions and data share one memory, one address space, one bus.
  • Four components: CU + ALU (CPU), Memory, I/O, System bus.
  • Fetch-decode-execute cycle: PC → IR → decode → operand fetch → execute → store.
  • Von Neumann bottleneck: one bus serializes instruction and data traffic; Harvard architecture (separate I/D memory and bus) is the classic remedy, used today in L1 caches.

CPU Design

The Central Processing Unit consists of a register set, the ALU, and the control unit, interconnected by an internal bus. Registers are divided into user-visible general-purpose registers (for holding operands and intermediate results) and control/status registers that are essential to instruction execution: the Program Counter (PC, holds the address of the next instruction), Instruction Register (IR, holds the instruction currently being decoded), Memory Address Register (MAR, holds the address to be accessed in memory), Memory Buffer/Data Register (MBR/MDR, holds the value read from or to be written to memory), Stack Pointer (SP), and the Program Status Word / Flags register (records condition codes such as Zero, Carry, Sign, Overflow).

The control unit sequences the micro-operations needed to execute each instruction. Two implementation styles exist: hardwired control, where combinational logic (state machine, PLA) directly generates control signals — fast but rigid and hard to modify; and microprogrammed control, where each machine instruction is interpreted by a sequence of micro-instructions stored in a control memory (control store) — slower but flexible, and easy to extend or debug (used historically in CISC machines such as the IBM System/360 and early x86).

The instruction cycle generalizes fetch-decode-execute into: Fetch cycle (get opcode), Indirect cycle (resolve indirect addressing if present), Execute cycle (perform the operation), and Interrupt cycle (check for and service a pending interrupt before fetching the next instruction). CPU organization also differs by how many "visible" registers/operands an instruction can name: a single-accumulator machine implicitly uses one accumulator register, while a general register-file machine (e.g., a RISC processor with 32 registers) makes many registers explicitly addressable, reducing memory traffic.

  • Exam pointers
  • Know the special-purpose registers by name and role: PC, IR, MAR, MBR/MDR, SP, PSW/flags.
  • Hardwired control: fast, fixed logic. Microprogrammed control: flexible, stored micro-instructions in control store — classic CISC technique.
  • Extended instruction cycle: Fetch → Indirect → Execute → Interrupt.
  • Accumulator-based vs. register-file (general register) organization — trade-off between instruction length and memory traffic.

ALU Design

The Arithmetic Logic Unit is a combinational (with some sequential elements for shift/multiply) circuit that performs arithmetic operations (add, subtract, increment, compare) and logic operations (AND, OR, XOR, NOT) on data supplied from registers, producing a result and a set of condition-code flags: Zero (Z), Carry (C), Sign/Negative (N), and Overflow (V). A control input (the function-select lines) chooses which operation the ALU performs on a given cycle; internally this is implemented with multiplexers that route the chosen operation's output to the ALU's output bus.

The arithmetic core is built from binary adders. A ripple-carry adder chains n full adders, where each stage's carry-out feeds the next stage's carry-in; it is simple but slow because the carry must propagate through all n stages (O(n) delay). A carry-lookahead adder computes carry signals in advance using generate (Gi = Ai·Bi) and propagate (Pi = Ai⊕Bi) terms, so carries for all bit positions are available in parallel — much faster (roughly O(log n) delay) at the cost of more gates. Subtraction is implemented by adding the two's complement of the subtrahend (invert bits, add 1), so one adder circuit serves both addition and subtraction, with a control line selecting complement-and-add-1 for subtract.

A wide ALU (e.g., 32-bit) is typically built by cascading identical 1-bit ALU slices, each capable of add, AND, OR, and NOT for that bit position, with the carry chain connecting slices — an example of the regularity design principle. The logic unit performs Boolean operations bit by bit, useful for masking, setting/clearing bits, and comparisons; a barrel shifter (a network of multiplexers) performs arbitrary-distance shifts/rotates in a single cycle, which is much faster than shifting one bit at a time.

  • Exam pointers
  • ALU = arithmetic operations + logic operations + shifter, selected by function-select control lines; produces Z, C, N, V flags.
  • Ripple-carry adder: simple, O(n) delay. Carry-lookahead adder: computes generate/propagate terms, O(log n) delay, faster but more hardware.
  • Subtraction via two's complement addition — reuses the adder.
  • n-bit ALU built by cascading 1-bit ALU slices (regularity); barrel shifter for single-cycle shifts.

Instruction Set and Instruction Format

An instruction is encoded into fields: the opcode (operation code, specifying what to do), one or more operand/address fields (specifying where the data is), and often a mode field (specifying how to interpret the address field, i.e., the addressing mode). Instruction set design must decide instruction length (fixed, as in RISC, versus variable, as in CISC), the number of explicit operands, and how opcode and operand fields divide the available bits.

Instructions are classified by the number of addresses (operands) they name. A three-address instruction, e.g. ADD R1, R2, R3 (R1 ← R2 + R3), names two source operands and a distinct destination — expressive but longer encoding. A two-address instruction, e.g. ADD R1, R2 (R1 ← R1 + R2), overwrites one source with the result, saving bits at the cost of destroying an operand. A one-address instruction, e.g. ADD M (AC ← AC + M), implicitly uses the accumulator (AC) as both an implied source and destination — compact, common in early machines. A zero-address instruction, e.g. ADD on a stack machine, takes both operands from the top of an operand stack and pushes back the result — no address field needed at all, at the cost of extra push/pop traffic.

Fixed-length instruction formats (typical of RISC, e.g., all MIPS instructions are 32 bits) simplify fetch and decode and enable pipelining, since the next instruction's address and the field boundaries are always predictable. Variable-length formats (typical of CISC, e.g., x86 instructions range from 1 to 15 bytes) improve code density — common operations get short encodings — but complicate instruction fetch, decode, and pipelining because instruction boundaries are not known until partially decoded.

  • Exam pointers
  • Instruction fields: opcode, address/operand field(s), mode field.
  • Classification by operand count with examples: three-address, two-address, one-address (accumulator), zero-address (stack).
  • Fixed-length format → simpler pipelining (RISC); variable-length → better code density but harder decode (CISC).
  • Trade-off: number of addresses vs. instruction length vs. number of memory references needed to complete an operation.

Main Memory

Main memory (primary memory) is the random-access store directly addressable by the CPU, holding the currently executing program's instructions and data. It is organized as an array of addressable words (or bytes), accessed through the Memory Address Register (MAR, carries the address) and Memory Buffer/Data Register (MBR/MDR, carries the data being read or written), with Read and Write control lines to indicate the direction of transfer.

Main memory is built from semiconductor RAM — predominantly DRAM for capacity/cost reasons (see the Memory Types topic). To increase effective bandwidth, memory is often organized in interleaved banks: consecutive addresses are spread across multiple independent banks so that several words can be accessed in an overlapped pipeline fashion, hiding the access latency of any single bank. Memory is the middle tier of the memory hierarchy — larger and slower than cache/registers, but smaller and faster than secondary (disk/SSD) storage.

Because main memory is far slower than the CPU, systems universally interpose cache memory (hardware-managed, transparent to software) between the CPU and main memory, and use virtual memory (software/hardware-managed, via the operating system and MMU) to give programs the illusion of more memory than physically exists — both are covered as separate topics below, and together with main memory they form the classical memory hierarchy: registers → cache → main memory → secondary storage, trading capacity for speed at each level.

  • Exam pointers
  • MAR/MBR (MDR) are the interface registers between CPU and memory; Read/Write control lines direct the transfer.
  • Memory interleaving spreads consecutive addresses across banks to allow overlapped/pipelined access and higher bandwidth.
  • Main memory sits in the middle of the memory hierarchy: registers (fastest, smallest) → cache → main memory → secondary storage (slowest, largest).
  • Principle governing the whole hierarchy: exploit locality of reference to keep frequently used data close to the CPU.

Virtual Memory

Virtual memory is a memory-management technique that gives each process the illusion of a large, contiguous, private address space (the logical/virtual address space), independent of how much physical RAM actually exists and independent of what other processes are doing. The Memory Management Unit (MMU), working with operating-system-maintained page tables, translates virtual addresses generated by the CPU into physical addresses in real memory — this translation happens on every memory reference, so it is accelerated by a Translation Lookaside Buffer (TLB), a small hardware cache of recent virtual-to-physical translations.

The dominant implementation is paging: both virtual and physical address spaces are divided into fixed-size blocks (pages and frames respectively, commonly 4 KB). A page table maps each virtual page number to a physical frame number, plus control bits (valid/invalid, dirty, referenced, protection). Not all pages need be resident in physical memory at once — this is demand paging: a page is loaded from disk only when first referenced. If a process accesses a page whose valid bit is off, the MMU raises a page fault; the OS then locates the page on backing store, chooses a physical frame to place it in (evicting another page if memory is full), updates the page table, and restarts the faulting instruction.

When physical memory is full, the OS must choose a victim page to evict, governed by a page-replacement algorithm: FIFO (evict the oldest-loaded page — simple but can suffer Belady's anomaly), Optimal (evict the page not needed for the longest time in the future — ideal but not realizable, used only as a theoretical benchmark), LRU (Least Recently Used — evict the page not referenced for the longest time, approximates Optimal well and is widely implemented via reference bits/counters), and the Clock (second-chance) algorithm, a practical hardware-cheap approximation of LRU using a reference bit and a circulating pointer.

Virtual memory also provides protection (each process's page table restricts it to its own pages, preventing one process from reading or corrupting another's memory) and simplifies programming (no manual overlay management as was required on early machines). Segmentation is an alternative/complementary scheme that divides the address space into variable-length logical segments (code, stack, heap), matching a programmer's view of a program better than fixed-size pages but suffering external fragmentation; many systems use paged segmentation, combining both.

  • Exam pointers
  • Key terms: logical/virtual address, physical address, MMU, page table, TLB, page fault, demand paging.
  • Page-replacement algorithms: FIFO, Optimal, LRU, Clock/second-chance — know how each decides the victim and their relative merits.
  • Belady's anomaly: with FIFO, more frames can sometimes cause more page faults.
  • Benefits: larger-than-RAM address space, process isolation/protection, simplified programming; cost: page-fault latency, TLB miss overhead.
  • Paging (fixed-size, no external fragmentation, internal fragmentation possible) vs. segmentation (variable-size, matches logical program structure, external fragmentation possible).

Cache Memory

Cache memory is a small, fast memory placed between the CPU and main memory to reduce the average time to access data, exploiting the principle of locality: temporal locality (recently accessed data/instructions are likely to be accessed again soon) and spatial locality (data near a recently accessed address is likely to be accessed soon too). Because of this, caches are organized in fixed-size blocks/lines (e.g., 64 bytes) — an entire block is brought in on a miss, not just the single requested word, exploiting spatial locality.

Cache performance is characterized by hit rate/miss rate and Average Memory Access Time (AMAT) = Hit time + Miss rate × Miss penalty. Three mapping techniques determine where a main-memory block can be placed in the cache: direct mapping assigns each memory block to exactly one cache line, computed as (Block address) mod (Number of cache lines) — simple and fast, but suffers high conflict misses when two frequently used blocks map to the same line; fully associative mapping allows a block to go into any cache line, eliminating conflict misses but requiring a parallel (associative) search of every line's tag on every access — expensive in hardware; set-associative mapping (the common real-world compromise) divides the cache into sets of n lines each (n-way set-associative), a block maps to exactly one set — computed as (Block address) mod (Number of sets) — but can occupy any line within that set, balancing hardware cost against conflict misses.

Within a set/cache, when all lines are occupied and a new block must be brought in, a replacement policy chooses the victim — commonly LRU (evict the least recently used line) or random. Write policy governs how stores are handled: write-through updates cache and main memory simultaneously (simpler, keeps memory always consistent, but generates more memory traffic), while write-back updates only the cache and marks the line "dirty," writing it to memory only when the line is evicted (less memory traffic, but memory can be briefly stale and recovery/coherence logic is more complex). Related write-miss policies are write-allocate (bring the block into cache on a write miss) and no-write-allocate (write directly to memory, bypassing the cache, on a miss).

  • Exam pointers
  • Locality of reference (temporal + spatial) is the entire justification for caching — always state this first.
  • AMAT = Hit time + Miss rate × Miss penalty — a common numerical question.
  • Three mapping techniques: direct (1 line, mod-based, fast, more conflicts), fully associative (any line, parallel tag search, no conflict misses, costly), set-associative (n-way compromise) — know the placement formulas.
  • Write policies: write-through (memory always current, more traffic) vs. write-back (dirty bit, less traffic, complex on eviction).
  • Multi-level caches (L1/L2/L3) apply the same principles at increasing size and latency.

RISC vs. CISC

Reduced Instruction Set Computer (RISC) and Complex Instruction Set Computer (CISC) represent two philosophies of instruction-set and microarchitecture design. CISC evolved when memory was slow and expensive and compilers were primitive: the goal was to shrink the "semantic gap" between high-level languages and machine code by providing rich, powerful instructions (some doing multi-step work, e.g., a single instruction that loads a value, adds a memory operand, and stores the result), reducing program size and the number of instructions the compiler had to generate. Examples of CISC families include Intel x86 and the IBM System/360.

RISC emerged in the 1980s (Patterson and Hennessy's research, IBM 801) from the observation that a small set of simple, uniform, fixed-length instructions — each executable in (close to) one clock cycle — combined with an aggressive optimizing compiler and heavy use of pipelining, gave better real performance than complex instructions that took many cycles and complicated pipeline design. RISC designs favor a load-store architecture: only explicit LOAD and STORE instructions touch memory; all arithmetic/logic instructions operate strictly on registers, which keeps the instruction format simple and regular, and simplifies both pipelining and hazard detection.

Interrupts

An interrupt is a signal that suspends the CPU's normal instruction sequence so it can respond to an event requiring immediate attention, then resumes the interrupted program afterward. Interrupts are the primary mechanism by which slow I/O devices, timers, and error conditions get the CPU's attention without the CPU having to continuously poll them, making the system far more efficient than a pure polling approach.

Interrupts are broadly classified into: hardware interrupts, raised by external devices via a physical interrupt-request line, further divided into maskable interrupts (can be disabled by clearing the interrupt-enable flag, used for ordinary devices) and non-maskable interrupts (NMI) (cannot be disabled, reserved for critical events like power failure or memory-parity error); and software interrupts, deliberately triggered by an instruction in the running program (e.g., x86 INT n), commonly used to invoke operating-system services (system calls). A related category is the exception (or trap/fault), raised synchronously by the CPU itself when it encounters a condition such as divide-by-zero, illegal opcode, or a page fault.

The interrupt cycle proceeds as follows: after completing the current instruction, the CPU checks the interrupt line; if an interrupt is pending and not masked, it saves the current context (at minimum the Program Counter and status flags, pushed onto the stack), then fetches the address of the Interrupt Service Routine (ISR) — via a vectored scheme, where each interrupt source has a fixed slot in an interrupt vector table, so the hardware/OS jumps directly to the correct handler, or via a polled scheme, where the handler must query each device to find the one that raised the request. The ISR executes, and a special return instruction (e.g., IRET) restores the saved context, resuming the interrupted program exactly where it left off.

When multiple interrupts can occur, an interrupt priority scheme decides which is serviced first, and nested interrupts allow a higher-priority interrupt to itself interrupt a lower-priority ISR that is currently executing. Interrupts are also the trigger mechanism for Direct Memory Access (DMA) completion notification: the DMA controller performs the bulk data transfer, then interrupts the CPU only once, at the end, drastically reducing CPU involvement compared to programmed I/O.

  • Exam pointers
  • Classification: hardware (maskable/NMI) vs. software (INT instruction) vs. exceptions/traps (CPU-generated, synchronous).
  • Interrupt cycle: save PC + flags → fetch ISR address (vectored or polled) → execute ISR → IRET restores context.
  • Vectored interrupts (direct jump via interrupt vector table) vs. polling (query each device) — vectored is faster.
  • Interrupts relate directly to DMA: DMA moves data, then interrupts CPU only on completion — contrast with programmed I/O (CPU busy-waits) and interrupt-driven I/O (CPU notified per data unit).

Memory Types: RAM, DRAM, PROM, EPROM

Semiconductor memory is broadly split into RAM (read-write, volatile) and ROM family devices (read-only or write-rarely, non-volatile). Random Access Memory (RAM) means any memory location can be accessed in equal time regardless of its address (as opposed to sequential-access media like tape). RAM comes in two main technologies: Static RAM (SRAM), built from cross-coupled inverters (a 6-transistor flip-flop per bit), which holds its value as long as power is applied, needs no refreshing, and is very fast — used for CPU caches and registers, but is expensive and low-density; and Dynamic RAM (DRAM), which stores each bit as charge on a tiny capacitor accessed through a single transistor (1T1C cell), giving much higher density and lower cost per bit than SRAM, but the capacitor charge leaks away, so DRAM must be periodically refreshed (read and rewritten, typically every few milliseconds) — used for main memory.

Multiplicity of Functional Units

Rather than a single, general ALU handling every operation serially, high-performance CPU organizations provide multiple, specialized functional units — for example, separate units for integer addition, integer multiplication, floating-point addition, floating-point multiplication/division, logical operations, and branch/address calculation — each optimized in speed and area for its specific job. Because these units are independent, several instructions that use different functional units can be in progress at the same time, increasing overall throughput without necessarily increasing the clock rate.

The classic example is the CDC 6600 (Seymour Cray, 1964), which had ten separate functional units (two multiply units, one divide unit, one add unit, units for shift, boolean, branch, and increment operations) fed by a single instruction issue stream; a scoreboard (a hardware structure that tracks which registers and functional units are busy) dynamically dispatched each instruction to a free, appropriate unit as soon as its operands were ready, so instructions could complete out of program order provided there were no data dependencies. This idea directly foreshadowed the modern superscalar processor, which fetches and decodes multiple instructions per cycle and issues each one to whichever of several parallel execution units (ALU0, ALU1, load/store unit, FP unit, branch unit) is available and appropriate, using techniques like register renaming, reservation stations, and out-of-order execution to maximize the utilization of all the functional units simultaneously.

  • Exam pointers
  • Motivation: one instruction stream, many independent specialized functional units (int ALU, multiplier, divider, FP unit, branch unit) working concurrently to raise throughput.
  • Classic example: CDC 6600 — ten functional units plus a scoreboard for dynamic instruction dispatch and out-of-order completion.
  • Modern descendant: superscalar processors — multiple-issue per cycle across several parallel execution units.

Parallelism and Pipelining in the CPU

Pipelining is a technique that overlaps the execution of multiple instructions by dividing instruction processing into a sequence of independent stages, each handled by dedicated hardware, so that while one instruction is in its execute stage, the next can simultaneously be in its decode stage, and the one after in its fetch stage — much like an assembly line. The classic 5-stage RISC instruction pipeline consists of: IF (Instruction Fetch), ID (Instruction Decode / register read), EX (Execute in the ALU), MEM (Memory access, for loads/stores), and WB (Write Back the result to the register file). In the ideal case, with k pipeline stages, a program of n instructions completes in (k + n − 1) cycles rather than k×n cycles for fully sequential execution, giving a theoretical speedup approaching k for large n.

In practice, pipelines suffer hazards that prevent the next instruction from executing in its designated cycle. A structural hazard occurs when two instructions need the same hardware resource simultaneously (e.g., one memory port needed for both instruction fetch and data access) — resolved by duplicating resources (separate I-cache and D-cache) or stalling. A data hazard occurs when an instruction depends on the result of a previous instruction still in the pipeline (Read-After-Write is the common case) — resolved by forwarding/bypassing (routing the ALU result directly to a dependent instruction's input before it is written back) or, when forwarding is not enough (e.g., load-use hazard), by inserting a stall/bubble. A control hazard arises from branch instructions, since the pipeline does not know the next instruction to fetch until the branch is resolved — mitigated by branch prediction (guessing the outcome and speculatively fetching down that path, with a flush/rollback penalty on misprediction), delayed branching, or branch-target buffers.

Beyond a single pipeline, CPUs exploit further instruction-level parallelism (ILP) through superscalar execution (issuing multiple instructions per cycle to multiple pipelines/functional units) and out-of-order execution (executing instructions as their operands become ready rather than strictly in program order, using scoreboards or Tomasulo's algorithm, then retiring results in order). Amdahl's Law bounds the benefit of any such parallelism: overall speedup is limited by the fraction of the program that must remain sequential, Speedup = 1 / ((1 − P) + P/S), where P is the parallelizable fraction and S is the speedup of that portion.

  • Exam pointers
  • 5-stage pipeline: IF, ID, EX, MEM, WB — draw the timing diagram showing overlap across instructions.
  • Ideal pipeline time = (k + n − 1) cycles for k stages, n instructions; ideal speedup → k as n grows.
  • Three hazard types with remedies: structural (duplicate resources/stall), data (forwarding, stall for load-use hazard), control (branch prediction, delayed branch).
  • Amdahl's Law: Speedup = 1 / ((1−P) + P/S) — a frequent numerical question.
  • Superscalar and out-of-order execution extend pipelining to exploit instruction-level parallelism further.

Overlapped CPU and I/O Operations

I/O devices (disks, printers, network interfaces) operate orders of magnitude slower than the CPU, so if the CPU simply waited (busy-waited/polled) for every I/O operation to finish, most of its cycles would be wasted. Overlapping CPU computation with I/O activity is therefore essential for system throughput, and progressively more sophisticated techniques have been developed to achieve it.

Programmed I/O is the simplest scheme: the CPU issues a command to the device and then repeatedly polls a status register until the device signals completion — no overlap at all, the CPU is fully occupied. Interrupt-driven I/O improves this: the CPU issues the I/O command and then continues executing other instructions/processes; the device raises an interrupt only when it is ready to transfer data (or has completed), so the CPU is freed for computation during the (long) device access time, though the CPU is still involved in moving each unit of data once notified. Direct Memory Access (DMA) goes further: a dedicated DMA controller is given the source/destination addresses and transfer count, and then autonomously moves an entire block of data directly between the device and main memory over the bus, without CPU involvement in the individual transfers — the CPU is interrupted only once, when the entire block transfer is complete, giving the highest degree of overlap.

Additional software techniques increase overlap further: buffering stores data temporarily so producer and consumer (e.g., device and CPU, or two processes) can proceed at different rates without waiting for each other; spooling (Simultaneous Peripheral Operation On-Line) writes output destined for a slow device (like a printer) to a fast disk buffer first, so the requesting process can continue immediately while a separate spooler process feeds the device at its own pace. Large mainframes historically used dedicated I/O channels/processors — small autonomous processors that manage entire I/O subsystems independently of the main CPU, the ultimate extension of offloading I/O management from the CPU.

  • Exam pointers
  • Progression of overlap: programmed I/O (no overlap, CPU polls) → interrupt-driven I/O (CPU freed during access, involved per transfer) → DMA (CPU freed entirely except one interrupt at block completion).
  • DMA controller needs source, destination, and count, and takes over the system bus for the transfer (cycle stealing).
  • Buffering and spooling are software-level techniques that further decouple CPU speed from device speed.
  • I/O channels/processors: dedicated hardware offloading entire I/O subsystems, historically used in mainframes.

Multiprogramming and Time-Sharing

Multiprogramming keeps several jobs resident in main memory at once so that when the currently running job must wait for an I/O operation (which is slow compared to the CPU), the operating system switches the CPU to another ready job instead of leaving it idle — this dramatically raises CPU utilization compared to earlier batch systems that ran one job at a time to completion (including its I/O wait). Multiprogramming requires the OS to manage multiple processes concurrently: memory partitioning/protection so jobs do not interfere with each other, and a scheduler that decides which resident job runs next.

Time-sharing (multitasking) extends multiprogramming for interactive use: the CPU is divided into short time slices (quanta), and the scheduler rapidly switches among multiple users'/processes' jobs, giving each the illusion of having its own dedicated machine, since the switching happens fast enough that users perceive immediate response. The canonical time-sharing scheduling discipline is Round Robin: each ready process gets the CPU for at most one quantum, then (if not finished) is moved to the back of the ready queue — fair, and bounds the worst-case wait, but throughput and overhead depend heavily on the quantum size (too small → excessive context-switch overhead; too large → degenerates toward FCFS and hurts interactive responsiveness).

The historical contrast is with pure batch processing, where jobs are submitted, queued, and run one after another with no user interaction during execution and no overlap between one job's I/O and another job's CPU use — multiprogramming and time-sharing were developed specifically to overcome batch processing's poor resource utilization and lack of interactivity, and together they underlie every modern general-purpose operating system.

  • Exam pointers
  • Multiprogramming: multiple jobs in memory, switch to another job on I/O wait, raises CPU utilization — contrast with single-job batch processing.
  • Time-sharing: adds time-slicing (quantum) for fast switching among interactive users, giving the illusion of simultaneous dedicated access.
  • Round Robin is the archetypal time-sharing scheduling algorithm; quantum size is the key tuning parameter (trade-off: responsiveness vs. context-switch overhead).
  • Progression: batch processing → multiprogrammed batch → time-sharing → modern multitasking OS.

Pipeline Computers

A pipeline computer applies the assembly-line concept of pipelining not only to the instruction cycle (instruction pipelining, discussed above) but also within individual arithmetic operations (arithmetic/functional pipelining), and across a stream of independent data sets. The general idea is to break any repetitive multi-step process into stages, each handled by dedicated hardware, so that once the pipeline is full, one result emerges every clock cycle even though each individual operation still takes several cycles to complete end to end.

A canonical example is a pipelined floating-point adder, broken into stages such as: (1) compare exponents and determine the required alignment shift, (2) align the mantissa with the smaller exponent by shifting it right, (3) add or subtract the aligned mantissas, and (4) normalize the result (shift and adjust the exponent so the mantissa is in standard form) and round. Each stage is implemented by separate hardware, and a stream of independent addition operations can be fed into the pipeline one per cycle, so that after the initial fill latency, the adder produces one completed sum every cycle instead of one every four cycles.

Instruction pipelining and arithmetic pipelining are complementary and are typically combined in real processors: the instruction pipeline overlaps fetch/decode/execute/writeback across successive instructions, while inside the execute stage, pipelined functional units (pipelined multiplier, pipelined floating-point unit) allow long-latency arithmetic operations to also be overlapped across successive instructions that use them, rather than blocking the whole pipeline until one slow operation finishes.

  • Exam pointers
  • Two flavors of pipelining: instruction pipelining (overlap fetch/decode/execute of successive instructions) and arithmetic/functional pipelining (overlap the internal stages of a repeated arithmetic operation, e.g., floating-point add).
  • Floating-point add pipeline stages: compare exponents → align mantissas → add mantissas → normalize/round.
  • Throughput improves (one result per cycle once full) even though the latency of any single operation is unchanged.

Array Computers

An array computer (also called an SIMD array processor) achieves parallelism spatially rather than temporally: a single control unit fetches and decodes one instruction stream, but broadcasts each instruction to a large array of Processing Elements (PEs), each with its own ALU and local memory, so that every PE executes the same instruction simultaneously but on its own distinct piece of data. This is the "Single Instruction, multiple Data" (SIMD) category of Flynn's taxonomy, which classifies architectures along two axes — number of instruction streams and number of data streams — into SISD (classic von Neumann uniprocessor), SIMD (array/vector processors, and today, GPUs), MISD (rare, e.g., some fault-tolerant pipelines), and MIMD (multiprocessors/multicomputers, each processor with its own instruction stream, e.g., modern multicore CPUs and clusters).

Array computers are especially well suited to problems expressed as operations over vectors and matrices — image processing, weather simulation, linear algebra — where the same arithmetic operation must be applied uniformly to every element of a large data structure. The historical exemplar is the ILLIAC IV, which connected dozens of PEs to a common control unit and interconnection network so that, for example, adding two large matrices element-by-element could be done by all PEs simultaneously rather than one element at a time.

Array (SIMD) computers differ fundamentally from pipeline computers: a pipeline computer achieves parallelism by overlapping different stages of processing over time for a stream of operations (temporal parallelism, one instruction stream, results emerge one after another), while an array computer achieves parallelism by replicating processing hardware across space so many data elements are processed at the identical instant under one shared instruction stream (spatial parallelism). Both are distinct from MIMD multiprocessors, where each processing unit has independent control and can execute a different instruction stream on different data at the same time.

  • Exam pointers
  • Array computer = SIMD: one control unit, many PEs, each PE has its own ALU/local memory, same instruction broadcast to all, different data per PE.
  • Flynn's taxonomy: SISD, SIMD, MISD, MIMD — know an example of each.
  • Example machine: ILLIAC IV; application domain: vector/matrix operations, image processing.
  • Contrast clearly: pipeline computer = temporal parallelism (overlap over time); array computer = spatial parallelism (replicated hardware across space).

Process Management and Scheduling

A process is a program in execution — it comprises the program's code, its current activity (represented by the value of the Program Counter and the contents of the processor's registers), and its own address space (stack, heap, data, text segments). The operating system represents every process by a Process Control Block (PCB), a data structure holding the process ID, process state, saved CPU register values (context), scheduling information (priority, pointers to scheduling queues), memory-management information (page tables, segment tables), and I/O status information (open files, allocated devices).

A process moves through a well-defined set of states during its lifetime: New (being created), Ready (waiting to be assigned the CPU), Running (instructions being executed), Waiting/Blocked (waiting for some event, typically I/O completion), and Terminated (finished execution). The OS uses different schedulers at different time scales: the long-term scheduler (job scheduler) decides which jobs are admitted into the system (controls the degree of multiprogramming); the short-term scheduler (CPU scheduler) decides which ready process runs next on the CPU, invoked very frequently; and the medium-term scheduler handles swapping — temporarily removing processes from memory to reduce the degree of multiprogramming and later reintroducing them.

CPU scheduling algorithms are compared using criteria such as CPU utilization, throughput (jobs completed per unit time), turnaround time (time from submission to completion), waiting time (time spent in the ready queue), and response time (time from submission until first response, important for interactive systems). Common algorithms: First-Come-First-Served (FCFS — simple, non-preemptive, but suffers the "convoy effect" where short jobs wait behind long ones); Shortest Job First (SJF — provably minimizes average waiting time, but requires knowing/estimating burst length and can starve long jobs); Priority Scheduling (runs the highest-priority ready process, can starve low-priority processes, mitigated by aging); Round Robin (time-quantum-based, preemptive, fair, good for time-sharing, discussed earlier); and Multilevel Queue/Multilevel Feedback Queue scheduling (partitions processes into queues, e.g., interactive vs. batch, each with its own algorithm and priority, with feedback queues allowing a process to move between queues based on observed behavior).

A worked example clarifies FCFS vs. SJF: given processes P1 (burst 24), P2 (burst 3), P3 (burst 3) arriving together, FCFS in submitted order 24,3,3 gives average waiting time (0+24+27)/3 = 17, whereas SJF ordering 3,3,24 gives average waiting time (0+3+6)/3 = 3 — demonstrating why SJF is optimal for minimizing average waiting time, though it requires burst-time knowledge that is rarely available in practice, only estimated (e.g., via exponential averaging of past bursts).

  • Exam pointers
  • PCB contents: PID, state, saved registers/context, scheduling info, memory info, I/O status.
  • Process states and transitions: New → Ready ⇄ Running → Waiting → Ready → ... → Terminated.
  • Three scheduler levels: long-term (admission), short-term (CPU dispatch), medium-term (swapping).
  • Scheduling criteria: CPU utilization, throughput, turnaround time, waiting time, response time.
  • Be ready to compute average waiting/turnaround time for FCFS, SJF, Priority, and Round Robin given a Gantt-chart style problem — a very common numerical question.

Process Co-operation: IPC and Synchronization

Cooperating processes are processes that can affect or be affected by other processes running in the system, typically because they share data. The OS provides Inter-Process Communication (IPC) mechanisms to let them exchange information: shared memory, where the OS maps a region of memory into the address space of two or more processes so they can read/write it directly (fast, since no kernel intervention is needed for each access, but the processes themselves must coordinate access); and message passing, where processes exchange data through OS-mediated send/receive primitives (pipes, message queues, sockets) — simpler to get correct and works across machines (distributed systems), but has more overhead per exchange since the kernel is involved in every transfer. Other common IPC facilities include named/unnamed pipes (a unidirectional byte stream between related or named processes), signals (asynchronous notifications), and sockets (for network-transparent communication).

Whenever multiple processes/threads access shared data concurrently, a race condition can occur — the final result depends on the unpredictable timing/interleaving of their execution, producing incorrect results. The section of code that accesses shared data is called the critical section, and any correct solution to the critical-section problem must satisfy three requirements: mutual exclusion (no two processes may be in their critical sections simultaneously), progress (a process not in its critical section cannot indefinitely block others from entering theirs), and bounded waiting (a limit exists on how many times other processes may enter their critical section before a waiting process is granted entry, preventing starvation).

The classic synchronization primitive is the semaphore, an integer variable accessed only through two atomic operations: wait(S) (also called P, decrements S, blocking the calling process if S becomes negative) and signal(S) (also called V, increments S, waking a blocked process if any are waiting). A binary semaphore (0 or 1) implements mutual exclusion directly (equivalent to a mutex lock); a counting semaphore can also manage a pool of a limited number of identical resources. Higher-level constructs include the mutex lock (a simple binary lock with acquire/release) and the monitor (a programming-language-level construct that bundles shared data with the procedures that access it and enforces mutual exclusion automatically, using condition variables for waiting on specific conditions inside the monitor).

Classic synchronization problems test understanding of these tools: the Producer-Consumer (bounded buffer) problem, where producers must not add to a full buffer and consumers must not remove from an empty one, solved using semaphores for "empty slots," "full slots," and mutual exclusion on the buffer; the Readers-Writers problem, where any number of readers may access shared data concurrently but a writer requires exclusive access; and the Dining Philosophers problem, where five philosophers alternately think and eat, need two shared forks to eat, and a naive solution can deadlock (each picks up their left fork and waits forever for the right) — solved by strategies such as limiting the number of philosophers allowed to pick up forks at once, imposing a strict global ordering on fork acquisition, or making one philosopher pick up forks in the opposite order.

DOS, Windows, UNIX

MS-DOS (Disk Operating System) is a single-user, single-tasking operating system for early IBM-PC-compatible microcomputers. It provides a simple command-line interface (COMMAND.COM), runs in the processor's real mode (direct physical addressing, no memory protection, so any program can crash the whole system), has no built-in multitasking or user-account concept, and manages files via the FAT (File Allocation Table) file system.

Microsoft Windows evolved from a graphical shell over DOS into a full protected-mode, preemptively multitasking operating system (from Windows NT/2000/XP onward). It provides a Graphical User Interface (GUI) as the primary interaction model, hardware abstraction and memory protection between processes, the NTFS file system (supporting permissions, journaling, encryption, large volumes), and a centralized configuration database (the Registry). Modern Windows supports multi-user accounts, preemptive multitasking, and networked/domain-based administration.

UNIX, originally developed at Bell Labs (Thompson and Ritchie) in the late 1960s/early 1970s, established design principles that remain influential: it is a multi-user, multitasking operating system built around a small kernel plus a large collection of small utility programs; it follows the philosophy "everything is a file" (devices, pipes, and even some kernel interfaces are accessed through the file-system namespace); it uses a hierarchical, tree-structured file system rooted at /; and, crucially, it was written almost entirely in the C programming language, which made it far more portable across hardware than earlier assembly-written operating systems. UNIX cleanly separates the kernel (process/memory/file/device management) from the shell (the user's command interpreter, itself just an ordinary program), so users can choose or replace their shell without touching the kernel.

File Systems, Filters, and Pipelines

A file system organizes how data is stored, named, and retrieved on secondary storage. It maintains a directory structure (in UNIX/Linux, a single hierarchical tree rooted at /; in DOS/Windows, a tree per drive letter) and, for each file, metadata describing its attributes and the disk blocks it occupies. UNIX-family file systems typically use an inode (index node) for each file — a structure holding the file's owner, permissions, timestamps, size, and pointers to its data blocks (direct pointers for small files, plus indirect/double-indirect pointers for larger ones) — while the directory itself is just a simple table mapping file names to inode numbers. DOS/Windows historically used the FAT (File Allocation Table), a linked-list-like table where each entry points to the next block of the file, later succeeded by NTFS's more sophisticated Master File Table. Disk space for a file can be allocated contiguously (fast sequential access, but fragmentation and difficult growth), as a linked list of blocks (easy growth, but poor random access and pointer overhead), or via indexed allocation (an index block lists all the file's block addresses, as inodes do — good random access, one extra lookup).

A filter, in the UNIX tradition, is a small program that reads data from standard input, transforms or analyzes it in some well-defined way, and writes the result to standard output — for example, grep (select lines matching a pattern), sort (order lines), wc (count lines/words/characters), cut, uniq, awk, and sed. Because every filter follows the same "stdin in, stdout out" convention, filters can be freely combined.

A pipeline connects the standard output of one command directly to the standard input of the next using the shell's pipe operator |, letting several small, single-purpose filters be composed into a more powerful data-processing chain without any temporary files. For example, cat access.log | grep "ERROR" | sort | uniq -c | sort -rn reads a log file, keeps only lines containing "ERROR," sorts them, counts duplicate lines, and finally sorts the counts in descending numeric order — a compact pipeline performing what would otherwise require a custom program. Redirection operators complement pipes: > and >> send a command's standard output to a file (overwrite vs. append), and < feeds a file's contents as a command's standard input.

  • Exam pointers
  • Inode holds file metadata + block pointers; directory maps name → inode number (UNIX/Linux model).
  • File allocation methods: contiguous, linked, indexed — know the trade-offs of each.
  • Filter = program following the stdin-in/stdout-out convention (grep, sort, wc, awk, sed); this uniformity is exactly what makes piping possible.
  • Pipeline (|) chains filters without intermediate files; redirection (>, >>, <) connects commands to files.

C-Shell

The C shell (csh, and its improved successor tcsh) is a UNIX command interpreter written by Bill Joy at Berkeley, designed with control-flow and expression syntax deliberately modeled on the C programming language, in contrast to the Bourne shell (sh) lineage. It was historically popular for interactive use because of convenience features it introduced: command history and history substitution (e.g., !! repeats the previous command, !n repeats history entry n, !$ reuses the last argument of the previous command), built-in aliases (short names bound to longer command strings, e.g., alias ll "ls -l"), and integrated job control (suspending a job with Ctrl-Z, resuming it in the background with bg or foreground with fg, listing jobs with jobs).

C-shell distinguishes shell variables (local to the current shell, set with set name = value, referenced as $name) from environment variables (inherited by child processes, set with setenv NAME value) — this split between set/setenv is a distinctive C-shell feature not present in that form in Bourne-style shells (which use a single export mechanism). C-shell scripting syntax mirrors C control structures, e.g., if ( condition ) then ... else ... endif and foreach var ( list ) ... end, rather than the Bourne shell's if ... then ... fi and for ... do ... done. Despite its interactive conveniences, csh scripting is generally considered less robust for complex shell programming than Bourne-family shells (sh/bash/ksh), which is why many system scripts still prefer the latter even on systems where csh/tcsh remains available for interactive login shells.

  • Exam pointers
  • C-shell = C-like syntax shell (Bill Joy, BSD); contrast with Bourne shell (sh) lineage.
  • Distinctive features: history substitution (!!, !n, !$), aliases, integrated job control (Ctrl-Z, bg, fg, jobs).
  • set (shell variable) vs. setenv (environment variable) — a signature C-shell distinction.
  • Control-flow keywords: if/then/else/endif, foreach/end — modeled on C, differing from sh's if/fi, for/done.

LINUX

Linux is a free, open-source, UNIX-like operating system kernel originally written by Linus Torvalds in 1991 and since developed collaboratively by a huge community under the GNU General Public License. Strictly, "Linux" refers to the kernel; a complete usable system ("distribution" or "distro" — e.g., Ubuntu, Debian, Fedora, Red Hat Enterprise Linux, CentOS) combines the Linux kernel with the GNU toolchain and utilities (compiler, shell, core utilities), a package manager, and typically a desktop environment or windowing system (X Window System or Wayland) for GUI use, which is why the fuller and more accurate term is "GNU/Linux."

Architecturally, Linux is a monolithic kernel (unlike microkernel designs): core services — process scheduling, memory management, file systems, networking, device drivers — run in kernel space for performance, but modularity is retained through loadable kernel modules (LKMs), which can be inserted into or removed from the running kernel without a reboot (e.g., to add a new device driver). Linux inherits and extends classic UNIX features: multi-user, multitasking operation with preemptive scheduling; a hierarchical file system supporting multiple file-system types (ext4, XFS, Btrfs, and others) mountable anywhere in the single directory tree; virtual memory with paging and swap; a permissions model based on user/group/other read-write-execute (rwx) bits (extended by Access Control Lists for finer-grained control); and robust TCP/IP networking built into the kernel.

Linux's openness, stability, and cost (free to use, modify, and redistribute) have made it the dominant OS for servers, cloud infrastructure, embedded systems, and (as the base of Android) mobile devices, and its shell/utility ecosystem is what makes the filters-and-pipelines model of computing (discussed above) practically ubiquitous today.

  • Monolithic kernel with loadable kernel modules — gives performance of monolithic design with some of the flexibility of modular design.
  • Inherited UNIX features: multi-user/multitasking, hierarchical file system, rwx permission model, TCP/IP networking, virtual memory.
  • Dominant in servers, cloud, embedded, and (via Android) mobile — largely due to being free and open-source.

Linear Data Structures: Lists, Stacks, and Queues

Linear data structures arrange elements sequentially, where each element (except the first/last) has a unique predecessor and successor. The two fundamental physical representations are the array (contiguous memory, O(1) random access by index via address = base + index×element_size, but O(n) insertion/deletion in the middle since elements must be shifted, and a fixed or costly-to-resize capacity) and the linked list (nodes scattered in memory, each holding data plus a pointer to the next node, giving O(1) insertion/deletion once the position is known, but only O(n) sequential access since there is no direct indexing, plus per-node pointer storage overhead). Variants include the singly linked list (one forward pointer per node), doubly linked list (forward and backward pointers, allowing O(1) reverse traversal and easier deletion), and circular linked list (the last node points back to the first, useful for round-robin style applications).

A stack is a Last-In-First-Out (LIFO) structure supporting two primary operations: push (add an element to the top) and pop (remove and return the top element), both O(1), typically implemented atop an array (with a top index) or a linked list (insert/remove at the head). Stacks are fundamental to program execution itself: the call stack stores each function call's return address, parameters, and local variables (its activation record

A queue is a First-In-First-Out (FIFO) structure supporting enqueue (add at the rear) and dequeue (remove from the front), modeling any first-come-first-served scenario — CPU ready queues, print spoolers, breadth-first search's frontier. A naive array-based queue wastes space as the front index advances, so a circular queue wraps the rear index back to 0 once it reaches the array's end, reusing freed slots. A deque (double-ended queue) allows insertion and removal at both ends, generalizing both stack and queue behavior. A priority queue, where each element carries a priority and the highest (or lowest) priority element is served first regardless of arrival order, is typically implemented with a heap for efficiency and is covered in detail as a separate topic below.

  • Exam pointers
  • Array: O(1) random access, O(n) mid-insert/delete. Linked list: O(1) insert/delete at a known position, O(n) access/search — know this trade-off cold.
  • Singly vs. doubly vs. circular linked list — know one advantage of each.
  • Stack = LIFO (push/pop, O(1)); underlies function-call recursion via the call stack/activation records; applications: parenthesis matching, undo, backtracking, expression evaluation.
  • Queue = FIFO (enqueue/dequeue); circular queue avoids wasted array space; deque supports both ends; priority queue serves by priority, not arrival order.

Searching: Linear Search, Binary Search, and Hashing

Linear (sequential) search examines each element one by one until the target is found or the list is exhausted — it requires no ordering of the data, has O(n) worst-case and average-case time, and O(1) space. Binary search requires the data to be sorted: it repeatedly compares the target to the middle element and discards the half of the remaining range that cannot contain the target, so the search space halves each comparison, giving O(log n) time. The algorithm maintains low and high bounds, computes mid = (low + high) / 2, and recurses/iterates on the left half if target < a[mid], the right half if target > a[mid], or returns mid on equality — a classic divide-and-conquer technique whose cost is the recurrence T(n) = T(n/2) + O(1).

Hashing aims for O(1) average-case search by computing a hash function h(key) that maps a key directly to an index (bucket) in a hash table, rather than comparing against other keys at all. A good hash function distributes keys uniformly across buckets to minimize collisions (two different keys mapping to the same index). Two standard collision-resolution families exist: chaining, where each bucket holds a linked list (or small tree) of all keys hashed to it, so a collision simply appends to that bucket's list — degrades gracefully, but needs extra pointer memory; and open addressing, where a colliding key is placed in another slot in the table itself, found by a probing sequence — linear probing (try the next slot, slot+1, slot+2, ...; simple but prone to clustering), quadratic probing (try slot+1², slot+2², ... to reduce clustering), and double hashing (use a second hash function to determine the probe step size, spreading collisions most evenly). Performance depends heavily on the load factor α = n/m (number of keys / number of buckets); as α approaches 1, open-addressing performance degrades sharply, so tables are resized (rehashed) once α exceeds a threshold (commonly 0.7).

Where the syllabus additionally names a hash tree as a search structure, this refers to organizing hashed keys in a tree rather than a flat array, combining hashing's fast key dispatch with a tree's ability to grow incrementally and support range/prefix operations. Two concrete forms are common: extendible hashing, a dynamic, tree-directed hashing scheme (widely used for database/file indexes) where a directory of pointers (indexed by the leading bits of a key's hash) points to buckets, and the directory doubles (or a bucket splits and a local sub-tree of the directory deepens) only for the region that overflows, giving O(1) average access with graceful, localized growth instead of a full-table rehash; and the Merkle (hash) tree, used for data-integrity verification (e.g., in version-control systems, blockchains, and distributed file systems), where each leaf is the hash of a data block and each internal node is the hash of its children's hashes, so a single root hash can certify the integrity of an entire large dataset, and any tampering can be localized in O(log n) by walking down the tree comparing hashes.

  • Exam pointers
  • Linear search O(n), no ordering required; binary search O(log n), requires sorted data, divide-and-conquer.
  • Hashing: h(key) → bucket index, average O(1) search; collisions resolved by chaining (linked lists per bucket) or open addressing (linear/quadratic probing, double hashing).
  • Load factor α = n/m governs performance; rehash/resize when α crosses a threshold.
  • Hash tree = tree structure built over hashed keys: extendible hashing (DB/file indexing, localized incremental growth) and Merkle tree (integrity verification via hash-of-hashes up to a root).

Search Trees: BST, AVL, and B-Tree

A Binary Search Tree (BST) is a binary tree where, for every node, all keys in its left subtree are smaller and all keys in its right subtree are larger. This ordering property makes search, insertion, and deletion all run in O(h), where h is the tree's height, by repeatedly comparing the target key to the current node and descending left or right. For a "bushy" (balanced) tree of n nodes, h = O(log n), giving efficient O(log n) operations; but a BST built from already-sorted input degenerates into a linked list (h = O(n)), making operations O(n) in the worst case — this is precisely the motivation for self-balancing trees.

An AVL tree (Adelson-Velsky and Landis) is a self-balancing BST that maintains, for every node, a balance factor (height of left subtree − height of right subtree) restricted to {−1, 0, +1}. Whenever an insertion or deletion would push a node's balance factor outside this range, the tree performs a rotation to restore balance: a single left or right rotation (LL or RR case) when the imbalance is on one consistent side, or a double rotation (LR or RL case) when the imbalance alternates direction down the path. Because rotations restore balance in O(1) time per rotation and at most O(log n) rotations/rebalancing checks are needed up the path after any update, AVL trees guarantee O(log n) search, insert, and delete in the worst case, at the cost of extra bookkeeping (storing/updating balance factors or heights) and more rotation overhead than an unbalanced BST.

A B-tree generalizes the BST to a multi-way balanced search tree, specifically designed for storage systems where each node corresponds to a disk block: a B-tree of order m allows each node to hold up to m−1 keys and up to m children, keeps all leaves at the same depth (perfectly balanced), and requires every non-root node to be at least half full — minimizing the number of disk reads (tree height) needed to reach any key, which matters enormously when each node access is an expensive disk I/O rather than a cheap in-memory comparison. This is exactly why B-trees (and their common variant, the B+ tree, where all actual data/records are stored only in the leaf nodes, which are additionally linked together for fast sequential/range scans, while internal nodes hold only routing keys) are the standard indexing structure used inside relational database management systems and file systems.

  • Exam pointers
  • BST: ordering property (left < node < right); O(h) operations; degenerates to O(n) on sorted input — this is the key motivation for balanced trees.
  • AVL: balance factor restricted to {−1,0,1}; rotations LL, RR, LR, RL restore balance; guarantees O(log n) worst case.
  • B-tree: multi-way, disk-oriented, all leaves at same depth, minimum occupancy per node — minimizes disk I/O/tree height.
  • B+ tree (used in DBMS indexes): data only at linked leaf level, internal nodes hold routing keys only — enables fast range queries.

Graphs

A graph G = (V, E) is a set of vertices V connected by a set of edges E, which may be directed or undirected, and weighted or unweighted. Graphs are represented in two standard ways: an adjacency matrix, an n×n matrix where entry [i][j] indicates (and, if weighted, gives the weight of) an edge from vertex i to vertex j — O(1) edge-existence lookup and simple to implement, but O(n²) space regardless of how sparse the graph actually is; and an adjacency list, where each vertex keeps a list of its neighboring vertices — space-efficient at O(V + E), the standard choice for sparse graphs, though checking whether a specific edge exists takes O(degree) rather than O(1).

The two fundamental traversal algorithms are Breadth-First Search (BFS), which explores a graph level by level using a queue (visit all neighbors of the start vertex before any neighbors of those neighbors), naturally finding the shortest path (fewest edges) from the source in an unweighted graph, with time complexity O(V + E); and Depth-First Search (DFS), which explores as far as possible along each branch before backtracking, implemented with an explicit stack or via recursion (using the call stack), also O(V + E), and the basis for detecting cycles, topological sorting (of a DAG), and finding connected components.

For weighted graphs, Dijkstra's algorithm computes the shortest path from a single source to all other vertices (non-negative edge weights only) by greedily selecting, at each step, the unvisited vertex with the smallest known tentative distance and relaxing its outgoing edges, typically implemented with a priority queue (min-heap) for O((V + E) log V) time. For finding a Minimum Spanning Tree (MST — a subset of edges connecting all vertices with minimum total weight, no cycles), Prim's algorithm grows a single tree one vertex/edge at a time by always adding the cheapest edge connecting the tree to a new vertex (again efficiently implemented with a priority queue), while Kruskal's algorithm sorts all edges by weight and greedily adds each edge that does not create a cycle (checked efficiently with a Union-Find/Disjoint-Set structure), both running in O(E log E) (dominated by the edge-sorting or heap operations).

  • Exam pointers
  • Adjacency matrix: O(n²) space, O(1) edge check — good for dense graphs. Adjacency list: O(V+E) space — good for sparse graphs.
  • BFS: queue-based, level-order, shortest path in unweighted graphs, O(V+E). DFS: stack/recursion-based, backtracking, cycle detection, topological sort, O(V+E).
  • Dijkstra: single-source shortest path, non-negative weights, priority-queue based, O((V+E) log V).
  • MST: Prim's (grow one tree, vertex by vertex) vs. Kruskal's (sort edges, avoid cycles via Union-Find) — both greedy, both O(E log E).

Priority Queues and Heaps

A priority queue is an abstract data type where each element has an associated priority, and the operation extract always removes the element with the highest (or, for a min-priority queue, the lowest) priority, regardless of insertion order — this generalizes the plain FIFO queue. The standard, efficient implementation is a binary heap: a complete binary tree (every level fully filled except possibly the last, which fills left to right) satisfying the heap property — in a min-heap, every parent's key is ≤ its children's keys (so the minimum is always at the root); a max-heap is the mirror image. Because the tree is complete, it can be stored compactly in a plain array with no pointers at all: for a node at array index i (0-based), its children are at indices 2i+1 and 2i+2, and its parent is at index ⌊(i−1)/2⌋.

Insertion appends the new element at the next free array slot (end of the array, preserving completeness) and then repeatedly compares it with its parent, swapping upward ("bubble up" / "sift up") until the heap property is restored — O(log h) = O(log n). Extracting the minimum/maximum removes the root, moves the last element in the array into the root position (preserving completeness), and then repeatedly compares it with its children, swapping downward with the smaller/larger child ("sift down" / "heapify") until the heap property is restored — also O(log n). An entire array of n elements can be converted into a heap in O(n) time (not O(n log n)) by applying sift-down starting from the last non-leaf node up to the root — this bottom-up build-heap procedure is the first phase of heapsort, which then repeatedly extracts the maximum (swapping it to the end of the array and shrinking the heap by one) to produce a fully sorted array in O(n log n) time, in-place, with no extra memory — an advantage over merge sort — though it is not a stable sort.

Priority queues/heaps are the standard implementation choice wherever "process the currently most urgent item next" is required: CPU priority scheduling, Dijkstra's and Prim's algorithms (both repeatedly extract the minimum-distance/cheapest-edge vertex), event-driven simulation, and Huffman coding (repeatedly combining the two least-frequent symbols/nodes).

  • Exam pointers
  • Binary heap = complete binary tree with the heap-order property, stored in an array; parent/child index formulas: children at 2i+1, 2i+2; parent at ⌊(i−1)/2⌋.
  • Insert: append + sift up, O(log n). Extract-min/max: replace root with last element + sift down, O(log n). Build-heap from an array: O(n), not O(n log n).
  • Heapsort: build-heap then repeatedly extract-max — O(n log n), in-place, not stable.
  • Applications: priority scheduling, Dijkstra/Prim (priority-queue-based shortest path/MST), Huffman coding.

Performance of Searching and Sorting Algorithms

Algorithm performance is expressed using asymptotic (Big-O) notation, which describes how running time or space grows as input size n grows, ignoring constant factors — this lets algorithms be compared independent of hardware or implementation details. For sorting, the key structural distinction is between simple O(n²) algorithms (easy to implement, fine for small or nearly-sorted inputs) and O(n log n) divide-and-conquer or heap-based algorithms (necessary for large inputs).

Bubble sort repeatedly steps through the list, swapping adjacent out-of-order elements, until no swaps are needed — simple but O(n²) even in typical cases. Selection sort repeatedly finds the minimum of the unsorted remainder and swaps it into place — always O(n²) comparisons regardless of input order, but only O(n) swaps, useful when swap cost is high. Insertion sort builds the sorted portion one element at a time, inserting each new element into its correct position among those already sorted — O(n²) worst/average case but O(n) best case (already sorted input) and efficient in practice for small or nearly-sorted arrays, which is why it is often used as the base case inside hybrid sorts. Merge sort recursively splits the array in half, sorts each half, and merges the two sorted halves in linear time — guaranteed O(n log n) in all cases (recurrence T(n) = 2T(n/2) + O(n)), stable, but needs O(n) auxiliary space for merging. Quicksort picks a pivot, partitions the array so smaller elements precede it and larger elements follow, then recursively sorts each partition — O(n log n) on average (excellent constants, usually the fastest in practice) but O(n²) worst case if the pivot choice repeatedly produces unbalanced partitions (e.g., naive first-element pivot on already-sorted input), mitigated by randomized or median-of-three pivot selection. Heapsort (described above) guarantees O(n log n) with O(1) extra space but is not stable and has worse constant factors than quicksort in practice.

String and List Processing

A string is a sequence of characters, typically stored either as a contiguous array with an explicit length (length-prefixed strings, as in Pascal) or as a contiguous array terminated by a special sentinel character (null-terminated strings, '\0', as in C) — the former makes length retrieval O(1) but limits maximum length or requires reallocation, while the latter allows arbitrary length but makes computing the length an O(n) scan. Basic string operations include concatenation, extracting a substring, comparison, and searching for a pattern within a larger text (pattern matching). The naive pattern-matching algorithm slides the pattern across the text one position at a time, comparing character by character, giving O(n·m) worst-case time for text length n and pattern length m; the Knuth-Morris-Pratt (KMP) algorithm improves this to O(n + m) by precomputing, for the pattern itself, how far it can safely skip ahead on a mismatch (using a "failure function" that captures the pattern's own repeated prefixes), so previously matched characters are never re-compared.

List processing refers to computation organized around traversing, constructing, and transforming linked-list-based structures rather than arrays — historically associated with the LISP family of languages, where the list is the fundamental data structure for both data and (via homoiconicity) code itself. Core list operations are: traversal (visiting every node once, O(n)), insertion and deletion at a known position (O(1) once located, since only pointers are relinked), search (O(n), since only sequential access is possible), reversal (rebuilding the chain of next-pointers so each node points to its former predecessor, O(n) time and O(1) extra space if done iteratively in place), and concatenation of two lists (O(1) if a tail pointer to the end of the first list is maintained, else O(n) to find it).

A generalized list extends the simple linear linked list by allowing an element itself to be another list (nested lists), which is exactly how structured/recursive data (trees, expressions, nested records) is represented uniformly in list-processing systems — a technique central to symbolic computation, where programs manipulate expressions (e.g., algebraic formulas, parse trees) represented as nested lists rather than raw numeric data.

  • Naive string matching O(n·m); KMP improves to O(n+m) using a precomputed failure function to avoid re-scanning matched characters.
  • List processing core operations and their costs: traversal O(n), insert/delete at known position O(1), search O(n), reversal O(n) time/O(1) space, concatenation O(1) with a tail pointer.
  • Generalized (nested) lists represent recursive/tree-structured data — foundational to LISP-style symbolic computation.

Prefix, Postfix, Infix Expressions, and Recursion

An arithmetic expression can be written in three equivalent notations. Infix is the conventional human notation, with the operator between its operands (e.g., A + B * C), but it requires operator precedence rules and parentheses to be unambiguous. Prefix (Polish notation) places the operator before its operands (e.g., + A * B C); postfix (Reverse Polish Notation) places the operator after its operands (e.g., A B C * +). Prefix and postfix are unambiguous without any parentheses or precedence rules, because the position of each operator relative to its exact operands is fixed by the notation itself — this is precisely why postfix is the internal representation of choice for expression evaluation in compilers and calculators.

Converting infix to postfix is done with the shunting-yard algorithm, using an operator stack: scan the infix expression left to right; output operands immediately; when an operator is read, pop and output any stack-top operators of greater or equal precedence, then push the new operator; a left parenthesis is pushed, and a right parenthesis pops and outputs operators until the matching left parenthesis is found (discarding both parentheses); at the end, pop and output all remaining operators. Evaluating a postfix expression is then a simple single left-to-right pass using a stack: push each operand onto the stack; when an operator is encountered, pop the required number of operands (two, for a binary operator), apply the operator, and push the result back — the value remaining on the stack at the end is the final answer. For example, evaluating 6 3 2 * +: push 6, push 3, push 2; on *, pop 3 and 2, compute 3×2=6, push 6 (stack: 6, 6); on +, pop 6 and 6, compute 6+6=12 — result 12 (equivalent to infix 6 + 3 * 2).

Recursion is a technique where a function solves a problem by calling itself on a smaller instance of the same problem, always requiring a base case (a smallest instance solved directly, without further recursive calls, which terminates the recursion) and a recursive case (which reduces the problem toward the base case). Each recursive call creates a new activation record/stack frame on the call stack, holding that call's parameters, local variables, and return address, which is exactly why deep recursion without a reachable base case exhausts the stack (stack overflow) and why recursion is intimately connected to the stack data structure discussed earlier. Direct recursion is a function calling itself; indirect (mutual) recursion is function A calling function B which calls A back. Tail recursion is the special case where the recursive call is the very last operation performed (nothing is done with its result afterward) — this allows a compiler to optimize the recursion into an iterative loop (tail-call optimization), reusing a single stack frame instead of growing the stack, though not all languages/compilers perform this optimization.

Classic recursive definitions include factorial (n! = n × (n−1)!, base case 0! = 1) and Fibonacci (fib(n) = fib(n−1) + fib(n−2), base cases fib(0)=0, fib(1)=1) — the naive recursive Fibonacci is instructive because it recomputes overlapping subproblems exponentially many times (O(2^n)), motivating memoization/dynamic programming as the standard fix, reducing it to O(n).

  • Postfix evaluation using a stack: push operands, on operator pop two/apply/push result — a very common numerical/trace question.
  • Recursion requires a base case + recursive case; each call adds a stack frame (activation record); no base case reached → stack overflow.
  • Direct vs. indirect (mutual) recursion; tail recursion enables tail-call optimization into iteration.
  • Naive recursive Fibonacci is O(2^n) due to repeated subproblems — memoization/DP reduces it to O(n); a good example linking recursion to algorithm efficiency.

Entity-Relationship (ER) Diagram

The Entity-Relationship model, introduced by Peter Chen, is a conceptual data-modeling technique used during database design to capture the structure of an application's data before it is translated into relational tables. An entity is a distinguishable real-world object or concept about which data is stored (e.g., Employee, Account, Branch); an entity set is a collection of similar entities. Entities have attributes — simple/atomic (cannot be subdivided, e.g., Age), composite (can be broken into sub-parts, e.g., Address = Street + City + ZIP), derived (computed from other data rather than stored, e.g., Age derived from Date-of-Birth, usually shown with a dashed oval), and multivalued (an entity can have several values for that attribute, e.g., PhoneNumbers, shown with a double oval). Every entity set must have a key attribute that uniquely identifies each entity instance.

A relationship is an association among two or more entities (e.g., an Employee "works in" a Department). Relationships carry a cardinality ratio describing how many entities on one side can be associated with entities on the other: one-to-one (1:1) (e.g., each Employee manages at most one Department, and each Department has at most one manager), one-to-many (1:N) (e.g., one Department has many Employees, but each Employee belongs to one Department), and many-to-many (M:N) (e.g., Students enroll in many Courses, and each Course has many Students — in the relational mapping this requires a separate junction/bridge table). Relationships also have participation constraints: total participation (every entity instance must participate in the relationship, drawn as a double line) versus partial participation (participation is optional, single line).

A weak entity is one that cannot be uniquely identified by its own attributes alone and depends on a related "owner" (strong/identifying) entity for its identification — for example, a Dependent entity identified only in combination with the Employee it belongs to; weak entities are conventionally drawn with a double-bordered rectangle, and their identifying relationship with a double-bordered diamond. When an ER diagram is mapped to relational tables, each strong entity set typically becomes a table (attributes become columns, the key attribute becomes the primary key), each 1:N relationship is realized by placing the "one" side's primary key as a foreign key in the "many" side's table, and each M:N relationship requires its own junction table holding foreign keys to both participating entities (plus any relationship-specific attributes).

  • Cardinality ratios: 1:1, 1:N, M:N — with examples; participation constraint: total (double line) vs. partial (single line).
  • Weak entity: no independent key, depends on an owner/strong entity, double-bordered rectangle + double-bordered identifying relationship.
  • ER-to-relational mapping rules: entity → table; 1:N → foreign key on the "many" side; M:N → separate junction table.

Data Flow Diagram (DFD)

A Data Flow Diagram is a graphical tool used in structured systems analysis to model how data moves through an information system, focusing purely on data flow and transformation rather than control flow, timing, or physical implementation. A DFD uses four standard symbols: a process (a rounded rectangle or circle, representing a transformation that takes input data and produces output data, labeled with an action verb, e.g., "Validate Loan Application"); a data store (an open-ended rectangle or pair of parallel lines, representing data at rest, e.g., a file or database table, such as "Customer Master File"); a data flow (a labeled arrow, representing data in motion between a process, data store, or external entity); and an external entity/terminator (a rectangle, representing a source or sink of data outside the system boundary, e.g., "Customer" or "Bank Branch").

DFDs are built in levels of increasing detail, following a top-down decomposition: the Context Diagram (Level 0) shows the entire system as a single process, connected only to its external entities, establishing the system's boundary and its inputs/outputs to the outside world; Level 1 decomposes that single process into its major sub-processes and shows the data stores and flows among them; and further levels (Level 2, and so on) decompose individual Level 1 processes into finer sub-processes, continuing until each process is simple enough to specify precisely (a "primitive" process, typically further described in a process specification or mini-spec).

A crucial correctness rule is balancing: the inputs and outputs of a process at one level must exactly match the inputs and outputs of the diagram that decomposes it at the next level down — no data may appear or disappear across a decomposition. Other conventions: a data flow may not go directly from one external entity to another (it must pass through at least one process of the system), a data store cannot connect directly to an external entity or to another data store (data must be read/written via a process), and every process should have at least one input and one output (a process with no input is a pure generator, which is usually a modeling error).

  • Exam pointers
  • Four DFD symbols: process (transforms data), data store (data at rest), data flow (labeled arrow), external entity (source/sink outside system boundary).
  • Leveling: Context Diagram (Level 0, whole system as one process) → Level 1 (major sub-processes) → Level 2+ (further decomposition).
  • Balancing rule: inputs/outputs must be preserved exactly across a decomposition level.
  • Illegal DFD constructs: external-entity-to-external-entity flow, data-store-to-external-entity flow, data-store-to-data-store flow — all must pass through a process.

Normalization (1NF, 2NF, 3NF, BCNF)

Normalization is a systematic, step-by-step process of decomposing relations (tables) to eliminate data redundancy and prevent modification anomalies: insertion anomalies (unable to record a fact because an unrelated fact is missing, e.g., cannot add a new course until a student enrolls in it, if course and enrollment data are combined), deletion anomalies (deleting one fact unintentionally erases another, e.g., deleting the last student in a course erases the course's information too), and update anomalies (a fact duplicated across many rows must be updated everywhere consistently, e.g., changing an instructor's office number in every row that names that instructor). Normalization is driven by functional dependencies (FDs): an FD X → Y means that the value of attribute(s) X uniquely determines the value of attribute(s) Y (X is called a determinant).

First Normal Form (1NF) requires that every attribute hold only a single, atomic value (no repeating groups or multi-valued/composite attributes within a single cell) — a table with a "Phone Numbers" column holding a comma-separated list of numbers violates 1NF and must be split into a separate row per number (or a separate related table). Second Normal Form (2NF) requires the table to already be in 1NF and additionally have no partial dependency — every non-key attribute must depend on the whole of a composite primary key, not just part of it (this rule is vacuous, i.e., automatically satisfied, for tables with a single-attribute primary key). Third Normal Form (3NF) requires 2NF and additionally no transitive dependency — no non-key attribute may depend on another non-key attribute rather than directly on the primary key (i.e., if Key → A and A → B, then Key → B is only a transitive, indirect dependency, and B should be moved to a separate table keyed on A). Boyce-Codd Normal Form (BCNF) is a stricter version of 3NF: for every non-trivial functional dependency X → Y in the relation, X must be a superkey (every determinant must be a candidate key) — this closes a subtle loophole where a table can satisfy 3NF yet still contain redundancy caused by an overlapping composite candidate key.

1NFAll attribute values atomic; no repeating groupsMulti-valued attribute redundancy
2NF1NF + no partial dependency on part of a composite keyRedundancy from partial-key dependency
3NF2NF + no transitive dependency (non-key → non-key)Redundancy from transitive dependency

A worked illustration: consider an unnormalized table Enrollment(StudentID, StudentName, CourseID, CourseName, InstructorName, InstructorOffice). Here CourseID → CourseName, InstructorName, InstructorOffice (a transitive dependency, since Enrollment's key is really StudentID+CourseID, and InstructorOffice depends on InstructorName which is itself only dependent on CourseID, not directly on the key), so 3NF decomposition splits this into Enrollment(StudentID, CourseID), Student(StudentID, StudentName), and Course(CourseID, CourseName, InstructorName, InstructorOffice) — and if InstructorOffice depends only on InstructorName (not on CourseID) that is itself a further transitive dependency requiring one more split into a separate Instructor table, illustrating that normalization is typically applied repeatedly until no dependency violations of the target normal form remain.

  • Exam pointers
  • Three anomalies normalization prevents: insertion, deletion, update anomalies — always name and illustrate all three.
  • 1NF: atomic values only. 2NF: no partial dependency (relevant only for composite keys). 3NF: no transitive dependency. BCNF: every determinant is a candidate key (stricter than 3NF).
  • Be able to take a given unnormalized/1NF table and decompose it step by step to 3NF/BCNF, naming the FD violated at each step — this is the standard 20-mark format for this topic.
  • Trade-off: normalization reduces redundancy/anomalies but increases the number of tables and the joins needed to reconstruct data (sometimes deliberately reversed via denormalization for read-heavy performance).

Structured Query Language (SQL)

SQL is the standard declarative language for defining, manipulating, and controlling data in a relational database. It is organized into sub-languages: the Data Definition Language (DDL) — CREATE TABLE, ALTER TABLE, DROP TABLE — defines and modifies schema objects; the Data Manipulation Language (DML) — SELECT, INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE — reads and modifies the data itself; the Data Control Language (DCL) — GRANT, REVOKE — manages user permissions; and the Transaction Control Language (TCL) — COMMIT, ROLLBACK, SAVEPOINT — manages the boundaries and durability of transactions.

The SELECT statement is SQL's core query tool: SELECT columns FROM table WHERE row_condition GROUP BY grouping_columns HAVING group_condition ORDER BY sort_columns, where WHERE filters individual rows before grouping, GROUP BY collapses rows sharing common values (typically paired with aggregate functions COUNT, SUM, AVG, MIN, MAX), and HAVING filters entire groups after aggregation (the key distinction from WHERE, which cannot reference aggregate results). Joins combine rows from two or more tables based on a related column: an inner join returns only rows with matches in both tables; a left (outer) join returns all rows from the left table plus matched rows from the right (unmatched right-side columns are NULL); a right (outer) join is the mirror image; and a full (outer) join returns all rows from both tables, matched where possible.

Example combining several of these ideas — total loan amount disbursed per branch, only for branches whose total exceeds one million, using an explicit inner join and grouping:

SELECT b.BranchName, SUM(l.Amount) AS TotalLoans
FROM Branch b
JOIN Loan l ON b.BranchID = l.BranchID
GROUP BY b.BranchName
HAVING SUM(l.Amount) > 1000000
ORDER BY TotalLoans DESC;

A subquery (nested query) is a query embedded inside another query's WHERE, FROM, or SELECT clause, useful for conditions that themselves require a query to evaluate, e.g., SELECT Name FROM Customer WHERE CustomerID IN (SELECT CustomerID FROM Loan WHERE Amount > 500000) finds customers who have at least one large loan. SQL statement effects can be scoped into an atomic unit with COMMIT (make changes permanent) and ROLLBACK (undo all changes since the last commit/savepoint), which is how SQL ties directly into the transaction and recovery mechanisms discussed in database security and disaster recovery below.

  • Exam pointers
  • Four SQL sub-languages: DDL (CREATE/ALTER/DROP), DML (SELECT/INSERT/UPDATE/DELETE), DCL (GRANT/REVOKE), TCL (COMMIT/ROLLBACK/SAVEPOINT).
  • WHERE filters rows before grouping; HAVING filters groups after aggregation — a frequently tested distinction.
  • Join types: inner (matches only), left/right outer (all of one side + matches), full outer (all of both sides) — know what appears as NULL in each case.

Database Security and Protection

Database security addresses controlling who may access what data and in what way, while database protection more broadly covers safeguarding data integrity and availability against both malicious action and accidental failure. Authentication verifies a user's identity (username/password, or stronger multi-factor methods) before granting any database connection at all. Authorization then determines what an authenticated user may do, implemented in SQL through GRANT (e.g., GRANT SELECT, INSERT ON Accounts TO teller_role;) and REVOKE, often organized around roles (named bundles of privileges assigned to users, simplifying administration compared to granting privileges to each user individually). A view (a virtual table defined by a stored query) is a key security tool: it can expose only specific columns/rows of an underlying table to a class of users (e.g., a teller sees customer names and balances but not social security numbers), enforcing fine-grained access control without duplicating data.

Data at rest and in transit can additionally be protected with encryption, so that even someone who bypasses access controls (or intercepts network traffic) cannot read the underlying values without the decryption key. Integrity constraints — domain constraints (a column's value must be of the correct type/range), key constraints (primary keys must be unique and non-null), and referential integrity constraints (a foreign key value must either be null or match an existing primary key value in the referenced table) — protect the database from becoming logically inconsistent, independent of any access-control concern.

Protection against concurrent-access problems and failures is provided by the transaction manager, which guarantees the ACID properties for every transaction: Atomicity (a transaction's operations either all take effect or none do — enforced via logging and rollback), Consistency (a transaction moves the database from one valid state to another, respecting all integrity constraints), Isolation (concurrently executing transactions do not see each other's uncommitted intermediate states, enforced via concurrency-control protocols such as two-phase locking or timestamp ordering), and Durability (once a transaction commits, its effects survive any subsequent system failure, enforced by writing changes — or at least a log of them — to non-volatile storage before acknowledging commit). Concurrency control specifically prevents interleaved transactions from producing lost updates, dirty reads, or inconsistent results, typically via locking protocols (shared locks for reads, exclusive locks for writes, with two-phase locking guaranteeing serializable schedules) or optimistic/timestamp-based methods that validate at commit time instead of locking throughout.

  • Exam pointers
  • Authentication (who you are) vs. authorization (what you may do) — GRANT/REVOKE, roles.
  • Views as an access-control mechanism: restrict visible rows/columns without physically separate copies of data.
  • Integrity constraints: domain, key (primary key uniqueness/non-null), referential (foreign key must match or be null).
  • ACID: Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability — define each and name the mechanism enforcing it (logging/rollback, constraints, locking/2PL, write-ahead logging).
  • Concurrency-control purpose: prevent lost updates, dirty reads, inconsistent retrieval among simultaneous transactions.

RDBMS

A Relational Database Management System implements the relational data model, proposed by E. F. Codd (1970), in which data is organized purely as relations — two-dimensional tables consisting of tuples (rows, each representing one record/instance) and attributes (columns, each holding one property, drawn from a defined domain of legal values). Unlike earlier hierarchical or network database models, the relational model requires no predefined access paths or pointers between records — relationships between tables are represented purely by matching data values (keys), which gives it its declarative query power and physical data independence (the physical storage can change without altering how queries are written).

Several kinds of keys structure a relation: a candidate key is any minimal set of attributes that uniquely identifies a tuple; a super key is any set of attributes containing a candidate key (a superset, possibly with extra unnecessary attributes); a primary key is the candidate key chosen by the designer as the table's main identifier (must be unique and non-null); a foreign key is an attribute (or set) in one table that references the primary key of another table, implementing inter-table relationships and referential integrity; and a composite key is any key made up of more than one attribute.

The theoretical foundation of RDBMS query processing is relational algebra, a set of operations that take one or more relations as input and produce a new relation as output: Select (σ, filters rows matching a condition), Project (π, chooses a subset of columns, removing duplicates), Union, Intersection, and Set Difference (combine two union-compatible relations), Cartesian Product (×, pairs every tuple of one relation with every tuple of another), and Join (⋈, effectively a filtered Cartesian product matching related tuples, the algebraic basis of SQL's JOIN clause). Codd additionally formulated twelve (numbered 0–12) rules defining what a system must satisfy to be considered "fully relational," covering aspects such as information being represented only as values in tables, guaranteed logical access to every data item via table name + key + column, systematic treatment of null values, and physical/logical data independence — used more as a conceptual benchmark than a strict certification test in modern practice.

  • Exam pointers
  • Relational model = tables (relations) of tuples (rows) and attributes (columns); no predefined pointers between records, unlike hierarchical/network models.
  • Key hierarchy: super key ⊇ candidate key → (designer chooses) primary key; foreign key implements inter-table references; composite key spans multiple attributes.
  • Relational algebra operations: Select (σ), Project (π), Union/Intersect/Difference, Cartesian Product (×), Join (⋈) — SQL's SELECT/JOIN are the practical realization of these.
  • Codd's 12 rules define the "fully relational" ideal — mention the concept even if not every rule is memorized verbatim.

Disaster Recovery Strategies and Mitigation

Disaster recovery (DR) planning ensures a database (and the business functions depending on it) can be restored to a correct, usable state after hardware failure, software failure, human error, or catastrophic events such as fire, flood, or cyberattack. Two governing metrics frame every DR strategy: the Recovery Point Objective (RPO), the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time (e.g., an RPO of 15 minutes means at most 15 minutes of transactions can be lost), and the Recovery Time Objective (RTO), the maximum acceptable time to restore service after an incident. Tighter RPO/RTO targets require more investment in redundancy and automation.

Backup strategies form the baseline: a full backup copies the entire database (simple to restore from, but slow and storage-heavy to take repeatedly); an incremental backup copies only data changed since the last backup of any kind (fast to take, but restoration requires replaying the last full backup plus every incremental since, in order); and a differential backup copies all data changed since the last full backup (larger than incremental but faster to restore, since only the last full plus the single latest differential are needed). At the database-engine level, recovery from a crash relies on the transaction log (write-ahead log), which records every update before it is applied to the actual database, combined with periodic checkpoints (a marker guaranteeing all earlier changes are safely on disk, so recovery need only replay the log from the last checkpoint forward); recovery then applies redo (reapply committed transactions' changes not yet on disk) and undo (roll back changes from transactions that had not committed at the time of failure) as needed. Shadow paging is an alternative recovery technique that avoids most logging by maintaining two page tables (current and shadow) and atomically switching which one is "live" only once all of a transaction's writes (to new, unmodified pages) are safely committed, keeping the old shadow version intact until the switch.

Beyond backups, resilience is engineered through redundancy: RAID (Redundant Array of Independent Disks) protects against single-disk failure via mirroring (RAID 1) or parity striping (RAID 5), so the array survives one disk loss without data loss; database replication and clustering keep a live, continuously synchronized secondary copy of the database (on separate hardware, often at a geographically separate site) that can take over with minimal downtime if the primary fails; and organizations classify recovery sites by readiness — a hot site (a fully equipped, continuously synchronized duplicate facility that can take over almost immediately, minimizing RTO but most expensive), a warm site (has hardware and connectivity ready but needs recent backups restored and some setup before going live, a middle ground), and a cold site (has only basic infrastructure — space, power, connectivity — with equipment and data restoration to be performed from scratch after a disaster, cheapest but slowest, largest RTO). A comprehensive Business Continuity Plan (BCP) ties these technical measures together with documented procedures, responsibilities, communication plans, and regular testing/drills, since untested recovery plans routinely fail when actually needed.

  • Exam pointers
  • RPO (how much data can we afford to lose) vs. RTO (how long can we afford to be down) — define both and relate to backup frequency/investment.
  • Backup types: full (complete, slow), incremental (changes since last backup of any type, fast to take/slow-ish to restore in sequence), differential (changes since last full, middle ground).
  • Log-based recovery: write-ahead logging, checkpoints, redo (committed but not yet on disk) and undo (uncommitted at crash time); shadow paging as a logging-free alternative.
  • Redundancy layers: RAID (disk-level), replication/clustering (database/site-level), and hot/warm/cold recovery sites — know the RTO/cost trade-off ordering (hot fastest/costliest, cold slowest/cheapest).
  • A Business Continuity Plan must be tested/rehearsed regularly — untested plans are a common exam-answer point often forgotten.

Legal and Ethical Issues in Handling Disaster

Beyond the technical mechanics of backup and recovery, an organization handling a data-loss or security-breach disaster faces legal obligations and ethical responsibilities that shape how the incident must be managed. Legally, most jurisdictions (and Nepal's own Electronic Transaction Act and related data-protection/privacy regulations, alongside sector-specific directives from the central bank/regulator for financial institutions) impose duties such as timely breach notification to affected customers and regulators, preservation of audit trails and transaction records for a mandated retention period, and compliance with data-localization or cross-border data-transfer restrictions where they apply. For a banking institution specifically, additional compliance obligations typically include central-bank/regulatory reporting of any incident affecting customer financial data, and adherence to any statutory minimum standards for record retention and system audit trails so that regulators and auditors can reconstruct events after the fact.

Ethically, an institution's responsibility extends beyond the bare legal minimum: maintaining confidentiality of customer data (not exposing more information than necessary even internally, following the principle of least privilege), preserving integrity (not silently altering or concealing the true extent of data loss or corruption), and ensuring availability is restored fairly and transparently for all affected customers rather than selectively. Ethical incident handling also demands honest, timely disclosure to stakeholders (customers, employees, management, regulators) rather than concealment or minimization of the disaster's scope, since delayed or misleading disclosure can cause greater harm (financial loss, fraud exposure) to customers who are not warned in time to protect themselves.

Good governance ties the legal and ethical dimensions together through accountability: clearly assigned responsibility (a named data-protection or incident-response officer), documented and auditable decision-making during the response, and post-incident review feeding back into policy so the same failure is less likely to recur. Liability considerations also matter — an organization that can demonstrate it followed a reasonable, documented, regularly tested disaster-recovery and security standard of care is generally in a far stronger legal and reputational position than one that cannot, which is why disaster recovery planning and its legal/ethical obligations are treated as inseparable in professional practice, not as an afterthought bolted onto a purely technical backup strategy.

  • Exam pointers
  • Legal duties after a data disaster/breach: timely notification to customers/regulators, statutory record-retention/audit-trail preservation, compliance with applicable data-protection law and, for banks, central-bank/regulatory reporting requirements.
  • Ethical duties: confidentiality (least privilege), integrity (no concealment of true impact), availability (fair, transparent restoration), and honest/timely disclosure to all stakeholders.
  • Governance: named accountability/incident-response responsibility, documented decisions, post-incident policy review.
  • Demonstrable, tested compliance with a reasonable DR/security standard of care reduces legal and reputational liability after an incident.

IT Development in Nepal: Issues and Challenges

Information Technology in Nepal has grown quickly since the early 2000s, but its development is uneven and constrained by structural problems. Internet penetration and mobile subscriptions have risen sharply, government services are moving online, and the banking sector has become one of the most digitised parts of the economy. The core issues can be grouped into a few themes. Infrastructure remains weak outside cities: unreliable electricity, limited last-mile connectivity, and dependence on international bandwidth routed through India (with limited routes via China) create single points of failure. Human resources and skills are a persistent problem — there is a shortage of specialised professionals in cybersecurity, cloud, data engineering and enterprise architecture, worsened by heavy brain drain as skilled graduates emigrate. The digital divide is geographic (urban vs. rural, hill vs. Terai), economic, gender-based and linguistic, so a large share of the population cannot meaningfully use digital financial services even when they are available.

Other challenges include cybersecurity maturity (rising fraud, phishing, ATM/card skimming, and attacks on payment systems, against a background of thin incident-response capacity and a young legal framework), low digital and financial literacy, a weak start-up and R&D funding environment, fragmented and slowly-enforced policy and regulation, and data localisation and governance gaps (no comprehensive data-protection regime until recently, so privacy rules are scattered across sector laws). Electronic Transaction Act, 2063 (2008)

The Electronic Transaction Act, 2063 (ETA) is Nepal's principal cyber law. It gives legal recognition to electronic records, electronic documents and digital (electronic) signatures, and provides for the security and regulation of electronic transactions. Before the ETA, a contract or document in electronic form had no clear legal standing; the Act establishes that information shall not be denied legal effect merely because it is in electronic form. It also defines cyber offences and prescribes penalties, and it created the institutions needed to run a public-key trust system in Nepal.

On the digital signature side, the ETA is built on Public Key Infrastructure (PKI). It provides for a Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA) as the apex regulator, and licensed Certifying Authorities (CAs) that issue digital certificates binding a person's identity to a key pair. A digitally signed electronic record is treated as authentic and attributable to the signer, and such a signature carries evidentiary weight. This is the legal backbone that lets banks accept electronically signed instructions, use certificates for secure systems, and rely on the digital signatures embedded in payment and messaging systems.

On the offences side, the ETA criminalises acts such as unauthorised access to a computer/network (hacking), damaging or altering computer source code, unauthorised copying/piracy, publishing illegal or offensive material in electronic form, breach of confidentiality, and computer-related fraud, with fines and/or imprisonment. It also provides for adjudication and a tribunal mechanism for disputes. Because the Act dates from 2063 and technology has moved on, it is widely regarded as needing modernisation (for cloud, data protection, and new fraud types), so answers should acknowledge its limitations as well as its role.

  • Exam pointers
  • Year: 2063 (2008). Popularly called Nepal's "Cyber Law".
  • Two halves to cover: (a) legal recognition of e-records and digital signatures via PKI; (b) cyber offences and penalties.
  • Key institutions: Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA) and licensed Certifying Authorities.
  • Typical offences to list: unauthorised access/hacking, damage to source code, piracy, publishing illegal content, breach of privacy/confidentiality, e-fraud.
  • Mention it is due for amendment/modernisation (no full data-protection coverage; predates cloud and modern threats) (confirm exact section numbers and penalty amounts from the official document).

Secured Transactions Act, 2063 (2006)

The Secured Transactions Act, 2063 addresses a different but banking-critical problem: the use of movable and intangible property as collateral. Traditionally Nepali lenders accepted mainly land and buildings (immovable property) as security, which excluded businesses whose main assets are movable — inventory, machinery, vehicles, receivables, crops, intellectual property. The Act creates a legal framework for creating, perfecting, prioritising and enforcing a security interest over such movable property, thereby widening the base of acceptable collateral and supporting access to credit for SMEs.

The centrepiece is a centralised, electronic Secured Transactions (Collateral) Registry. Lenders register their security interest in the registry so that the interest becomes public and enforceable against third parties. Registration establishes priority — generally the first to register has the superior claim — which reduces the risk of the same movable asset being pledged to several lenders. If the borrower defaults, the Act provides a mechanism for the secured creditor to enforce and realise the collateral. This is why the registry is sometimes described as making "dead" movable assets usable as "live" collateral.

For RBB and other banks/FIs, the Act and its registry matter operationally: credit officers search the registry before lending against movable assets, file their own interests, and rely on registered priority to manage recovery. It complements the Credit Information Bureau (credit history) by covering collateral status, together improving lending decisions and reducing over-pledging and fraud.

  • Exam pointers
  • Year: 2063 (2006). Purpose: allow movable/intangible property as collateral.
  • Central idea: a Secured Transactions / Collateral Registry to register security interests, establish priority, and enable enforcement.
  • "First to register, first in priority" is the key rule to state.
  • Benefit: expands credit access for SMEs and businesses whose assets are non-land (inventory, receivables, machinery).
  • Distinguish from the Credit Information Bureau (CIB): registry = collateral/security interests; CIB = borrower credit history (confirm the exact registry office name and its parent body from the official document).

NRB IT Policy and IT Guidelines for Banks/FIs

Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB), as the central bank and regulator, issues an Information Technology Guideline (and related unified/prudential directives) that all licensed banks and financial institutions (BFIs) must follow. The purpose is to ensure that as banking becomes technology-dependent, institutions manage IT and cyber risk to a common minimum standard, protect customer data and funds, and maintain the stability and integrity of the payment and banking system. For RBB the guideline is effectively the compliance baseline for its core banking system, digital channels and data centre operations.

The main themes typically covered are: IT governance (board and senior management responsibility, an IT steering committee, IT strategy and budget); information security (security policy, access control, network and endpoint security, encryption, patch and vulnerability management); risk management and periodic IS audit; business continuity and disaster recovery (BCP/DRP, a geographically separate DR site, defined RTO/RPO, and regular DR drills); data centre standards and change management; outsourcing and vendor management (since many BFIs rely on shared core-banking and payment vendors); digital/electronic banking controls (card, mobile, internet banking, ATM security); incident management and reporting to NRB; and increasingly data protection and fraud monitoring.

NRB reinforces the guideline through supervision, on-site and off-site inspection, and by requiring institutions to appoint responsible officers (for example an information security function) and to report material IT incidents. Because technology and threats evolve, NRB periodically revises the guideline and issues circulars, so answers should present it as a living, regularly updated framework rather than a single fixed document.

  • Exam pointers
  • Issuer and audience: NRB issues it; it binds all BFIs (banks/financial institutions).
  • Memorise the theme list: IT governance, information security, risk management, IS audit, BCP/DRP, data centre, outsourcing/vendor, digital-banking controls, incident reporting.
  • Key operational terms: DR site, RTO/RPO, DR drills, IT steering committee, IS audit.
  • Purpose in one line: standardise and supervise IT/cyber risk across the banking system to protect customers and financial stability.

ICT Policy of Nepal

Nepal's national direction for the sector is set by the Information and Communication Technology (ICT) Policy, 2015 (2072 BS), which superseded earlier IT policies (notably the IT Policy 2000 and 2010). Its vision is to transform Nepal into an information- and knowledge-based society and economy, using ICT to accelerate socio-economic development, improve service delivery, and integrate the country into the global digital economy. It is the umbrella policy under which sectoral e-services — including digital finance — are meant to develop.

The policy's main themes include expanding ICT infrastructure and broadband access (including rural connectivity, often financed through the Rural Telecommunication Development Fund), promoting e-government and online public services, developing human capital and ICT skills, encouraging the ICT industry, innovation and entrepreneurship (IT parks, outsourcing, exports), ensuring cybersecurity and legal frameworks, and bridging the digital divide so access is inclusive. It set targets for broadband penetration and universal access, reflecting the ambition to make internet a basic utility.

The policy is closely associated with the later Digital Nepal Framework (2019), which operationalises the vision into sectors (digital foundation plus verticals such as agriculture, health, education, energy, tourism, finance, and urban infrastructure) and specific initiatives. For a banking exam, the important link is that the ICT Policy and Digital Nepal Framework provide the national mandate for digital payments, financial inclusion, and cashless-economy goals that NRB and BFIs implement.

Electronic Cheque Clearing (ECC), IPS, RTGS and connectIPS

Nepal's national payment plumbing is run largely by Nepal Clearing House Limited (NCHL) — a company promoted by NRB together with banks — for retail and cheque payments, while NRB itself operates the RTGS for large-value settlement. Understanding how the four systems differ (real-time vs. batch, large-value vs. retail, cheque vs. electronic transfer) is the heart of this topic.

Electronic Cheque Clearing (NCHL-ECC) replaced the old manual, physical cheque exchange with image-based cheque truncation. Instead of physically transporting paper cheques between banks, the presenting bank scans the cheque and transmits the image plus MICR data electronically to the clearing house; clearing is done in sessions and settled on a deferred net basis across banks' accounts at NRB. This dramatically cut clearing time (from days to same/next day) and reduced handling risk.

Interbank Payment System (NCHL-IPS) is an electronic funds-transfer system for retail and bulk payments — direct credits and direct debits — such as salaries, dividends, vendor payments, and government disbursements. It is batch/session based with deferred net settlement, so it is efficient for high volumes of relatively low-value transactions but not instantaneous.

Real Time Gross Settlement (RTGS), operated by NRB, settles large-value and time-critical transactions individually and in real time, gross (one-by-one, not netted), with immediate finality. It is the backbone for interbank settlement and high-value corporate/government payments and typically applies above a value threshold. Because settlement is final and immediate, it removes settlement risk on big transfers.

connectIPS is NCHL's consumer-facing e-payment portal/app. A user links one or more bank accounts to a single connectIPS profile and can then make fund transfers, pay bills, taxes, fees, EMI, and merchant payments directly from the bank account (account-to-account), rather than through a wallet balance. It rides on the IPS/RTGS rails and is part of NCHL's broader NEPALPAY brand family (which also includes NEPALPAY QR).

SWIFT and Online Banking Security

SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) is the global secure messaging network that banks use to exchange standardised financial messages — most importantly for cross-border payments, remittances, trade finance (LCs) and treasury. SWIFT does not move money itself; it moves the instructions to move money, using structured message types (traditional MT messages, and the newer ISO 20022 MX messages). Each institution has a unique BIC/SWIFT code. For RBB, SWIFT is essential for foreign-currency transfers, correspondent banking and inward remittances, which are a major part of Nepal's economy.

Because SWIFT is a high-value target, SWIFT runs the Customer Security Programme (CSP) with a mandatory Customer Security Controls Framework (CSCF) that members must self-attest against annually. Typical controls include segregating and hardening the SWIFT environment, strict access control and multi-factor authentication for operators, the four-eyes/maker-checker principle on message release, monitoring and logging, and secure connectivity. These controls arose partly in response to real-world attacks where fraudsters sent fraudulent payment instructions after compromising a bank's local environment.

Online banking security more broadly rests on layered controls: encryption in transit (TLS/HTTPS), strong authentication (passwords plus OTP/2FA/MFA, and device binding), transaction authorisation controls (limits, whitelisting payees, maker-checker for corporate banking), fraud monitoring and anomaly detection, secure application development, and customer-side controls against phishing, vishing, SIM-swap and malware. NRB's IT guideline and card-scheme rules (e.g. EMV, PCI-DSS for card data) reinforce these. Customer awareness is treated as a control in its own right, since social engineering is the dominant attack path in Nepal.

  • Exam pointers
  • SWIFT = secure messaging, not settlement; it carries instructions. Identifier = BIC/SWIFT code.
  • Message formats: legacy MT moving to ISO 20022 (MX).
  • Security programme: SWIFT CSP / CSCF, annual self-attestation, MFA, maker-checker, environment segregation.
  • Online banking layers: TLS, MFA/OTP, transaction limits, maker-checker, fraud monitoring, PCI-DSS/EMV, awareness.
  • Common Nepal threats to name: phishing/vishing, SIM-swap, OTP-sharing scams, card skimming.

SIEM, VPN, Digital Certificate, Digital Signature and PKI

SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) is a system that collects, correlates and analyses logs and security events from across the bank's infrastructure — servers, firewalls, core banking, endpoints, network devices — to detect threats, raise alerts, and support incident response and compliance. It combines log management with real-time correlation rules and, increasingly, behavioural analytics. In a bank, the SIEM typically feeds a Security Operations Centre (SOC) and is central to meeting NRB's monitoring and incident-reporting expectations.

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) creates an encrypted tunnel over an untrusted network (usually the internet), so that remote branches, ATMs, staff or vendors can connect to bank systems confidentially and with integrity. Site-to-site VPNs securely link branch networks to the data centre, while remote-access VPNs let authorised users connect from outside; both rely on strong cryptography (e.g. IPsec/TLS) and should be combined with MFA and least-privilege access.

The remaining three concepts are tied together by Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) — the ecosystem of keys, certificates and authorities that makes asymmetric cryptography usable at scale. A digital certificate (typically X.509) is an electronic document that binds a public key to an identity and is issued and signed by a trusted Certifying Authority (CA); it lets a relying party trust that a public key really belongs to the named entity. A digital signature is created by hashing a message and encrypting the hash with the signer's private key; anyone can verify it using the signer's public key (from the certificate), which provides authentication, integrity and non-repudiation. PKI supplies the CAs, registration authorities, certificate distribution and revocation (CRL/OCSP) that make this trust chain work — and in Nepal it is legally anchored by the Electronic Transaction Act 2063 and the office of the Controller of Certifying Authorities.

Use of Fintech in the Nepalese Banking Sector

Fintech has spread rapidly in Nepal over the last decade, moving the country meaningfully toward a less-cash economy and expanding financial inclusion. The regulatory basis is NRB's Payment and Settlement Act, 2019 and related bylaws/directives, under which NRB licenses Payment Service Providers (PSPs) — such as digital wallets — and Payment System Operators (PSOs) — such as NCHL and card networks. This licensing regime is what distinguishes regulated fintech from informal money transfer, and it is administered through NRB's Payment Systems Department.

The most visible fintech channels are: digital wallets (for example eSewa, Khalti, IME Pay) that hold a prepaid balance for bill payment, top-ups, remittance and merchant payment; mobile and internet banking offered directly by banks including RBB; QR-code payments, which have become the dominant merchant-acceptance method; and card and ATM networks. On QR, two developments matter: Fonepay (an F1Soft network) which popularised interoperable bank/wallet QR, and NEPALPAY QR, the national interoperable QR standard promoted through NCHL — the goal being a single QR that any bank or wallet app can scan, so merchants need only one code. connectIPS (covered above) is the account-based counterpart of the wallet model.

For banks, fintech is both an opportunity and a challenge. It lowers the cost of serving customers, supports agent/branchless banking and government-to-person payments, and drives fee/float income and deposit stickiness. At the same time it raises cybersecurity, fraud, AML/CFT and consumer-protection obligations, and pressures banks to partner with or compete against nimble fintech firms. The emerging global trend of open banking / open APIs — where customers can authorise third parties to access their bank data or initiate payments — is beginning to be discussed in Nepal; interoperable rails like NEPALPAY and connectIPS are steps in that direction, though a full open-banking framework is still nascent. Answers should present Nepal as having strong retail-payment adoption but an evolving regulatory and open-banking layer.

  • Exam pointers
  • Regulatory anchor: Payment and Settlement Act 2019 + NRB licensing of PSPs and PSOs.
  • Channels to list: wallets (eSewa, Khalti, IME Pay), mobile/internet banking, QR, cards/ATM, connectIPS.
  • QR interoperability: Fonepay network and the national NEPALPAY QR standard — "one QR for all".
  • Benefits: financial inclusion, less-cash economy, agent/branchless banking, G2P payments, lower cost.
  • Risks/duties: cybersecurity, fraud, AML/CFT, consumer protection, data privacy.
